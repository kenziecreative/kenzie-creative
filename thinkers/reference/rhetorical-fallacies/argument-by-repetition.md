# Argument by repetition

> Repeating a claim persistently to make it seem true despite lack of evidence or logic.

## Description

Argument by repetition uses repeated exposure to make a claim feel familiar, accepted, or inevitable without adding evidence or reasoning.

Repetition can be useful for memory, coordination, and emphasis. It becomes fallacious when frequency is doing the work that evidence should be doing.

## How It Works

Repeated statements become easier to process. That ease can be mistaken for truth, consensus, or importance.

Repetition can also wear down resistance. A person or group may stop challenging the claim because every objection is met with the same line again.

## Signature Phrasing

- "As I've said before..."
- "Everyone knows this."
- "We keep coming back to the same answer."
- "I have already told you."
- Repeating the same slogan, claim, or framing after objections.

## Ask Yourself

> What evidence, mechanism, or reasoning supports this claim besides the fact that I keep hearing it?

## What It Is Not

Repeating a point is not automatically fallacious. People may repeat important information because it was missed, misunderstood, or operationally necessary.

The issue is whether repetition clarifies an already supported point or replaces support entirely.

## How to Respond

Ask for new support, not another pass through the same sentence.

- "I hear the claim. What evidence supports it?"
- "What would change your mind?"
- "Can we separate repetition from proof?"
- "Has this point answered the objection, or only restated the position?"

If a repeated phrase is dominating a group, write the claim down once, then require the conversation to add reasons, examples, or tests.
