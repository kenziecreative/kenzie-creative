# Proof by assertion

> Repeating a statement as true without evidence to force acceptance despite lack of proof.

## Description

Proof by assertion treats confident assertion as if it were proof. A claim is stated as settled without evidence, and objections are met by restating the claim rather than supporting it.

It overlaps with argument by repetition, but the center of gravity is slightly different: proof by assertion is about refusing the burden of proof; argument by repetition is about using familiarity and frequency to create acceptance.

## How It Works

The speaker creates the feeling that the matter is already decided. Confidence, speed, status, and persistence can make others feel that asking for proof is unreasonable or behind the conversation.

When repeated, the assertion can also exhaust opponents. The discussion becomes a test of stamina rather than evidence.

## Signature Phrasing

- "It is just a fact."
- "That is how it is."
- "I do not need to prove the obvious."
- "I have said it before and I will say it again."
- Reasserting the claim after being asked for evidence.

## Ask Yourself

> Has the claim been supported, or only stated with confidence?

## What It Is Not

Not every direct statement needs a full proof. In normal conversation, people often use shorthand for things already established or easily checked.

The fallacy appears when the assertion is doing argumentative work and the speaker refuses to provide reasons, evidence, criteria, or a way to test the claim.

## How to Respond

Put the burden of proof back where it belongs.

- "What supports that claim?"
- "What would count as evidence against it?"
- "Can you walk me from the evidence to the conclusion?"
- "I understand that you believe it; I am asking why."

If the person keeps asserting, pause the loop: "We are repeating the conclusion now. I am looking for the reason."
