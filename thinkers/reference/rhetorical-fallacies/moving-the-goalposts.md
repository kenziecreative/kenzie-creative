# Moving the goalposts

> Changing criteria for success or agreement after they have been met to avoid conceding a point.

## Description

Moving the goalposts happens when the criteria for proof, success, agreement, or completion change after they have been met.

The result is a conversation or process where resolution keeps receding. Each time one standard is satisfied, a new standard appears.

## How It Works

The tactic controls the terms of victory. By keeping the criteria unstable, the person moving the goalposts avoids conceding, approving, deciding, or being accountable.

It can also happen unintentionally when standards were never clearly defined at the start. Ambiguity gives disappointment a place to hide.

## Signature Phrasing

- "That's not good enough"
- "You haven't really proved that"
- "Now show me..."
- "We need more than that"
- "That was not what I meant by success."

## Ask Yourself

> Were the criteria clear before the evidence or work was delivered, or are they changing after the fact?

## What It Is Not

Raising standards is not always unfair. New evidence, higher stakes, or better understanding may legitimately change what is required.

The difference is transparency. A legitimate standard change explains why the previous criteria were insufficient and whether the same rule will apply consistently going forward.

## How to Respond

Make the criteria explicit and time-bound.

- "What would count as sufficient?"
- "Are these the criteria we are agreeing to now?"
- "Did the evidence meet the standard we set earlier?"
- "If the standard is changing, what changed and why?"
- "Will this new standard apply consistently?"

In work settings, document acceptance criteria before the work begins and distinguish new requirements from missed requirements.
