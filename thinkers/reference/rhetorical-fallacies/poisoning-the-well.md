# Poisoning the well

> Discrediting an opponent before they speak to bias the audience against them.

## Description

Poisoning the well discredits a person, source, or position before it is heard, so the audience receives the argument through a prejudiced frame.

It is a preemptive form of ad hominem. Instead of answering the argument, the speaker prepares the audience to distrust whatever the target says.

## How It Works

Poisoning the well uses priming. Once the audience is told that a person is dishonest, biased, foolish, dangerous, disloyal, or compromised, later claims are filtered through that suspicion.

It also creates a reputational trap. If the target responds defensively, the response can be framed as confirmation of the original warning.

## Signature Phrasing

- "Before you listen to them, remember..."
- "You cannot trust anything they say."
- "Of course they will deny it."
- "Only someone like that would argue this."
- Introducing unrelated negative information before the person speaks.

## Ask Yourself

> Is this background information relevant to evaluating the claim, or is it being used to make fair evaluation impossible?

## What It Is Not

It is not poisoning the well to disclose relevant conflicts of interest, expertise limits, deception history, or context that affects credibility.

The distinction is whether the information helps calibrate trust or blocks evaluation altogether. "This expert is funded by the company, so inspect the evidence carefully" is different from "They are funded, so nothing they say matters."

## How to Respond

Separate source evaluation from argument evaluation.

- "How is that information relevant to this claim?"
- "Let's hear the argument before judging it."
- "What evidence would we accept regardless of who presented it?"
- "If credibility is a concern, what verification would address it?"

If the reputational information is relevant, keep it proportional: use it to decide how carefully to verify, not as an automatic substitute for verification.
