# Motte-and-bailey

> A rhetorical tactic where a controversial claim is defended by retreating to a much easier, uncontroversial one when challenged.

## Description

Motte-and-bailey is a rhetorical tactic where someone advances a bold, controversial claim, then retreats to a modest, easily defensible claim when challenged.

The "bailey" is the attractive but harder-to-defend position. The "motte" is the safer position. The problem is not changing one's mind. The problem is moving between two claims while pretending they are the same claim.

## How It Works

The tactic exploits ambiguity between a strong version and a weak version of a claim.

When the bold version is useful, the speaker benefits from its force. When the bold version is challenged, the speaker defends only the modest version and may accuse critics of misrepresenting them.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I only meant..."
- "You are attacking a straw man."
- "Obviously I was just saying..."
- "No reasonable person would disagree with the basic point."
- A sudden retreat from a sweeping claim to a harmless one.

## Ask Yourself

> Which exact version of the claim is being defended right now, and is it the same version that created the controversy?

## What It Is Not

Clarifying a claim is not automatically motte-and-bailey. People often speak imprecisely and then refine their meaning in good faith.

The tactic appears when the speaker continues to benefit from the stronger claim while only defending the weaker one.

## How to Respond

Pin down the claim before continuing.

- "Are you defending the stronger claim or the narrower one?"
- "I agree with the modest version. Do you still stand by the stronger version?"
- "Let's write the claim in one sentence before we evaluate it."
- "If the original claim is being revised, what exactly is changing?"

If the narrower claim is acceptable, say so without conceding the broader one.
