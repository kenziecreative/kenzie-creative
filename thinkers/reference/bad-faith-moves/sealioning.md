# Sealioning

> Repeatedly demanding evidence or explanation in bad faith while performing politeness.

## Description

Sealioning mimics sincere inquiry. The person asks for clarification, sources, definitions, or proof, but ignores answers, shifts demands, or repeats questions until the target is exhausted.

The performance of civility is part of the tactic. If the target refuses, they can be framed as unreasonable.

## Mechanism

Sealioning exploits norms of openness and good-faith explanation. Most people want to be fair, so they keep answering longer than the exchange deserves.

The bad-faith questioner controls the pace and burden: the target must keep proving, while the questioner risks little.

## What It Avoids

- Stating the questioner's own position.
- Accepting answers as answers.
- Naming a standard of satisfaction.
- Advancing the conversation.
- Respecting the target's time and boundaries.

## Recognition Cues

- Questions repeat after being answered.
- The standard for a satisfactory answer keeps moving.
- The questioner remains polite but unresponsive.
- Ending the exchange is framed as proof that you cannot defend your view.

## Common False Positives

Persistent questioning can be sincere, especially when a topic is complex or trust is low.

The key difference is responsiveness. Good-faith questioners update, narrow, or acknowledge answers.

## How to Respond

Ask what would count as a satisfactory answer. Summarize what has already been answered. Set a boundary on time, scope, or number of follow-ups.

If the pattern continues, stop explaining and name the process problem.

## Decision Prompt

Is this person using questions to understand, or using questions to keep me performing explanation indefinitely?
