# Gish gallop

> Overwhelming an opponent with many weak claims so careful rebuttal becomes impossible.

## Description

A Gish gallop is a bad-faith debate move where someone rapidly presents many weak, misleading, or under-supported claims. The target cannot answer them all in the available time, so unanswered claims appear to stand.

The point is not to clarify truth. The point is to create the feeling of overwhelming evidence while preventing careful examination of any single piece.

## Mechanism

The move exploits asymmetry. Making a claim is fast; checking and refuting it takes time. A speaker can produce ten claims in a minute, each of which may take several minutes to examine.

It also exploits audience optics. The person producing more claims may look prepared, confident, or dominant.

## What It Avoids

- Depth.
- Source quality.
- Causal precision.
- Definitions.
- Accountability for any one claim.
- Prioritizing the strongest evidence.

## Recognition Cues

- Rapid-fire claims without time for response.
- Refusal to pick the strongest claim.
- Moving to a new point as soon as one is challenged.
- Treating unanswered claims as conceded.
- Many points, little depth.

## Common False Positives

A long list is not automatically a Gish gallop. Sometimes a situation genuinely has many relevant facts, or a person is anxious and disorganized.

The pattern becomes bad faith when quantity is used to prevent evaluation.

## How to Respond

Do not try to answer everything. Ask them to choose the strongest claim. Set the rule: one claim, one source, one response at a time.

If there is an audience, name the asymmetry: "It takes seconds to make a claim and minutes to check it." Track parked claims as parked, not conceded.

## Decision Prompt

Which one claim is strongest, and can it survive careful examination on its own?
