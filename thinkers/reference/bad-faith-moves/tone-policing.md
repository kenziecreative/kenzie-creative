# Tone policing

> Deflecting from the substance of a message by focusing on the speaker's emotional delivery.

## Description

Tone policing shifts attention from what was said to how it was said. Anger, frustration, urgency, or bluntness becomes the issue, while the underlying claim is avoided.

This does not mean tone never matters. The bad-faith version is selective: tone is invoked to avoid uncomfortable content, especially when the speaker has less power.

## Mechanism

The move exploits social norms of politeness and professionalism. If the target accepts the frame, they must defend their delivery instead of the substance.

It can also make justified emotion look like irrationality.

## What It Avoids

- The actual claim.
- The harm or concern being raised.
- Power differences in who is allowed to be emotional.
- Accountability for the listener's response.
- The possibility that emotion is evidence of stakes.

## Recognition Cues

- "Calm down" arrives before engagement with the issue.
- The same tone standard is not applied evenly.
- Politeness becomes more important than accuracy or harm.
- The speaker is asked to make the message comfortable before it will be heard.

## Common False Positives

Requests for clearer or safer communication can be legitimate. Abuse, threats, or contempt should not be excused as passion.

The distinction is whether tone feedback helps the conversation happen or prevents the substance from being addressed.

## How to Respond

Acknowledge the tone briefly if useful, then return to the claim. Ask whether the listener is willing to address the substance separately from delivery.

If tone standards are uneven, name the asymmetry.

## Decision Prompt

Is tone being discussed to make the conversation possible, or to avoid the issue that made the tone understandable?
