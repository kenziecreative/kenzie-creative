# Doublespeak

> Language designed to obscure, sanitize, or invert meaning so people cannot easily name what is happening.

## Description

Doublespeak uses language against clarity. It can make harmful actions sound neutral, coercive actions sound benevolent, restrictions sound like freedoms, or accurate descriptions sound extreme.

It is stronger than ordinary euphemism. Euphemism softens; doublespeak often reverses the moral meaning.

## Mechanism

Doublespeak changes the available frame. If the accepted term already carries the speaker's preferred judgment, listeners must do extra work to recover the underlying reality.

Over time, the manufactured term can capture the conversation. Even critics may feel forced to use the misleading language because it has become the default.

## What It Avoids

- Plain description.
- Moral weight.
- Accountability for the underlying action.
- Shared access to reality.
- Debate on neutral terms.

## Recognition Cues

- The term sounds calm while the underlying reality is severe.
- Replacing the phrase with plain language changes the emotional meaning.
- Accurate wording is treated as rude, partisan, or dramatic.
- The label makes the opposite position sound inherently immoral or absurd.

## Common False Positives

Not every technical or institutional phrase is doublespeak. Some specialized language is precise.

The diagnostic is whether the term replaces a clearer word in a way that protects power or reverses moral weight.

## How to Respond

Translate the phrase into the simplest accurate language and continue using that language calmly. Ask what the term means in concrete action.

Do not let the debate happen entirely inside the manufactured vocabulary.

## Decision Prompt

If this were described in the plainest accurate words, would the audience react differently?
