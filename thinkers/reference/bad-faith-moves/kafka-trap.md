# Kafka trap

> Treating a person's denial of an accusation as further evidence that the accusation is true.

## Description

A Kafka trap creates a no-win frame. The target is accused, and any attempt to deny, explain, or defend themselves is interpreted as confirmation of guilt.

The move is bad faith because it removes the possibility of disconfirmation. Evidence no longer matters; the target's response has been pre-classified.

## Mechanism

The trap works through circular reasoning and social pressure. The accused person is pushed into a defensive posture, and the defense is then treated as suspicious.

This can intimidate people into silence because every response seems to deepen the accusation.

## What It Avoids

- Evidence.
- A falsifiable claim.
- Fair standards of proof.
- The possibility that the accusation is mistaken.
- Mutual accountability for reasoning.

## Recognition Cues

- Denial is treated as guilt.
- Requests for evidence are treated as proof of bad character.
- The accusation cannot be disproven even in principle.
- The target is asked to prove an internal state or identity.

## Common False Positives

Some defensive reactions can be relevant evidence, especially when paired with facts. Denial alone is not always meaningless.

The Kafka trap appears when denial is automatically treated as proof without independent evidence.

## How to Respond

Refuse the circular frame. Ask for the specific claim, evidence, and standard that would change the accuser's mind.

If no standard exists, name that the conversation is not evidence-based and disengage or move to a fair process.

## Decision Prompt

What evidence would count against this accusation, and if nothing would, why am I treating this as a fair inquiry?
