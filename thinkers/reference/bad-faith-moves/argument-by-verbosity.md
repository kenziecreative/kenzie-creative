# Argument by verbosity

> Burying a weak or evasive point under excessive detail, jargon, tangents, or length.

## Description

Argument by verbosity uses volume to create the impression of substance. The speaker may offer long explanations, technical language, side issues, citations, caveats, and repeated qualifications without answering the central question.

The problem is not length itself. Complex topics sometimes need detail. The bad-faith version uses detail to prevent clarity.

## Mechanism

Verbosity exploits fatigue and authority cues. Listeners may assume that more words mean more expertise, or they may give up because untangling the answer costs too much effort.

It also changes the interaction: instead of evaluating the claim, people must first locate it.

## What It Avoids

- A direct answer.
- A clear claim that can be tested.
- Accountability for relevance.
- Prioritizing the strongest evidence.
- Letting the other person understand enough to respond.

## Recognition Cues

- The explanation is long but the answer is hard to summarize.
- Jargon appears where plain language would work.
- Side issues multiply when the central issue is pressed.
- Requests for a direct answer produce more context, not clarity.

## Common False Positives

Some people are anxious, technical, neurodivergent, or trying to be thorough. A long answer is not automatically bad faith.

The pattern becomes suspect when length repeatedly prevents the core question from being answered.

## How to Respond

Ask for the main claim in one or two sentences. Then ask which evidence directly supports that claim.

If the speaker keeps expanding, set a process boundary: "I can engage one clear point at a time."

## Decision Prompt

What is the central claim, and which part of this explanation directly supports it?
