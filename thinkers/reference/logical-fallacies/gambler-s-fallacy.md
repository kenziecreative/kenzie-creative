# Gambler's Fallacy

> The mistaken belief that if something happens more frequently than normal during a given period, it will happen less frequently in the future (or vice versa), in a situation where the events are statistically independent.

## Description

The gambler's fallacy is the mistaken belief that independent random events become "due" to balance out after a streak.

If a fair coin lands heads five times in a row, the next flip is still not owed to tails. The previous flips may feel like they create pressure in the sequence, but the coin has no memory.

This fallacy matters outside gambling whenever people infer that a run of outcomes must reverse simply because it has gone on for a while.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is treating independent events as if they self-correct over short sequences.

Common versions include:

- "We have lost five deals, so the next one is bound to close."
- "This stock has fallen all week, so it has to rebound."
- "They have had too much good luck lately."
- "After this many misses, a hit is due."

## Why It Persuades

Humans expect small samples to look balanced. Random streaks feel suspicious because they do not match our intuitive picture of fairness.

It also gives people emotional relief. Believing a reversal is due can make uncertainty feel more manageable.

## What It Avoids

The fallacy avoids asking:

- Are these events actually independent?
- Has the underlying probability changed?
- Is the sample large enough to infer a pattern?
- What mechanism would make the next event more likely?

## Common False Positives

Some events are not independent. A basketball player's confidence, a machine's wear, market dynamics, or team morale can create real dependencies between outcomes.

The test is whether there is a mechanism connecting past outcomes to future probabilities. "The dice have no memory" applies to independent events, not to systems where feedback changes the odds.

## How to Respond

Shift attention from the streak to the mechanism.

- "Are these outcomes independent?"
- "What changed that would alter the next probability?"
- "How large is the sample?"
- "Are we reacting to randomness or to a real pattern?"

In decisions, replace "due" language with base rates, current conditions, and process quality.

## Decision Prompt

> What evidence shows the next outcome is more likely, besides the fact that the previous outcomes formed a streak?
