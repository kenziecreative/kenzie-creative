# The Straw Man Fallacy

> Misrepresenting someone’s argument to make it easier to attack.

## Description

The straw man fallacy misrepresents a person's actual position, then attacks the distorted version as if it were the real argument.

The distortion may exaggerate, oversimplify, moralize, literalize, or remove context. The speaker wins against a weaker target while avoiding the stronger claim actually made.

Straw man arguments are common in polarized settings because caricatures are easier to reject than complicated, partly reasonable positions.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is replacing the real argument with a weaker substitute.

Common versions include:

- "So you want to eliminate all standards."
- "You are saying we should never take risks."
- "They think money solves everything."
- "You just want everyone to agree with you."

## Why It Persuades

Straw men are persuasive because they make the opponent's position sound extreme, foolish, selfish, or dangerous.

They also give audiences a clean emotional target. A messy tradeoff becomes a simple villain, and the speaker appears decisive for rejecting it.

## What It Avoids

The fallacy avoids engaging the strongest version of the claim:

- What was actually said?
- What is the most reasonable interpretation?
- Which part is true, partly true, or worth considering?
- What tradeoff is the original argument trying to address?

## Common False Positives

Summarizing someone's argument is not automatically a straw man. People often need to simplify to check understanding.

The test is whether the summary is fair enough that the original speaker would recognize it. If the person says, "That is not my position," the conversation should pause and repair the representation before criticism continues.

## How to Respond

Correct the representation before defending the substance.

- "That is not quite what I am saying."
- "The narrower claim is..."
- "Can you repeat my position in a way I would agree with before responding?"
- "I agree that the extreme version would be bad. That is not the proposal."

When you are critiquing someone else, steelman first: state the strongest fair version of their view, then explain where you disagree.

## Decision Prompt

> Am I responding to the actual claim, or to an easier version of it?
