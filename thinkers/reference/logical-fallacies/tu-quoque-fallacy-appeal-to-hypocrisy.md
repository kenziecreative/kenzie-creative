# Tu Quoque Fallacy (Appeal to Hypocrisy)

> Dismissing someone’s argument by attacking their own personal behavior as being inconsistent with their argument.

## Description

Tu quoque, or appeal to hypocrisy, rejects a claim because the person making it behaves inconsistently with it.

The inconsistency may reveal something important about credibility, sincerity, discipline, or fairness. But it does not automatically answer whether the claim is true. A person can fail to live up to a valid principle.

This is a subtype of ad hominem: the person becomes the focus, and the argument is left unresolved.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is treating hypocrisy as disproof.

Common versions include:

- "You do it too, so your criticism is invalid."
- "Look who's talking."
- "You do not practice what you preach."
- "Until you are perfect, you cannot say anything about this."

## Why It Persuades

Hypocrisy is morally salient. People react strongly to inconsistency, especially when someone seems to be claiming authority, superiority, or exemption from rules.

Tu quoque also changes the emotional direction of the exchange. The person who raised the criticism suddenly has to defend themselves instead of the original claim being examined.

## What It Avoids

The fallacy avoids separating two questions:

- Is the claim true?
- Is the speaker consistent, credible, or fair?

Both questions may matter, but one does not automatically settle the other.

## Common False Positives

Calling out hypocrisy is not always fallacious. Hypocrisy can matter when the issue is selective enforcement, hidden incentives, bad faith, abuse of power, or trustworthiness.

The fallacy appears when the inconsistency is used to avoid the substance entirely. "You also do this, so let's apply the rule consistently" is different from "You also do this, so the rule is wrong."

## How to Respond

Separate truth from consistency.

- "My inconsistency may be fair to discuss, but does it change whether the claim is true?"
- "Let's address the rule and then address whether it is being applied consistently."
- "You may be right that I have not lived up to it. The question is whether the standard is sound."
- "If hypocrisy is the concern, what consistent standard should apply to everyone?"

If you are the inconsistent speaker, acknowledge it quickly. Owning the gap reduces the power of the deflection.

## Decision Prompt

> Does the person's inconsistency disprove the claim, or does it raise a separate credibility and fairness issue?
