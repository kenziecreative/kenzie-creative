# The Common Belief Fallacy (Argumentum ad Populum)

> The fallacy of assuming that because many people believe something, it must be true.

## Description

The common belief fallacy, also known as argumentum ad populum, assumes a claim is true because many people believe it.

This overlaps with the bandwagon effect but is narrower. Bandwagon reasoning says, "Many people are doing it, so I should do it." Common belief reasoning says, "Many people believe it, so it must be true."

Shared belief can sometimes be informative, but popularity alone does not establish truth. Large groups can repeat myths, inherit assumptions, copy prestigious voices, or mistake familiarity for evidence.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is using belief-count as proof.

Common versions include:

- "Everyone knows that."
- "Most people agree."
- "It is common sense."
- "That many people cannot all be wrong."

## Why It Persuades

Common belief feels safe. If many people accept something, disagreeing can feel socially risky and cognitively arrogant.

Repetition also creates familiarity, and familiarity can feel like truth. A claim heard often may seem obvious even when no one has checked it.

## What It Avoids

The fallacy avoids asking:

- What evidence supports the belief?
- How did the belief spread?
- Are believers relying on each other rather than independent verification?
- What would knowledgeable dissenters say?

## Common False Positives

Consensus is not automatically fallacious. Expert consensus, broad replication, and accumulated practical experience can be strong evidence.

The difference is whether the belief is popular because it has been independently tested or merely popular because it has been repeated. The more independent, informed, and accountable the agreement is, the stronger the signal.

## How to Respond

Ask for the evidence behind the belief, not just the number of believers.

- "What is the basis for that common belief?"
- "How would we know if this widely held view were wrong?"
- "Are people independently arriving at this, or repeating one another?"
- "What do informed dissenters argue?"

If the claim matters, move from popularity to testable support: data, mechanisms, track record, or expert consensus.

## Decision Prompt

> Is this belief widely held because it is well supported, or does it feel supported because it is widely held?
