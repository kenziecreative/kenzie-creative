# Slippery Slope Fallacy

> Arguing that a relatively small first step will inevitably lead to a chain of related events culminating in some significant (usually negative) effect.

## Description

The slippery slope fallacy claims that a first step will inevitably trigger a chain of events ending in an extreme outcome, without showing that the chain is likely or unavoidable.

The structure is usually: if we allow A, then B will happen, then C, then eventually Z, so A must be rejected. The weakness is not concern about consequences. The weakness is asserting inevitability without evidence for each link.

Slippery slope arguments are powerful in policy, workplace, family, and personal decisions because they make a small change feel like the first step toward losing control.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is treating a possible sequence as an inevitable slide.

Common versions include:

- "If we make one exception, rules will mean nothing."
- "If we allow this policy, total abuse is next."
- "If we miss this deadline, the entire project will collapse."
- "If I try and fail once, I will lose all confidence."

## Why It Persuades

It turns uncertainty into fear. People are more motivated by vivid future loss than by abstract probability.

It also exploits control anxiety. When a boundary moves, people may imagine that no boundaries will remain.

## What It Avoids

The fallacy avoids inspecting the chain:

- What are the intermediate steps?
- How likely is each step?
- Where are the stopping points?
- What safeguards, criteria, or reversibility exist?
- Has this chain happened in comparable cases?

## Common False Positives

Some slopes are real. Small concessions can create precedents, incentives, or path dependence that make later outcomes more likely.

The difference is evidence. A legitimate slope argument can show mechanisms, probabilities, thresholds, and safeguards. A fallacious one relies on inevitability and fear.

## How to Respond

Break the chain into links.

- "What has to happen between A and Z?"
- "Which link is most uncertain?"
- "Where could we stop or reverse course?"
- "What evidence suggests this sequence is likely?"
- "What guardrails would address the concern without rejecting the first step entirely?"

If the concern is legitimate, convert it into a risk-management plan rather than an all-or-nothing rejection.

## Decision Prompt

> Is this a demonstrated chain of incentives and constraints, or a vivid story about losing control?
