# Sunk Cost Fallacy

> Continuing a behavior or endeavor as a result of previously invested resources (time, money, or effort), even when it is clear that the continued investment is not the best decision.

## Description

The sunk cost fallacy happens when past investments of time, money, effort, reputation, or emotion drive a decision that should be based on future value.

Sunk costs are already spent. They may matter emotionally, politically, or narratively, but they cannot be recovered by continuing. The relevant question is what choice creates the best future from here.

This fallacy is common in projects, relationships, careers, purchases, strategies, and beliefs because quitting can feel like admitting the past was wasted.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is using unrecoverable past investment as a reason for future investment.

Common versions include:

- "We have already spent too much to stop."
- "I have come too far to quit."
- "If we cancel now, all that work was for nothing."
- "I need to get my money's worth."

## Why It Persuades

People hate realizing a loss. Continuing preserves the story that the original choice might still be redeemed.

Sunk costs also attach to identity. Quitting can feel like becoming the kind of person or organization that gives up, admits error, or wastes effort.

## What It Avoids

The fallacy avoids the forward-looking decision:

- What are the future costs and benefits?
- What is the opportunity cost of continuing?
- What would we choose if we inherited this situation today?
- What evidence would justify more investment?
- What can be salvaged without continuing the whole path?

## Common False Positives

Past investment is not always irrelevant. It may create capabilities, obligations, switching costs, relationships, or strategic assets that affect future value.

The fallacy appears when the past cost itself is treated as the reason to continue. A legitimate continuation case should point to future returns, remaining costs, and realistic alternatives.

## How to Respond

Reset the decision to the present.

- "If we were starting today, would we choose this?"
- "What future value justifies the next investment?"
- "What are we giving up by continuing?"
- "What can we preserve, learn, sell, reuse, or honor without staying committed?"

If emotion is high, separate the grief of loss from the logic of next action. The past can be acknowledged without being obeyed.

## Decision Prompt

> Am I trying to make the future better, or trying to make the past feel justified?
