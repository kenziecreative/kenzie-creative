# Loaded question

> A loaded question contains an unjustified assumption, trapping the respondent into accepting it by answering.

## Description

A loaded question embeds an unproven assumption inside the question itself. If the respondent answers directly, they may appear to accept the hidden premise.

The classic structure is "Have you stopped doing X?" Both yes and no imply that X was happening. In real conversations, loaded questions are often subtler: they frame motives, guilt, negligence, extremism, incompetence, or bad faith before those things have been established.

Loaded questions can be accidental, but they are especially powerful as a pressure tactic because people are socially trained to answer questions rather than challenge their framing.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is hiding a claim inside a question and then treating the answer as confirmation.

Common versions include:

- "Why did you ignore the data?"
- "When did you decide to stop being transparent?"
- "Why are you opposed to accountability?"
- "Do you admit this was irresponsible?"

## Why It Persuades

Questions feel neutral, even when they are not. A loaded question can smuggle an accusation into the conversation while preserving the appearance of inquiry.

It also creates a timing advantage. The respondent must either answer the question and accept the frame, or challenge the premise and risk looking evasive.

## What It Avoids

Loaded questions avoid proving the embedded premise:

- Did the alleged action happen?
- Is the motive being assumed?
- Are there alternative explanations?
- Is the wording forcing guilt, negligence, or disloyalty?

## Common False Positives

Not every pointed question is loaded. If the premise has already been established, a direct question can be fair.

The issue is whether the question assumes what still needs to be shown. "Why did the launch slip after the vendor missed delivery?" is reasonable if the vendor miss is documented. "Why did you mismanage the launch?" embeds blame before it has been examined.

## How to Respond

Do not accept the premise by reflex. Separate the question from its assumption.

- "I do not accept that premise."
- "What evidence supports the assumption in the question?"
- "Can you ask that without assuming the conclusion?"
- "The question seems to imply X. Are you asking whether X happened?"

If the question is hostile, answer the fair version: "If the question is whether the protocol was followed, here is what happened."

## Decision Prompt

> What would I be conceding if I answered this question exactly as phrased?
