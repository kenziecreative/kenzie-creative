# Equivocation

> Using a word or phrase with multiple meanings to mislead or confuse in an argument.

## Description

Equivocation happens when a key word or phrase shifts meaning inside an argument. The argument appears to hold together because the same term is repeated, but the term is doing different work in different places.

Some equivocation is deliberate. Some is accidental. Either way, it can make people think they agree when they are using different definitions, or make a weak argument appear stronger than it is.

This fallacy is common around abstract words such as freedom, safety, fairness, respect, efficiency, loyalty, harm, evidence, success, and accountability.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is relying on a shared word while changing its meaning.

Common versions include:

- Using "right" to mean both legal permission and moral entitlement.
- Using "efficient" to mean both faster and cheaper.
- Using "safe" to mean both physically safe and emotionally comfortable.
- Using "proven" to mean both strongly supported and absolutely certain.

## Why It Persuades

People often assume shared language means shared meaning. Repetition of the same word creates a feeling of continuity even when the meaning has drifted.

Equivocation also lets a speaker borrow the emotional force of one definition while applying it to another. A word can carry moral weight in one sense and technical meaning in another.

## What It Avoids

Equivocation avoids precision:

- What exactly does this term mean here?
- Is it being used the same way throughout the argument?
- Would the conclusion still follow if we replaced the ambiguous word with a specific definition?
- Are people agreeing with the word while disagreeing about the meaning?

## Common False Positives

Flexible language is not always fallacious. Words can have context-sensitive meanings, and normal conversation often depends on nuance.

The fallacy appears when the shift in meaning changes the logic of the argument, hides a tradeoff, or lets someone claim agreement that has not actually been reached.

## How to Respond

Slow the conversation down and define the important terms.

- "What do you mean by that word in this context?"
- "Are we using this term the same way we used it earlier?"
- "Can we replace the abstract word with a concrete description?"
- "What would this mean in observable behavior or decision criteria?"

In group settings, write the contested term down with a working definition. Many disagreements become clearer once the hidden definitions are visible.

## Decision Prompt

> Is this argument using the same word consistently, or is the meaning shifting as the conclusion changes?
