# False Dilemma

> Presenting a limited set of options, usually two, as if they are the only possible choices when other meaningful options exist.

## Description

A false dilemma presents a limited set of options, usually two, as if they are the only possible choices when other meaningful options exist.

The fallacy is not the existence of a binary. Some choices really are binary: sign or do not sign, leave or stay, vote yes or vote no. The fallacy appears when a complex situation is artificially compressed so that alternatives, combinations, delays, conditions, or reframings disappear.

False dilemmas are powerful because they make the frame feel like reality. Once you accept the offered menu, you may spend all your energy choosing between bad options instead of questioning why those are the only options on the table.

## How It Works

False dilemmas work by controlling the option set.

People often accept the shape of a question before evaluating the answer. If the question is "Do we cut quality or miss the deadline?" the hidden assumption is that scope, staffing, sequencing, stakeholder expectations, and the deadline itself are fixed. The false dilemma makes those assumptions invisible.

It also reduces cognitive load. Two choices feel easier than a messy field of tradeoffs. That simplicity can be clarifying when the binary is real, but distorting when the simplicity is imposed.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is treating the offered menu as complete without proving that other options are impossible, unavailable, or irrelevant.

Common versions include:

- "Either you're with us or against us."
- "We have no choice."
- "It's this or failure."
- "You can have quality or speed, not both."
- "Pick one."
- "There are only two kinds of people..."
- "If you don't support this plan, you must not care about the goal."

## Why It Persuades

False dilemmas are persuasive because they create urgency and moral pressure. They make hesitation feel like weakness, betrayal, or denial of reality.

They also simplify complex decisions. When people are tired, anxious, or overloaded, a binary can feel like relief.

## What It Avoids

False dilemmas avoid questions about the option set:

- What assumptions make these the only choices?
- Can we combine parts of the options?
- Can we sequence the decision?
- Can we change scope, timing, resources, criteria, or constraints?
- Who benefits if alternatives stay invisible?

## Common False Positives

Not every binary is false. Sometimes the structure of the situation really creates two choices: comply with a legal deadline or miss it; accept an offer before it expires or lose it; vote for or against a motion.

The test is whether the options are naturally constrained or rhetorically constrained. A real binary can usually explain why alternatives are unavailable. A false dilemma treats alternatives as unthinkable, disloyal, unserious, or already impossible without showing the work.

## How to Respond

Do not answer the binary immediately. Reopen the option set first.

- Ask, "What makes these the only options?"
- Name assumptions: "This assumes the deadline and scope are both fixed. Are they?"
- Add a third option, even if imperfect, to break the spell of the binary.
- Separate values from options: "We both care about the goal. I disagree that this is the only path."
- If the binary is real, shift to mitigation: "If those are truly the only options, what can we do to reduce the cost of the one we choose?"

## Decision Prompt

> What third, fourth, hybrid, delayed, conditional, or reframed option is being excluded?
