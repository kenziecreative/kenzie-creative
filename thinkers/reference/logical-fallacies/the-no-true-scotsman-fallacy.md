# The No True Scotsman Fallacy

> A logical fallacy where someone dismisses counterexamples to a generalization by changing the definition of the group.

## Description

The No True Scotsman fallacy protects a generalization by redefining the group whenever a counterexample appears.

The classic form is: "No Scotsman would do that." When shown a Scotsman who did, the speaker replies, "Well, no true Scotsman would." The definition has shifted to preserve the claim.

This fallacy appears whenever identity labels, group membership, purity standards, or category definitions are adjusted after the fact to exclude inconvenient cases.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is making a generalization unfalsifiable by changing the definition of the category.

Common versions include:

- "Real professionals would never act that way."
- "They were not a true member of the movement."
- "That is not real leadership."
- "No genuine believer would do that."

## Why It Persuades

It protects identity. People often use groups, values, and labels to anchor self-concept, so counterexamples can feel threatening.

It also preserves simple stories. Instead of revising "people in this group can behave badly," the speaker preserves the idealized category.

## What It Avoids

The fallacy avoids testing the generalization:

- What counted as membership before the counterexample?
- Was the definition stated in advance?
- How many counterexamples would change the claim?
- Is the category descriptive or aspirational?

## Common False Positives

Some categories do have defining criteria. It is not fallacious to say someone does not belong to a group if they fail a clear, stable, relevant definition.

The fallacy appears when the definition changes only after the counterexample appears, or when "true" membership means "members who support my claim."

## How to Respond

Stabilize the definition.

- "What definition are we using for this group?"
- "Was that criterion part of the definition before this example?"
- "What counterexample would actually count?"
- "Are we describing the group as it is, or defending an ideal of what it should be?"

If the category is aspirational, name it as such: "You may mean this violates the group's values, not that the person was never part of the group."

## Decision Prompt

> Is this definition clarifying the category, or protecting the claim from counterexamples?
