# Argument From Ignorance Fallacy

> Assuming that a claim is true because there is no evidence to prove it is false, or that a claim is false because there is no evidence to prove it is true.

## Description

An argument from ignorance treats a lack of evidence as decisive proof. It says a claim must be true because it has not been disproven, or false because it has not been proven.

The fallacy confuses "we do not know" with "we know the opposite." It turns uncertainty into a conclusion before the evidence supports that conclusion.

This matters in decision-making because unknowns are often uncomfortable. People may fill gaps with certainty rather than hold the more accurate position: the available evidence is incomplete.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is converting missing evidence into proof.

Common versions include:

- "No one has proven this is dangerous, so it is safe."
- "No one has proven this works, so it cannot work."
- "You cannot prove it did not happen."
- "There is no record of it, so it never occurred."

## Why It Persuades

It offers closure. A clean answer feels better than sitting with ambiguity, especially when the decision is urgent or emotionally charged.

It can also shift the burden of proof. Instead of supporting a claim, the speaker demands that others disprove it.

## What It Avoids

The fallacy avoids asking:

- Who has the burden of proof?
- What kind of evidence would be expected if the claim were true?
- Have we looked in the right places?
- How should we act under uncertainty without pretending we have certainty?

## Common False Positives

Sometimes absence of evidence is meaningful. If a claim predicts that evidence should be easy to find and repeated searches fail, that absence can count against the claim.

The distinction is whether the absence is expected to be informative. "We found no safety incidents in a well-audited system" is different from "I have not personally heard of any incidents."

## How to Respond

Name the uncertainty without accepting the leap.

- "That shows we do not know yet; it does not prove the claim."
- "Who is making the claim, and what evidence would support it?"
- "What would we expect to observe if this were true?"
- "Can we distinguish 'not proven' from 'disproven' here?"

In decisions, pair the uncertainty with a risk posture: proceed, pause, test, monitor, or gather evidence depending on stakes.

## Decision Prompt

> Am I treating an evidence gap as information, or as a conclusion?
