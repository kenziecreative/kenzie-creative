# Ad Hominem Fallacy

> Attacking the person making the argument, rather than the argument itself.

## Description

An ad hominem fallacy shifts attention from a claim to the character, identity, motives, credentials, or personal history of the person making it.

The problem is not that character never matters. Credibility, incentives, conflicts of interest, and track record can all be relevant. The fallacy appears when those factors are used as a substitute for answering the claim itself.

Ad hominem moves are especially tempting in tense conversations because attacking the messenger feels faster than doing the harder work of evaluating the message.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is treating something about the speaker as if it settles whether their argument is true, false, strong, or weak.

Common versions include:

- "You only believe that because of your background."
- "What would you know? You are not an expert."
- "Of course you would say that."
- "Only someone like you would think this."

## Why It Persuades

Personal attacks are emotionally vivid. They give listeners an easy reason to dismiss a claim without doing much analysis.

They also recruit status instincts. Once a person is framed as foolish, corrupt, naive, biased, or hypocritical, the audience may feel less need to inspect the evidence.

## What It Avoids

Ad hominem avoids the actual question:

- Is the claim accurate?
- Is the evidence sufficient?
- Does the reasoning hold?
- What would change our mind?

## Common False Positives

It is not automatically ad hominem to consider credibility. If someone has a conflict of interest, a history of falsifying evidence, or relevant expertise gaps, that can matter.

The key test is whether credibility is being used to calibrate trust or to dodge the argument. "This source has a conflict, so we should verify the data" is reasonable. "This source has a conflict, so the claim is false" is not enough.

## How to Respond

Bring the conversation back to the claim without getting pulled into defending the person.

- "That may be relevant to credibility, but what is wrong with the argument?"
- "If someone else made the same point, how would we evaluate it?"
- "Let's separate the source from the evidence."
- "What part of the claim do you think is inaccurate?"

If the attack is directed at you, do not spend all your energy repairing your image. Restate the argument and ask for a direct response.

## Decision Prompt

> If I ignored who said this, how would I evaluate the claim, evidence, and reasoning?
