# Red Herring Fallacy

> Introducing an irrelevant topic into an argument to divert the attention of listeners or readers from the original issue.

## Description

A red herring introduces an irrelevant or only loosely related issue to pull attention away from the original question.

The diversion may be emotional, impressive, morally charged, or technically detailed. The key feature is that it changes the subject without resolving the issue that was actually on the table.

Red herrings can be deliberate, but they can also happen when someone feels defensive and reaches for a safer topic.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is substituting a different issue for the one being examined.

Common versions include:

- Answering criticism of one decision by describing unrelated successes.
- Responding to a factual question with a moral speech.
- Introducing a more dramatic problem to make the current one seem petty.
- Shifting from accountability to sympathy without addressing the concern.

## Why It Persuades

Red herrings exploit attention. Once the audience follows the new topic, the original issue loses urgency.

They also exploit emotion. A topic that is more alarming, noble, or personally touching can make returning to the original question feel cold or narrow.

## What It Avoids

The fallacy avoids answering:

- What was the original question?
- Has it been answered?
- How does the new topic bear on the original issue?
- Are we postponing accountability by broadening the conversation?

## Common False Positives

Not every tangent is a red herring. Sometimes a related issue genuinely changes the context, reveals a hidden assumption, or identifies a higher-priority risk.

The test is relevance. A useful reframing explains how it affects the original question. A red herring simply pulls the room elsewhere.

## How to Respond

Acknowledge the new topic without surrendering the original one.

- "That may matter, but the question was..."
- "Let's park that and come back to the current issue."
- "How does this answer the original concern?"
- "We can discuss both, but I do not want to lose the first question."

In meetings, keep a visible issue list so legitimate side topics are captured instead of allowed to derail the discussion.

## Decision Prompt

> Did this new topic help answer the original question, or did it replace it?
