# Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy

> Cherry-picking data to fit a preconceived conclusion, just as a sharpshooter might fire randomly at a barn and then draw a target around the tightest cluster of bullet holes.

## Description

The Texas sharpshooter fallacy happens when someone looks at a set of data, finds a cluster or pattern after the fact, and then treats that pattern as if it confirms a prior hypothesis.

The name comes from the image of firing shots at a barn, then painting the target around the tightest cluster. The target is fitted to the results rather than the results being tested against a target chosen in advance.

This fallacy is common in analytics, politics, health claims, investing, hiring, and storytelling about success.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is treating an after-the-fact pattern as if it were predicted or independently tested.

Common versions include:

- Highlighting one successful prediction while ignoring many failed ones.
- Running many analyses and reporting only the striking result.
- Explaining success by focusing only on survivors.
- Creating a category after seeing which cases fit it.

## Why It Persuades

Patterns are compelling. A cluster feels meaningful, especially when someone wraps it in a confident story.

The fallacy also hides the denominator. Audiences see the hits, not the total number of chances to find a hit.

## What It Avoids

The fallacy avoids precommitment and full accounting:

- Was the hypothesis defined before looking at the data?
- How many comparisons were tried?
- What results were ignored?
- Would the pattern appear in new data?
- What is the base rate of similar-looking clusters by chance?

## Common False Positives

Exploratory analysis is legitimate. Looking for patterns can generate useful hypotheses.

The fallacy appears when an exploratory pattern is presented as confirmed evidence. Exploration should be labeled as exploration and then tested on fresh data or with a pre-registered criterion.

## How to Respond

Ask about the target, the misses, and the denominator.

- "Was this hypothesis defined in advance?"
- "How many other patterns were checked?"
- "What cases do not fit the story?"
- "Does this pattern hold in new data?"
- "What would have counted as a failed prediction?"

In analysis work, separate discovery from confirmation: use one dataset to generate the idea and another to test it.

## Decision Prompt

> Was the target drawn before the shots, or after the cluster appeared?
