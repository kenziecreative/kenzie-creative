# Argument from Authority Fallacy

> Claiming that something must be true simply because it is asserted by someone who is regarded as an "expert" or authority figure.

## Description

An argument from authority fallacy treats a claim as true because an authority figure, expert, institution, celebrity, senior leader, or credentialed person says it is true.

Authority can be useful evidence. Expertise helps people navigate complex domains where they cannot personally verify everything. The fallacy appears when authority replaces evidence, reasoning, domain fit, or independent verification.

This is common in workplaces, politics, marketing, health claims, and any setting where hierarchy or expertise can discourage questioning.

## Flawed Move

The flawed move is using status as proof.

Common versions include:

- "Trust me, I know what I am talking about."
- "The executive team already decided."
- "A famous expert said this, so it must be right."
- "This comes from a prestigious institution."

## Why It Persuades

Authority lowers cognitive effort. If someone credible has already evaluated the issue, accepting their view can feel efficient and socially safe.

It also protects people from status risk. Questioning an authority can feel disrespectful, career-limiting, or socially costly, even when the question is reasonable.

## What It Avoids

The fallacy avoids showing the work behind the claim:

- What evidence supports it?
- Is the authority speaking inside their domain?
- Are there other qualified views?
- What assumptions or incentives might shape the recommendation?

## Common False Positives

It is not a fallacy to rely on expert judgment when the expert is relevant, transparent, and evidence-aligned. In many technical domains, expert consensus is a strong signal.

The mistake is treating authority as the end of inquiry. A responsible appeal to expertise can still explain why the authority is relevant, what evidence they used, and where uncertainty remains.

## How to Respond

Respect the expertise while asking for the reasoning.

- "What evidence led them to that conclusion?"
- "Is this within their area of expertise?"
- "Do other qualified people disagree?"
- "What would we look at if we could not use the credential as the deciding factor?"

In hierarchical settings, make the question about decision quality rather than personal challenge: "I want to understand the basis so we can apply it correctly."

## Decision Prompt

> Am I using authority as a guide to evidence, or as a replacement for evidence?
