# Gaslighting

> Causing someone to doubt their own perception, memory, or judgment by insistently denying or distorting reality.

## Description

Gaslighting is a sustained pattern in which one person undermines another's grasp of what's real. It works by repetition and confidence — the manipulator denies events that happened, insists on a version of the past that contradicts the target's clear memory, reframes the target's emotional reactions as overreactions or imagined, and keeps doing it long enough that the target stops trusting their own mind. The name comes from the 1944 film where a husband dims the gaslights and tells his wife she's imagining it.

It is rarely a single incident. The damage is in the accumulation — months or years of small denials that erode the target's confidence in their own perception until they look to the manipulator to tell them what's true.

## How It Works

Memory is reconstructive, not photographic. When someone we trust contradicts our recollection with confidence, our brains start hedging — adjusting the memory toward the version we're being told. The manipulator exploits this by being relentlessly certain about contested events, never wavering, never granting that the target might be right. Over time the target's epistemic baseline shifts: they stop trusting their own perception by default and start checking it against the manipulator's version.

Three reinforcing mechanisms make gaslighting durable:
1. **Confidence asymmetry.** The manipulator is certain; the target is, by design, uncertain. Confidence reads as credibility.
2. **Isolation.** Gaslighting works better when the target has fewer outside perspectives to corroborate their memory. It often pairs with `isolation`.
3. **Self-doubt as recruitment.** Once the target starts doubting themselves, they become an active participant in the distortion — checking with the manipulator before trusting their own perception.

## Signature Phrasing

- "That never happened."
- "You're remembering it wrong."
- "You're being too sensitive — that's not what I said."
- "You're imagining things."
- "You're crazy."
- "I never said that."
- "Why do you make everything into a problem?"
- "Everyone agrees with me — you're the only one who sees it that way."

## Ask Yourself

> Am I doing more work than usual to confirm my own memory of basic events?

## When It's Done to You

It often starts with a clear-eyed realization: this person says things didn't happen the way they happened. You don't doubt your memory — you doubt what they're going to claim next. So you start collecting evidence. You save texts. You take notes after conversations. You ask other people to confirm what they witnessed. You're building a record because you've learned that without one, the manipulator will rewrite the past and you'll have nothing to push back with.

That evidence-collecting is sane. It's also exhausting, and it's a sign of the relationship's shape — you should not have to litigate basic events with someone who's supposed to be on your side.

The harder, later stage is when the doubt does start to bleed in. After enough repetitions, the manipulator's confidence wears down your own. You start prefacing statements with "I think" or "I might be wrong, but…" when you didn't used to. You feel disoriented after conversations and can't quite say why. You've gone from collecting evidence against their distortion to checking the evidence to make sure your own memory is right.

If any of this is familiar, what to do:

- **Keep the record, but know what it's for.** Contemporaneous notes — texts, dates, quotes — are a defensive tool when you're dealing with someone who reliably distorts what was said. They protect you in real-time arguments and they protect you from drift over time. You don't need to justify keeping them.
- **Triangulate.** Tell one trusted person (outside the manipulator's reach) what's happening. Their reaction is data. If they're alarmed by what you describe, that's information.
- **Notice the pattern, not just the incidents.** Gaslighting works because each individual instance feels small and arguable. The pattern across months is what's damning. Look at the shape of the last six months, not the last six minutes.
- **Stop arguing about reality.** Trying to convince a gaslighter that their version is wrong is the trap. They are not confused; they are insisting. Disengaging from the argument is more protective than winning it.
- **Get outside the system.** This is the pattern where outside help — a therapist, a lawyer, a friend who knew you before — does the most work. Gaslighting is hard to solve from inside the relationship because the relationship is the mechanism.

## What It Is Not

Gaslighting is not ordinary disagreement, sincere misremembering, different interpretations of an ambiguous event, or someone needing time to understand your perspective. People can remember things differently without trying to destabilize each other.

The key features are repetition, insistence, power, and effect. Gaslighting repeatedly pressures the target to distrust their own perception while making the manipulator's version the required version of reality.
