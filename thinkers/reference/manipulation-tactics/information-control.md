# Information Control

> Controlling what the target knows — selective sharing, withholding, asymmetric access — to shape their decisions and constrain their options.

## Description

Information control is power over what the target gets to see. The manipulator becomes the gatekeeper for some category of information that matters to the target — finances, other people's intentions, professional norms, what's happening in the family, what's happening in the relationship — and uses that gatekeeping to shape what the target can decide.

It runs on a spectrum. At the soft end, it's selective sharing — telling the target the parts of a story that lead toward the manipulator's preferred conclusion and omitting the rest. In the middle, it's withholding key information until decisions have already been made. At the hard end, it's outright concealment — a partner who hides finances, a manager who hides why decisions are being made, a family member who controls what other family members know about each other.

It's distinct from privacy. Everyone has things they keep to themselves. Information control becomes a manipulation when the asymmetry serves the manipulator's leverage — when the target is making decisions on partial information specifically because the manipulator has decided what they get to know.

## How It Works

Three mechanisms:

1. **Decisions on incomplete information are bad decisions.** When the manipulator controls the inputs, they can shape the outputs without ever having to argue. The target consents to plans that look reasonable in light of what they know — and what they know is what the manipulator chose for them to know.

2. **Asymmetry creates dependency.** When the manipulator is the only source of information about something — finances, family dynamics, professional context — the target can't function without going through them. Independent action becomes effectively impossible because the target doesn't have the data.

3. **The omission is invisible until later.** Most information control isn't a lie; it's a missing fact. The target can't refute what wasn't said. They often don't realize information was being managed until they encounter the omitted information from another source — sometimes years later, sometimes in the form of a financial surprise, a discovered communication, an offhand comment from a third party.

The technique pairs heavily with **isolation** because isolation removes the alternative information channels. A target with healthy outside relationships and independent access to the relevant domains is harder to manage informationally. A target whose only window onto money, family, work, or even basic news is the manipulator is far more controllable.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I didn't tell you because I didn't want to worry you."
- "You don't need to deal with the financial stuff — I have it handled."
- "I was going to mention it, but it didn't seem important."
- "Don't talk to [person] about this — they'll just confuse the issue."
- "I'm protecting you from [the full picture]."
- "Trust me. You don't want to know the details."
- "It's complicated. Let me handle it."

## Ask Yourself

> What would I be deciding differently right now if I had access to the information this person has?

## When It's Done to You

You're regularly surprised by things you should have known earlier. Decisions get presented to you as fait accompli. You don't have direct access to information that affects your life — finances, schedules, what other people in your circle have said or decided. When you ask for information, you get partial answers, deferrals, or "I'll explain later." You feel like you're navigating with a map that someone else drew, and pieces of the map keep filling in after the fact.

A specific tell: things that affect you keep being decided without your input, and the explanation is always "I didn't think you needed to be involved" or "it just made sense to handle it."

If any of this is familiar, what to do:

- **Get direct access.** Don't accept summaries when you can have the source. Read the bank statements yourself. Get on the email thread. Go to the meeting. Talk to the third party directly. The person who wants to control information will resist this; the resistance itself is data.
- **Notice what gets framed as "protecting you."** Protection can be real. It can also be cover. The way to tell: does the protection require you to remain dependent? Real protection makes you stronger over time. Information control as protection keeps you in the dark.
- **Build alternative channels.** Even if it's awkward. Talk directly to other family members, other team members, other parties. Develop your own sources of information about the domains you've been letting them gatekeep. Independent access ends information control faster than confronting it does.
- **Track surprises.** Keep a mental or actual log of "things I learned about after the fact that affected me." Patterns become visible quickly. If the same kinds of things keep being decided without you, that's the manipulation being made legible.
- **For financial information control, treat it as the serious thing it is.** Financial information control is a documented pattern in coercive-control relationships. If a partner controls all the money information, that's not a minor relationship quirk — it's a structural problem with serious downstream consequences. Don't normalize it.

## When You're Doing It

You can recognize this in yourself by what you decide not to share with people who are affected by what you know. Do you tell them what you know about the finances, about the schedule, about what someone else said about them? Or do you hold it back, decide what they "need to know," shape the version they get?

The story you might be telling yourself: "I'm protecting them from worrying," "it's not their business," "they'll just get upset if I tell them," "I'm waiting for the right time," or "they can't handle it."

Sometimes those things are true — there are real situations where withholding is appropriate (giving someone time, respecting a confidence, waiting for confirmation before raising alarm). The pattern that becomes information control is when withholding is the *default* with this particular person. If you regularly decide what they get to know about things that affect them — and your reasons sound reasonable to you in the moment but the cumulative effect is that they consistently make decisions on partial information — that's information control, even if you'd describe it as care.

A particular form to watch for: **the curated update.** Telling someone what happened is different from telling them what you did. If your version of events systematically makes you look better and them look worse, you're not informing them; you're framing them. They're then making decisions inside the frame you supplied.

## Why It Works for You

Information control usually answers two needs. The first is **protection** — you genuinely don't want them to be hurt by hard information, and managing what they know feels like care. The second is often **control over the outcome** — you have a preferred resolution, and ensuring they decide in line with it is easier when you control what they're deciding on.

The need underneath is real. Wanting to protect people you love from pain is honorable. The corrosive part is that information control doesn't actually protect them — it disempowers them. People who can't see clearly can't act for themselves. The "protection" you're providing is actually the removal of their capacity to navigate their own life. They will eventually realize this, and the consequence is usually a profound rupture of trust — not because of the original hard information, but because of the years of being managed.

## The Honest Version

The honest version is sharing the information that affects them, on their timeline rather than yours, and letting them decide how to handle it.

Instead of: "I didn't tell you because I didn't want to worry you."
Try: telling them, with whatever context helps. Trust them to handle worry; that's what adulthood is.

Instead of: handling the finances quietly so they don't have to think about it.
Try: making sure they have full visibility, even if you do most of the actual administration. The visibility is what matters; the administration is just labor allocation.

Instead of: filtering what other people said into a curated summary.
Try: telling them what was actually said, including the parts you didn't like. Let them have their own reaction.

The structural shift: assume the person you're with can handle information. If you genuinely think they can't, that's a different conversation — about capacity, about the relationship, about what's actually going on. Pre-deciding for them, repeatedly, is how relationships develop the asymmetry that eventually destroys them.

If you find that the prospect of full information transparency feels intolerable — that you'd lose too much control, that they'd react badly, that it would force conversations you don't want to have — that's where the actual work is. Information control is what unspoken anxiety looks like when it has time to become a habit. The conversations you've been avoiding by managing what they know are the conversations that need to happen for the relationship to be real.

## What It Is Not

Information control is not the same as privacy, confidentiality, discretion, or waiting to share unverified information. People are allowed to have private thoughts, protect confidences, and avoid spreading rumors.

The tactic appears when information that affects someone's choices is repeatedly withheld, filtered, or routed through one gatekeeper so their autonomy depends on the gatekeeper's version of reality.
