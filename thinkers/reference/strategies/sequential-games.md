# Sequential Games

> Games where players take turns making their moves, and have some information about the other players’ previous moves.

## Description

A sequential game is a strategic interaction where players move in order, and later players can respond to earlier moves.

The order matters. A first move can create commitment, reveal information, set a frame, trigger retaliation, invite cooperation, or close off options. Unlike simultaneous games, sequential games have a visible path: each move changes what the next move means.

Conversations, negotiations, chess games, legislative processes, hiring funnels, product launches, and conflict escalations often have sequential structure.

## Model Setup

- Players move one after another.
- Each move may reveal information or change available options.
- Players may observe some or all previous moves.
- Strategy includes not only what to choose, but when to choose it.

## Mechanism

Sequential games work through anticipation and response.

Your current move is partly valuable because of what it causes next. A concession may invite reciprocity or exploitation. A public commitment may bind your future self. A delay may create information or signal weakness. The sequence gives actions meaning beyond their immediate effect.

## When to Use

Use this model when:

- Timing matters as much as the move itself.
- One person's action changes another person's options.
- Public commitment, signaling, or sequencing affects outcomes.
- You need to plan not just the next move, but the next few branches.

## When It Backfires

Sequential thinking backfires when you over-script people.

Real humans do not always follow the branch you expect. They misunderstand, refuse, surprise, forget, get emotional, change incentives, or leave the game. Thinking five moves ahead is useful only if you keep updating after each move.

## Real-World Translation

Ask:

- What does this move make easier or harder next?
- What information does it reveal?
- What response does it invite?
- Does moving first help me shape the game, or does waiting give me better information?
- What branch would force me to change plans?

## Decision Prompt

> What future response am I setting up with this move?

## Practical Examples

- In a negotiation, making the first offer can anchor the conversation, but it also reveals information.
- A legislative debate unfolds through proposals, amendments, votes, and public statements. Each move changes the next feasible move.
- A difficult conversation is sequential: starting with accusation creates one path; starting with a specific observation and request creates another.
