# The Role of Intuition

> Fast pattern recognition that should be treated as a signal to inspect, not a fact to obey.

## Description

Intuition is the felt sense that something is right, wrong, promising, risky, familiar, or off before you can fully explain why. It often comes from pattern recognition below conscious narration.

Good intuition can be extremely valuable. Bad intuition can be bias wearing the costume of wisdom.

## Mechanism

Intuition draws on experience, emotion, memory, pattern exposure, and bodily response. It is strongest in domains where the person has repeated feedback, clear patterns, and enough experience to calibrate.

It is weakest in novel environments, emotionally loaded situations, noisy data, and domains where feedback is delayed or misleading.

## When to Use

Use intuition as an input when time is short, information is incomplete, or expertise has been built through repeated feedback.

Use it as a prompt for inquiry when the stakes are high: "What might I be noticing, and how could I test it?"

## When It Backfires

Intuition backfires when it is treated as certainty. A gut feeling may reflect availability bias, fear, attraction, prejudice, past trauma, wishful thinking, or overconfidence.

It also backfires when people dismiss intuition entirely. Sometimes the body notices inconsistency before the conscious mind can name it.

## Practical Use

- Name the intuition without immediately obeying it.
- Ask what pattern it might be detecting.
- Check whether you have real experience in this domain.
- Look for evidence that would confirm or disconfirm the feeling.

## Decision Prompt

Is this intuition calibrated pattern recognition, emotional residue, or a bias asking to be treated as insight?
