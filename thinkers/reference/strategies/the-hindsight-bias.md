# The Hindsight Bias

> A strategy-facing safeguard against rewriting uncertainty after the outcome is known.

## Description

This entry overlaps with the primary cognitive-bias entry for [Hindsight Bias](../cognitive-biases/hindsight-bias.md). Keep this strategy version as a decision-review tool: it focuses on learning from past decisions without pretending the outcome was obvious all along.

Hindsight bias distorts accountability. Good decisions can have bad outcomes, and bad decisions can get lucky. If you judge only from the result, you learn the wrong lesson.

## Mechanism

After an outcome occurs, the mind rebuilds the past around that outcome. Ambiguity shrinks, warning signs look louder, and alternative futures feel less real.

This makes people overconfident about prediction and unfair in evaluating decisions made under uncertainty.

## Strategic Use

Use this lens in retrospectives, postmortems, performance reviews, investing, strategy review, crisis analysis, and personal reflection.

The goal is to reconstruct what was knowable at the time, what assumptions were reasonable, and what process could improve next time.

## When It Backfires

The label backfires when it excuses negligence. Some events were not truly surprising; warnings may have been ignored.

Avoid using hindsight bias as a shield against responsibility. The question is whether the risk was unknowable, known but underweighted, or known and dismissed.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical review separates outcome quality from decision quality. Manipulative review uses hindsight to blame people for not knowing what only became obvious later.

The ethical line is whether the review improves future judgment or merely assigns shame after the fact.

## Practical Examples

- A team compares the original decision memo to the actual result instead of relying on memory.
- A leader asks, "What did we know then?" before judging a failed decision.
- An investor records confidence levels before making a bet, then reviews calibration later.

## Decision Prompt

What did we actually know at the time, and what are we only seeing because the outcome has already happened?
