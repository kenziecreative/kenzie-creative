# Scenario Analysis

> The process of analyzing possible future events by considering alternative possible outcomes.

## Description

Scenario analysis is a way of preparing for uncertainty without pretending you can predict the future.

Instead of asking, "What will happen?", you build a small set of plausible futures and ask, "What would we do if this happened?" The value is not in guessing correctly. The value is in discovering which choices are robust across several futures, which choices only work under one fragile assumption, and which early signals would tell you the world is moving in one direction rather than another.

Good scenario analysis gives you strategic peripheral vision. It helps you notice when the future is starting to diverge from your default story.

## Mechanism

Scenario analysis works by separating **uncertainty** from **preference**.

People tend to plan around the future they hope for, fear most, or find easiest to imagine. Scenario work deliberately creates multiple futures so the mind cannot collapse too quickly around one favored story.

A useful scenario set usually has:

1. **A focal decision.** What choice are you trying to make?
2. **Key uncertainties.** What factors would most change the outcome?
3. **Distinct futures.** What 3-4 plausible combinations of those uncertainties matter?
4. **Implications.** What would each future mean for your decision?
5. **Signals.** What would tell you which scenario is becoming more likely?

## When to Use

Use scenario analysis when a decision is important, uncertain, and exposed to forces you do not control:

- Market shifts, launches, hiring plans, budgets, career moves, policy changes, crises, or long-term strategy.
- Decisions where the cost of being surprised is high.
- Situations where people are treating one forecast as inevitable.
- Planning conversations stuck between optimism and pessimism.

It is most useful when you can act differently depending on which signals appear.

## When It Backfires

Scenario analysis backfires when it becomes theater: a deck of imaginative futures that does not change any decision.

It also fails when you create too many scenarios. The point is not to map every possible future. It is to pick a few futures different enough to stress the decision.

Other failure modes:

- **Smuggling in the preferred future.** One scenario is treated as "real" and the others as decoration.
- **False confidence.** Imagining a scenario can feel like preparing for it, but preparation requires triggers, owners, and actions.
- **Catastrophe fixation.** Worst-case scenarios matter, but if every scenario is doom, the exercise stops helping you choose.

## Ethical Boundary

Scenario analysis can be used to clarify uncertainty or to manipulate it. A leader can present selective scenarios to make one choice feel inevitable.

Use the method to widen perception, not to launder a decision already made. If a scenario set excludes the future that would challenge your preferred plan, the exercise is not honest.

## Practical Examples

- A startup deciding whether to hire aggressively builds three scenarios: demand doubles, demand stays flat, and funding tightens. The aggressive hiring plan only works in the first scenario, while a staged hiring plan performs acceptably in all three.
- A nonprofit planning a program considers three funding futures: grant renewed, grant delayed, grant lost. The exercise reveals which commitments can be made safely now and which should wait.
- A person considering a career change imagines three plausible futures: the new field grows, the field becomes crowded, and their current role improves. The comparison clarifies which move preserves the most optionality.

## Decision Prompt

> Which choice still makes sense across several plausible futures, and what early signal would tell me to adapt?
