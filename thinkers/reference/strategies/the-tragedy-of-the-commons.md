# The Tragedy of the Commons

> A situation where individual users, acting independently according to their own self-interest, behave contrary to the common good of all users by depleting or spoiling a shared resource.

## Description

The Tragedy of the Commons is a shared-resource failure where individually rational use depletes or damages the resource everyone depends on.

The classic example is a shared pasture. Each herder benefits from adding another animal, while the cost of overgrazing is spread across all users. Each individual choice makes sense locally. In aggregate, the pasture is destroyed.

The tragedy is not that people are stupid. It is that the system separates individual benefit from shared cost.

## Model Setup

- A resource is shared.
- Individual users can take from it or burden it.
- Each user receives the full benefit of their own use.
- The cost is distributed across the group or deferred into the future.
- Without governance, overuse becomes likely.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is cost externalization.

If I get the benefit and everyone shares the cost, I have an incentive to take more. If everyone reasons this way, the shared resource collapses. The tragedy is often slow because early extraction looks harmless.

## When to Use

Use this model when:

- A shared resource is degrading.
- Individual incentives reward overuse.
- Costs are delayed, hidden, or spread across many people.
- No one owns maintenance clearly.
- People say, "My small contribution to the problem does not matter."

It applies to fisheries, climate, public spaces, attention commons, shared codebases, meeting calendars, organizational trust, and community moderation.

## When It Backfires

The model backfires when it is used to claim that shared resources always require top-down control.

Commons can be governed by communities, norms, monitoring, quotas, access rules, pricing, stewardship, or mutual accountability. The tragedy is a design warning, not proof that people cannot cooperate.

## Real-World Translation

Design against commons failure by changing incentives:

- Make use visible.
- Limit extraction.
- Assign stewardship.
- Create replenishment duties.
- Price or constrain overuse.
- Protect the resource before collapse makes cooperation harder.

## Decision Prompt

> Who gets the benefit of using this shared resource, and who pays the cost?

## Practical Examples

- Overfishing: each boat benefits from catching more fish, but the depleted fishery harms everyone.
- A shared codebase: each team ships shortcuts to meet deadlines, while maintenance debt accumulates for the whole engineering organization.
- A company calendar: every team schedules meetings because each meeting seems locally useful, but the shared attention pool collapses.
