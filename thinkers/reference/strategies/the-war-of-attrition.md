# The War of Attrition

> A game where two players engage in a costly conflict, and the winner is the one who can hold out the longest.

## Description

The War of Attrition is a model of a costly standoff where the winner is the player willing or able to endure longer.

Both sides pay a cost while the conflict continues. The prize may go to whoever refuses to quit, but the longer the contest lasts, the more value is burned. The winner can end up winning something that has become less valuable than the cost of winning it.

This model explains price wars, lawsuits, strikes, political standoffs, relationship deadlocks, and ego-driven conflicts where the question becomes not "what is the best outcome?" but "who will blink first?"

## Model Setup

- Two or more players want a prize or concession.
- Each player can continue or quit.
- Continuing imposes a cost over time.
- The player who holds out longest wins, unless the prize is destroyed first.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is endurance as leverage.

Each side hopes the other will quit first. But as both continue, both pay. The sunk-cost pull gets stronger: "We have already suffered this much, so we cannot stop now." The conflict can become self-justifying.

## When to Use

Use the model to recognize attrition dynamics, not to romanticize them.

It applies when:

- The cost of continuing accumulates.
- Each side believes quitting is worse than enduring.
- The prize is fixed or symbolic.
- Public commitment makes backing down expensive.
- Both sides may be overestimating their staying power.

## When It Backfires

Attrition backfires when endurance becomes identity.

You may win the formal contest and lose money, trust, health, time, reputation, or future options. You may also misread the other side's constraints and discover they can endure longer than you.

## Real-World Translation

Before staying in, ask:

- What is the cost per unit of time?
- What is the prize still worth?
- What is my exit rule?
- What would make the other side quit, and is that assumption real?
- Is there a side deal that preserves more value than continued endurance?

## Decision Prompt

> If I were not already in this fight, would I pay today's cost to enter it now?

## Practical Examples

- Two airlines enter a price war. Both lose money while waiting for the other to exit the route.
- Two people in a conflict refuse to apologize first. The relationship keeps paying the cost while each waits for the other to break.
- A lawsuit continues because both sides have spent too much to accept a settlement that would have looked reasonable earlier.
