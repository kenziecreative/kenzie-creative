# Parkinson's Law

> Work tends to expand to fill the time, attention, and complexity allowed for it.

## Description

Parkinson's Law names a common planning failure: tasks grow to occupy the space available. Extra time can turn into extra polish, extra anxiety, extra meetings, extra scope, or delayed starting.

The lesson is not "always use unrealistic deadlines." The lesson is that open-ended capacity invites expansion unless the work has clear constraints.

## Mechanism

Work expands because people respond to available slack, ambiguity, status, perfectionism, and avoidance. If completion is not clearly defined, the task can keep absorbing attention.

Time boxes, decision rules, and scope limits counteract expansion by forcing tradeoffs earlier.

## When to Use

Use Parkinson's Law when planning projects, meetings, writing, review cycles, decision processes, and personal work blocks.

It is especially useful when a task is important enough to become bloated but not clear enough to finish.

## When It Backfires

It backfires when tight deadlines become a management weapon. Compressing work beyond reality causes quality problems, burnout, hidden debt, and fear.

It also backfires when speed becomes the only value. Some work genuinely requires time for exploration, relationship, or reflection.

## Practical Use

- Define "done" before starting.
- Time-box exploratory work separately from execution.
- Shorten meetings by clarifying the decision needed.
- Use constraints to reveal what matters, not to deny reality.

## Decision Prompt

What limit would force the useful version of this work to appear without creating avoidable damage?
