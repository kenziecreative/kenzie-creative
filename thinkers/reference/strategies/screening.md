# Screening

> Designing choices, questions, or tests that help reveal information you cannot directly observe.

## Description

Screening is used by the less-informed party in an information asymmetry. You cannot directly see someone's reliability, priorities, risk level, competence, honesty, or fit, so you design a process that gives those qualities a chance to reveal themselves.

Good screening does not mean making people jump through hoops. It means asking for evidence that is relevant, proportional, and hard to fake.

## Mechanism

Screening works through self-selection and evidence. A well-designed choice causes different types of people to choose differently. A well-designed test reveals the behavior or capacity you actually care about.

The screen must match the hidden information. If you want to know whether someone can do the work, a realistic work sample may reveal more than a polished interview. If you want to know whether a partner is serious, a small early commitment may reveal more than enthusiasm.

## When to Use

Use screening when the cost of being wrong is meaningful and the other party has information you do not. Common settings include hiring, lending, insurance, vendor selection, partnerships, dating, community access, and high-trust collaboration.

Use it gently when the relationship matters. A screen can protect both sides by making expectations clear early.

## When It Backfires

Screening backfires when it measures what is easy instead of what matters. A burdensome hiring process may select for people with free time rather than people with ability. A social test may select for performance rather than sincerity.

It also backfires when it becomes intrusive, biased, or humiliating. A screen that damages trust may cost more than the risk it was meant to reduce.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical screening is relevant, proportionate, transparent enough to be fair, and respectful of dignity. Unethical screening extracts free labor, invades privacy, discriminates through proxies, or sets traps.

The ethical question is whether the screen reveals needed information or simply shifts risk and burden onto the other person.

## Practical Examples

- A hiring team uses a paid, realistic work sample instead of vague personality questions.
- A buyer asks for references, insurance, and a small pilot before signing a large vendor contract.
- A community sets a clear moderation policy and observes early participation before granting broader access.

## Decision Prompt

What do I need to learn, what evidence would reveal it fairly, and what burden does the screen place on the other person?
