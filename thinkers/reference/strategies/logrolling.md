# Logrolling

> Trading across issues so each side gives on lower-priority items to gain what matters most.

## Description

Logrolling is a negotiation strategy for multi-issue situations. Instead of splitting every issue down the middle, each side identifies what it values most and trades concessions on issues it values less. The goal is not equal pain. The goal is a package where both sides get more of what matters to them.

This is one of the main ways negotiations move from zero-sum bargaining to mutual-gain problem solving. It requires curiosity about the other side's priorities rather than fixation on their first stated position.

## Mechanism

Logrolling works because people often care about different things. One side may care more about price, while the other cares more about speed, predictability, status, risk, creative control, future access, or reduced administrative burden.

When priorities differ, concessions are not equally costly. You can give up something that matters less to you in exchange for something that matters more. The overall agreement can become better for both sides without pretending that everyone values every term the same way.

## When to Use

Use logrolling when a negotiation has multiple issues, when both sides seem stuck on one visible demand, or when the stated disagreement may be masking different underlying priorities.

It is especially useful in workplace planning, partnerships, household decisions, contracts, and team conflicts where people need to keep working together after the agreement is made.

## When It Backfires

Logrolling backfires when one side does not understand its own priorities. If you trade away something that later turns out to matter, the agreement can feel like a betrayal even if it looked rational at the time.

It also backfires when the negotiation includes hidden stakeholders. A manager might trade away a reporting requirement that seems minor to them but is essential for finance, compliance, or another team.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical logrolling makes priorities explicit enough for informed tradeoffs. It becomes manipulative when one side hides a critical consequence, disguises a concession as costlier than it is, or trades away the interests of people who are not represented.

The standard is not perfect transparency about every preference. It is enough clarity that the trade does not depend on deception.

## Practical Examples

- A candidate accepts a slightly lower starting salary in exchange for remote flexibility, a learning budget, and a six-month compensation review.
- Two teams competing for the same engineering time agree that one gets the next sprint while the other gets priority in the following release and more influence over shared tooling.
- In a vendor negotiation, the buyer gets stronger support guarantees while the vendor gets a longer contract term.

## Decision Prompt

Which issues matter more to each side, and what can we trade because our priorities are different?
