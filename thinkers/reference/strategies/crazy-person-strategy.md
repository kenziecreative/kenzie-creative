# Crazy Person Strategy

> Performing irrationality or unpredictability to make others afraid of challenging you.

## Description

Crazy Person Strategy, sometimes discussed as a "madman" posture, is an intimidation pattern. A person tries to make others believe they are volatile enough to create disproportionate costs if opposed.

The goal is not genuine irrationality. The goal is leverage through fear: making other people manage, appease, or preempt the supposedly unpredictable actor.

## Mechanism

The tactic works by increasing perceived downside. If people believe a person may explode, retaliate, derail the meeting, punish dissent, or destroy the deal, they may concede simply to avoid the episode.

It is often confused with authenticity or strong emotion. The diagnostic is whether volatility repeatedly produces compliance from others.

## Defensive Use

Use this lens when someone's unpredictability consistently shifts burden onto everyone else. Ask what concessions the behavior extracts and what would happen if others stopped organizing around it.

Defensive responses include slowing the interaction, moving to written agreements, refusing rushed concessions, involving neutral structure, and setting limits on participation.

## When It Backfires

It backfires by destroying trust, reputation, and future cooperation. People may comply in the moment while quietly reducing access, building coalitions, documenting behavior, or leaving.

It also fails when the other side has strong boundaries or better alternatives. Volatility only creates leverage when others feel trapped.

## Ethical Boundary

Using fear of instability to control others is coercive. Strong emotion is human; strategic volatility that makes others responsible for preventing your escalation is manipulation.

The ethical line is whether the behavior expresses a real state honestly or is being used to make other people surrender agency.

## Practical Examples

- A negotiator suddenly becomes explosive whenever price is discussed, training the other side to avoid the issue.
- A leader's unpredictable anger causes staff to pre-clear harmless decisions, concentrating power through fear.
- A family member threatens emotional collapse whenever boundaries are named, making others responsible for avoiding the reaction.

## Decision Prompt

Is this unpredictability information I should respect, or pressure designed to make me manage someone else's escalation?
