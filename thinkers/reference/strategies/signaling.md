# Signaling

> A credible action that reveals private qualities, intentions, constraints, or commitments.

## Description

Signaling is what one party does when it knows something about itself that others cannot directly observe. The signal is meant to make that hidden quality believable: competence, seriousness, trustworthiness, resources, commitment, need, or intent.

Words can signal, but costly or observable actions usually signal more strongly. Showing up on time, making a deposit, publishing audited results, accepting accountability, or taking a visible risk can communicate more than a claim.

## Mechanism

A signal becomes credible when it is harder or costlier for the wrong type of person to send. If anyone can say "I am committed," the statement is weak. If someone rearranges resources, accepts consequences, or submits to verification, the signal carries more information.

Signals also depend on audience interpretation. A costly action can be misread if the audience does not value the cost or does not understand what it proves.

## When to Use

Use signaling when trust, quality, or seriousness is not yet established and the other party has reasonable uncertainty. It is useful in hiring, sales, leadership, negotiation, dating, markets, and public commitments.

Choose signals that are relevant to the thing you are trying to prove. If you want to signal reliability, show reliability. If you want to signal expertise, expose your reasoning or track record.

## When It Backfires

Signaling backfires when the signal becomes detached from the underlying reality. Status symbols, credentials, performative busyness, and public declarations can crowd out the actual quality they were meant to reveal.

It can also create arms races. When everyone spends more on signals, the signal may become more expensive without making decisions more accurate.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical signaling makes true qualities easier to evaluate. Unethical signaling counterfeits credibility, hides relevant weakness, or uses costly display to intimidate rather than inform.

The ethical test is whether the signal helps the audience make a better judgment or merely manipulates their perception.

## Practical Examples

- A consultant shares a concise diagnostic before proposing work, signaling competence through the quality of thinking rather than claims.
- A company offers a strong warranty because it is confident in product quality and willing to bear the cost if wrong.
- A leader publicly names a measurable commitment, then reports progress against it, signaling seriousness through accountability.

## Decision Prompt

What hidden quality needs to be believed, and what action would credibly reveal it without pretending to prove more than it does?
