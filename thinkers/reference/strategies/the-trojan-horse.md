# The Trojan Horse

> A hidden threat packaged as a gift, opportunity, tool, favor, or harmless request.

## Description

The Trojan Horse pattern appears when something presented as beneficial or low-risk carries a concealed cost, dependency, obligation, access path, or threat. The surface offer lowers defenses; the hidden payload creates the real effect.

This can appear in cybersecurity, contracts, relationships, politics, workplace favors, and influence attempts. The common structure is attractive exterior, concealed risk.

## Mechanism

The tactic exploits trust and urgency. People scrutinize threats more carefully than gifts. If the offer feels generous, convenient, prestigious, or time-sensitive, the recipient may skip normal checks.

The hidden risk often appears only after acceptance, when reversal is costly or embarrassing.

## Defensive Use

Use this lens when an offer asks for access, commitment, data, dependency, public endorsement, confidentiality, or urgency disproportionate to its stated value.

The defensive move is not cynicism toward every gift. It is proportionate inspection: what comes bundled with this, what access does it create, what future obligation does it imply, and what would it cost to unwind?

## When It Backfires

For the person using it, the Trojan Horse pattern destroys credibility once discovered. For the target, paranoia can also backfire if every offer is treated as hostile.

The skill is calibrated caution: more scrutiny when stakes, access, or irreversibility are high.

## Ethical Boundary

Hiding material risk inside a benevolent presentation is deception. Ethical offers make meaningful costs, constraints, and obligations visible enough for informed choice.

The boundary is whether the recipient can understand what they are accepting.

## Practical Examples

- A "free" tool requests unnecessary access to sensitive data.
- A contract concession hides a long-term dependency in a clause few people notice.
- A workplace favor creates an implied personal debt that later becomes pressure.

## Decision Prompt

What access, obligation, dependency, or hidden cost comes bundled with this attractive offer?
