# The Dollar Auction

> A game that illustrates how people can get trapped into an escalating commitment to a losing course of action.

## Description

The Dollar Auction is a model of escalation trap: people keep investing in a losing contest to avoid realizing a loss.

The classic game auctions a dollar. The highest bidder wins the dollar, but the second-highest bidder must also pay their bid and gets nothing. Once two bidders are near a dollar, the second-highest bidder faces a trap: quit and lock in the loss, or bid again and hope to escape. The other bidder then faces the same trap. Soon people bid more than a dollar for a dollar.

The model shows how rational local moves can become irrational globally.

## Model Setup

- There is a prize.
- Multiple players invest to win it.
- Losing after investing is painful.
- Additional investment appears to reduce or avoid the immediate loss.
- The contest structure keeps moving the loss forward.

## Mechanism

The Dollar Auction works through loss aversion and sunk cost.

Each new bid is justified as avoiding the pain of the previous bid. The player stops asking, "Is the prize worth this total cost?" and starts asking, "Can I spend a little more to avoid losing what I already spent?"

That shift is the trap.

## When to Use

Use this model when you see escalation driven by fear of being the loser:

- Bidding wars.
- Lawsuits.
- Product rebuilds that keep missing.
- Political campaigns.
- Personal conflicts where apology feels like losing.
- Projects kept alive because killing them would embarrass the sponsor.

## When It Backfires

The model backfires if you use it to dismiss all persistence. Sometimes continuing is correct because the future payoff still exceeds the future cost.

The Dollar Auction warning applies when the reason for continuing is mainly the pain of stopping, not the value of continuing.

## Real-World Translation

The protection is a pre-committed exit rule:

- Maximum bid.
- Maximum time.
- Kill criteria.
- Independent review.
- A rule that ignores past cost and evaluates only future cost and future value.

## Decision Prompt

> Am I investing because the future value justifies it, or because stopping would make the past loss real?

## Practical Examples

- Two companies bid for an acquisition target. The price exceeds the target's rational value, but each side has already invested reputation and executive attention. Losing now feels humiliating, so both keep bidding.
- A team keeps rebuilding a failed product because canceling it would make the last year feel wasted.
- Two people keep escalating an argument because backing down now would make the previous harsh words feel unjustified.
