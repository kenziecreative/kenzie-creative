# Flexible Approaches

> Adapting means while preserving the goal as new evidence changes the terrain.

## Description

A flexible approach keeps the objective clear while treating the path as adjustable. It assumes the first plan was made with incomplete information and should improve through contact with reality.

Flexibility is not aimlessness. It is disciplined adaptation: notice, learn, adjust, and keep moving toward the underlying purpose.

## Mechanism

Flexibility works by shortening the distance between evidence and adjustment. Instead of defending the original plan, you treat feedback as information about the terrain.

The stable part is intent. The flexible parts are tactics, sequencing, resource allocation, roles, timing, and sometimes even the definition of success.

## When to Use

Use flexible approaches in complex, changing, or uncertain environments: product work, negotiations, learning, organizational change, creative projects, conflict, and strategy.

They are especially useful when feedback is available and the cost of small adjustment is lower than the cost of late failure.

## When It Backfires

Flexibility backfires when it becomes lack of commitment. Constant pivots can exhaust people, destroy trust, and prevent compounding progress.

It also backfires when leaders change direction without explaining what was learned. Adaptation should feel connected to evidence, not mood.

## Practical Use

- Name the goal and the assumptions behind the current path.
- Decide in advance what evidence would trigger a change.
- Keep changes small enough to learn from.
- Communicate what is stable and what is open to adjustment.

## Decision Prompt

What should stay fixed because it expresses the goal, and what should change because reality has taught us something?
