# Scarcity

> The increase in perceived value or urgency when something appears limited, exclusive, or disappearing.

## Description

Scarcity changes attention. When time, access, inventory, status, opportunity, or attention feels limited, people often evaluate faster and fear missing out.

Scarcity can reflect reality. Seats, budgets, time, energy, and openings can genuinely be limited. It becomes manipulative when the limitation is exaggerated, hidden, or manufactured to rush judgment.

## Mechanism

Scarcity works through loss aversion and opportunity cost. Losing a possible option can feel more painful than never having seen it. A closing window makes people focus on what they might miss rather than whether the option is right.

It also signals social value: if something is hard to get, people may assume it is desirable.

## When to Use

Use scarcity ethically by making real constraints visible: limited capacity, real deadlines, finite attention, expiring terms, or genuine availability.

Use it defensively when urgency appears. Ask whether the constraint is real, whether it matters to your decision, and what you would choose without the time pressure.

## When It Backfires

Scarcity backfires when people discover the limit was fake. Trust falls quickly when "last chance" turns out to mean "until the next last chance."

It can also create poor decisions by narrowing attention. People may buy, agree, or commit to avoid loss rather than because the choice serves them.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical scarcity clarifies a real constraint. Manipulative scarcity manufactures panic or hides the fact that the person has more time, options, or access than claimed.

The ethical question is whether urgency helps someone make a timely decision or prevents them from making a clear one.

## Practical Examples

- A facilitator says a meeting has only ten minutes left and moves the group to the decision that must happen today.
- A seller truthfully says only three spots remain because service quality depends on capacity.
- A buyer pauses after hearing "today only" and asks what materially changes tomorrow.

## Decision Prompt

What is actually scarce, who controls that scarcity, and would I still want this without the fear of missing out?
