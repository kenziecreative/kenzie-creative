# The Veil of Ignorance

> A fairness test: design the rule as if you did not know what position you would occupy under it.

## Description

The Veil of Ignorance is a thought experiment from philosopher John Rawls. Imagine you are designing a society, institution, policy, or rule, but you do not know where you will land inside it. You do not know your class, race, gender, health, ability, family background, talents, beliefs, social position, or bargaining power. You only know that once the veil lifts, you will have to live under the rule you designed.

The thought experiment asks: what would you choose if you could not rig the rule for your current position?

Its power is that it strips away self-serving certainty. A policy that looks reasonable from the top may look predatory from the bottom. A workplace norm that feels efficient to leadership may feel punishing to caregivers, junior employees, disabled people, or anyone with less leverage. The veil forces the decision-maker to reason without knowing whether they will be powerful or vulnerable.

## Core Test

Ask:

> Would I still choose this rule if I did not know whether I would be advantaged or disadvantaged by it?

Then make the uncertainty concrete:

- What if I were the least powerful person affected?
- What if I had the least money, time, health, status, or information?
- What if I had to live under this rule without special access or exceptions?
- What protections would I want built in before I knew who I would be?

The test does not require identical outcomes for everyone. It asks whether the rule can be justified from a position of impartial uncertainty.

## What It Helps You See

The veil is useful for detecting hidden self-interest in rules that claim to be neutral.

It helps reveal:

- Rules that are easy for powerful people to comply with and costly for vulnerable people.
- Policies that shift risk downward.
- Exceptions that quietly protect insiders.
- Systems that reward people for advantages they did not earn.
- Decisions that look efficient only because someone else's cost is being ignored.

It is especially useful when the decision-maker will not personally bear the worst consequences of the decision.

## When to Use

Use the veil when designing rules, policies, norms, contracts, institutions, or defaults that affect people in unequal positions.

Good contexts:

- Workplace policies around pay, leave, promotion, remote work, performance, and discipline.
- Product decisions that affect users with less information or power than the company.
- Public policy, taxation, healthcare, education, housing, criminal justice, and infrastructure.
- Family or group decisions where one person has more leverage.
- Any decision where you catch yourself thinking, "This seems fair from my side."

## When It Backfires

The veil breaks down when it becomes too abstract.

People cannot actually forget who they are. They may imagine the "least advantaged" person badly, or design for a generic vulnerable person who does not exist. The thought experiment should be paired with listening to people actually affected by the decision.

It can also over-correct toward safety if used alone. A rule designed only to protect the worst case may become rigid, slow, or unable to reward effort and excellence. Rawls's frame is a fairness test, not the only value that matters.

Another failure mode: using the veil performatively. "I considered everyone" is not the same as designing a rule that the least powerful person could reasonably accept.

## Practical Use

Use the veil as a structured check:

1. Name the rule or decision.
2. List the groups affected.
3. Identify who has the least power, information, money, time, or ability to exit.
4. Ask what the rule costs that person.
5. Redesign the rule so you could accept it without knowing which role you would occupy.
6. Reality-check the redesign with people closer to the affected position.

## Decision Prompt

> If I woke up tomorrow as the person with the least power under this rule, what protection would I wish the rule already had?

## Practical Examples

- A city council deciding where to build a landfill asks what process it would accept if council members did not know which neighborhood they lived in. The answer likely requires transparent criteria, environmental safeguards, compensation, and a refusal to dump the burden on the least politically powerful area.
- A company designing a remote-work policy asks what rule would be fair if executives did not know whether they would be parents, disabled employees, junior staff, managers, or people without quiet home offices.
- A product team designing cancellation flows asks what process would feel fair if they were the customer with the least technical skill, least time, and least ability to absorb another monthly charge.
