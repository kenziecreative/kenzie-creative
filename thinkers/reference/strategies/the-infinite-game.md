# The Infinite Game

> A game that is played not to win, but to continue playing.

## Description

The concept of the Infinite Game, popularized by James Carse and Simon Sinek, distinguishes between two types of games. A finite game is played for the purpose of winning. It has known players, fixed rules, and an agreed-upon objective. A football game is a finite game. An infinite game, on the other hand, is played for the purpose of continuing the play. It has known and unknown players, the rules are changeable, and the objective is to keep the game going. Business, careers, and life are all infinite games.

## When to Use

This is a mindset to adopt for any long-term endeavor. When you realize you are playing an infinite game, you stop thinking about how to “win” and you start thinking about how to build a system that is resilient and adaptive enough to keep playing, generation after generation. Your goal is not to beat your competitors, but to build an organization that can outlast them.

## When It Backfires

It can backfire if you are playing an infinite game with a finite mindset. If you are obsessed with short-term wins, with beating your quarterly numbers, and with vanquishing your rivals, you will make decisions that are good for the short term but terrible for the long term. You will sacrifice trust for transactions, and you will burn out your people and your resources in the pursuit of a “victory” that is ultimately meaningless.

## Practical Examples

- The Cold War was a classic example of two players (the US and the USSR) playing an infinite game with a finite mindset. Both were so obsessed with “winning” that they brought the world to the brink of nuclear annihilation. A more infinite-minded player would have been focused not on destroying the other, but on building a more stable and prosperous world system.
