# Liking

> The influence created when warmth, similarity, familiarity, or admiration makes a person easier to trust.

## Description

Liking is a powerful social shortcut. We are more receptive to people we enjoy, identify with, feel respected by, or want approval from. That can support healthy rapport, but it can also bias judgment.

The key distinction is whether liking opens the door to attention or replaces evaluation. Good relationships make trust easier; they should not make evidence unnecessary.

## Mechanism

Liking lowers defensiveness. Similarity, sincere appreciation, shared identity, humor, cooperation, and repeated positive contact can make a request or idea feel safer.

It can also create a halo effect, where one appealing quality spills over into judgments about competence, honesty, or fit.

## When to Use

Use liking ethically by building genuine rapport, showing respect, and finding real common ground. In leadership and collaboration, people often hear hard truths better from someone they believe respects them.

Use it defensively when charm, similarity, or personal warmth is making you skip normal judgment.

## When It Backfires

Liking backfires when it leads to favoritism, poor hiring, weak accountability, or vulnerability to charm. It can also pressure people to say yes because saying no feels socially costly.

It damages trust when warmth is revealed as purely instrumental.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical rapport respects the other person's agency. Manipulative liking creates artificial intimacy, flattery, or similarity to lower someone's guard.

The ethical test is whether the relationship would still feel honest if the influence goal were named.

## Practical Examples

- A manager builds rapport before giving feedback, but still grounds decisions in evidence.
- A buyer notices they prefer one vendor mainly because the representative is enjoyable, then checks objective criteria.
- A colleague uses sincere appreciation to make collaboration easier, not to pressure agreement.

## Decision Prompt

Is liking helping me listen more openly, or is it making me evaluate less carefully?
