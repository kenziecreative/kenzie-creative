# The Impostor Syndrome

> A pattern of discounting competence and fearing exposure despite meaningful evidence of ability.

## Description

Impostor syndrome is the experience of feeling like a fraud despite evidence that you are capable, trusted, or legitimately learning. It often appears in transitions, high-achievement environments, underrepresentation, public evaluation, or roles with unclear standards.

The feeling is not proof of incompetence. It is a signal about threat, identity, comparison, and uncertainty.

## Mechanism

Impostor feelings persist through biased evidence accounting. Success is attributed to luck, timing, help, or deception; mistakes are treated as proof of unworthiness.

The pattern can become self-reinforcing. Fear of exposure leads to overwork, hiding uncertainty, avoiding risk, or rejecting recognition, which prevents accurate calibration.

## When to Use

Use this lens when someone repeatedly dismisses achievement, feels uniquely unqualified, or believes normal learning gaps mean they do not belong.

Use it on yourself when fear of being exposed is stronger than the actual evidence against your competence.

## When It Backfires

The label backfires when it individualizes a real structural problem. Sometimes people feel like outsiders because the environment is biased, vague, hostile, or exclusionary.

It also backfires if used to dismiss legitimate skill gaps. The answer may be both reassurance and development.

## Practical Use

- Track evidence of competence and growth, not just mistakes.
- Separate "I am learning" from "I am a fraud."
- Ask trusted people what standards they are actually using to judge the work.
- Look for environmental factors that make belonging feel conditional.

## Decision Prompt

What evidence am I discounting, what standard am I imagining, and what support or skill-building would make this clearer?
