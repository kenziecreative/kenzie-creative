# Opportunity Cost

> The value of the next-best alternative that you give up when you make a choice.

## Description

Opportunity cost is the value of the best alternative you give up when you choose something.

It is the invisible price of a yes. When you spend a dollar, an hour, a favor, a meeting slot, a week of attention, or a year of life on one path, that same resource cannot be spent on the next-best path. The cost of the choice is not only what you paid. It is what the choice prevents.

Opportunity cost matters because people tend to evaluate choices in isolation: "Is this worth doing?" The sharper question is comparative: "Is this worth doing more than the best other thing I could do with the same resources?"

## Mechanism

Attention and resources feel reusable until they are actually committed. Opportunity-cost thinking makes the tradeoff visible before commitment hardens into momentum.

It works by forcing three clarifications:

1. **Scarce resource.** What is actually constrained: money, time, energy, attention, reputation, trust, political capital, or optionality?
2. **Next-best alternative.** What is the strongest realistic use of that same resource?
3. **Comparative value.** Does the chosen option beat that alternative by enough to justify the commitment?

The discipline is not about listing every possible thing you could do. It is about naming the best displaced option.

## When to Use

Use opportunity cost when a decision consumes a scarce resource:

- Taking on a project, client, job, partnership, or obligation.
- Deciding whether to continue something that already has momentum.
- Allocating team focus across competing priorities.
- Choosing between comfort now and optionality later.
- Saying yes because the request is "small" without noticing the cumulative load.

It is especially useful when something looks free. A free meeting still costs attention. A free favor still costs time and goodwill. A free tool can still cost switching effort, privacy, or future lock-in.

## When It Backfires

Opportunity-cost thinking backfires when it becomes an excuse to never commit.

The point is not to keep every door open forever. Optionality has its own cost. If you keep comparing after a decision is made, you can drain attention from the path you chose and turn every commitment into regret.

It also backfires when people compare a real option to an imaginary perfect alternative. The next-best alternative must be real enough to execute. "I could be doing something more meaningful with my life" is not a usable comparison until it becomes a specific path with actual costs.

## Ethical Boundary

Opportunity-cost language can be used coldly. Not every human commitment should be reduced to optimization. Relationships, duties, care, grief, and integrity often involve choices whose value is not captured by efficiency.

Use opportunity cost to clarify tradeoffs. Do not use it to launder selfishness as rationality or to pressure someone into treating every obligation as a market transaction.

## Practical Examples

- A company invests $1 million in a marketing campaign. The opportunity cost is not just the money. It is the engineers they did not hire, the product debt they did not address, the runway they gave up, and the experiment they can no longer afford.
- You accept a standing weekly meeting because it seems harmless. The opportunity cost is not one hour; it is the deeper work that never gets a clean block, plus the context-switching cost around the meeting.
- You stay in a role because it is comfortable. The opportunity cost may be learning, salary growth, creative energy, or proximity to the work you actually want.

## Decision Prompt

> What is the best real alternative use of this same resource, and would I still choose this if that alternative were placed next to it?
