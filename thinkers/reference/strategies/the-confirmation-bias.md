# The Confirmation Bias

> A strategy-facing safeguard against building decisions only from evidence that already agrees with you.

## Description

This entry overlaps with the primary cognitive-bias entry for [Confirmation Bias](../cognitive-biases/confirmation-bias.md). Keep this strategy version as a practical decision safeguard: it focuses on how to design choices, meetings, research, and plans so disconfirming evidence can survive.

Confirmation bias is dangerous strategically because it makes a bad plan feel increasingly proven. Every selected data point strengthens confidence while the missing evidence disappears from view.

## Mechanism

People seek, notice, interpret, and remember evidence that supports what they already believe. In organizations, this can become process design: teams ask friendly questions, choose confirming metrics, invite agreeable voices, and call the result analysis.

The bias is strongest when identity, status, money, or prior commitment is attached to the belief.

## Strategic Use

Use this lens before major decisions, reviews, debates, and research efforts. Design the process so the easiest path is not merely finding support.

The practical aim is not to become perfectly unbiased. It is to make contradiction easier to find and harder to punish.

## When It Backfires

The label backfires when used only against other people. "You are biased" often becomes a way to protect your own belief from challenge.

It can also create false balance if every claim is treated as equally plausible despite uneven evidence.

## Ethical Boundary

Using confirmation bias in communication to selectively feed people only what confirms their identity or fear is manipulation. Ethical persuasion respects people's ability to encounter relevant contrary evidence.

The line is whether evidence is being curated for clarity or filtered to prevent revision.

## Practical Examples

- A team runs a pre-mortem before launch, asking what evidence would show the plan is wrong.
- A researcher writes down what would change their mind before collecting data.
- A leader invites the strongest dissenting view before public commitment makes reversal costly.

## Decision Prompt

What evidence would make me change my mind, and have I made it easy enough to find?
