# Herd Behavior

> People copying the actions of a larger group, often because the group seems to know something.

## Description

Herd behavior occurs when individuals follow the movement of others instead of relying on independent judgment. It can happen in markets, organizations, politics, fashion, hiring, social media, and everyday decisions.

It is not always irrational. Sometimes the group really has useful information. The danger is when everyone assumes someone else knows why the group is moving.

## Mechanism

Herd behavior spreads through visible action. Each person sees others choosing and treats that choice as evidence. As more people follow, the action becomes more persuasive, even if the original evidence was weak.

The loop is strongest under uncertainty, speed, fear of missing out, reputational pressure, and lack of private information.

## What It Helps You See

This lens helps you ask whether a trend reflects independent evidence or copied behavior. It also reveals how bubbles, panics, fads, and internal consensus can build without anyone intentionally misleading the group.

The key diagnostic is independence: are many people making separate judgments, or is each person using the previous person's action as proof?

## When to Use

Use this concept when popularity, momentum, or visible adoption is driving decisions faster than evidence.

It is especially useful in investing, hiring trends, product hype, organizational initiatives, and public opinion cascades.

## When It Backfires

The label backfires when it turns into automatic contrarianism. The crowd can be right, especially when many independent observers have real information.

Rejecting something only because it is popular is still letting the crowd control your judgment.

## Practical Use

- Ask what evidence started the trend.
- Look for independent sources rather than repeated copies of the same claim.
- Delay irreversible commitments during high emotional momentum.
- Compare the crowd's behavior against your own goals, constraints, and risk tolerance.

## Decision Prompt

Am I seeing independent evidence, or am I seeing people treat other people's behavior as evidence?
