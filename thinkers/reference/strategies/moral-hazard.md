# Moral Hazard

> A risk distortion where one party can take upside while someone else bears much of the downside.

## Description

Moral hazard appears when incentives separate decision-making from consequences. If a person or institution gets the benefits of risky behavior while another party absorbs the cost, risk-taking becomes too attractive.

This is a system design problem before it is a character problem. People respond to what the system rewards, tolerates, insures, or hides.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is insulation. The actor is protected from some negative consequences through insurance, bailouts, weak accountability, information gaps, limited liability, reputation shields, or organizational distance.

Once downside is shifted away, the actor may rationally take risks that are bad for the larger system.

## What It Helps You See

This lens reveals incentive misalignment. When behavior looks irresponsible, ask who gets the upside, who bears the downside, and who has enough information to notice the difference.

It also explains why good intentions are not enough when rewards point in the wrong direction.

## When to Use

Use moral hazard in incentive design, compensation, insurance, finance, management, governance, product metrics, and partnerships.

It is especially useful when one team creates work, risk, or damage that another team must clean up.

## When It Backfires

The concept backfires when used to blame people for needing protection. Insurance, safety nets, and forgiveness can be humane and socially valuable.

The problem is not protection itself. The problem is protection without aligned accountability.

## Practical Use

- Map upside, downside, decision rights, and accountability.
- Give decision-makers feedback about downstream consequences.
- Avoid metrics that reward short-term wins while hiding long-term costs.
- Create accountability that is proportionate, not punitive.

## Decision Prompt

Who benefits if this risk pays off, who pays if it fails, and are those people part of the decision?
