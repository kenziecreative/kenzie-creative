# Zero-Sum Games

> A situation where one person’s gain is exactly equal to another person’s loss.

## Description

A zero-sum game is a situation where one side's gain is exactly matched by another side's loss.

The total value is fixed. If I get more, you get less. Poker is the clean example: the money on the table is redistributed, not created. Many contests, elections, auctions, and allocation fights have zero-sum features.

The danger is not recognizing true zero-sum conditions. The bigger danger is falsely treating non-zero-sum situations as zero-sum. That mistake destroys trust, cooperation, and value that could have been created.

## Model Setup

- There is a fixed amount of value, status, territory, money, time, or winning positions.
- Players' interests are directly opposed over that fixed amount.
- A gain by one player necessarily reduces what remains for another.

Some situations are fully zero-sum. Many are partially zero-sum: a salary negotiation divides money, but the long-term employment relationship can still create value for both sides.

## Mechanism

Zero-sum framing changes behavior by making generosity look like weakness and cooperation look like self-harm.

When the pie is truly fixed, competition makes sense. When the pie can grow, zero-sum framing leaves value undiscovered. The key strategic question is whether the constraint is real or assumed.

## When to Use

Use zero-sum analysis when:

- A resource is genuinely fixed.
- Only one party can win a position, contract, election, bid, or allocation.
- The rules prevent value creation or side deals.
- You need to protect yourself from someone who is treating the interaction as extractive.

Even then, check whether the game is zero-sum only at one layer. A single contract award may be zero-sum; reputation in the industry is not.

## When It Backfires

The model backfires when it becomes a worldview.

Most human systems contain repeated interaction, reputation, relationships, shared interests, externalities, and future opportunities. If you treat those as irrelevant, you may win the immediate division and lose the larger game.

## Real-World Translation

Ask:

- Is the pie fixed, or can it grow?
- Is this one-shot, or will we interact again?
- Are there side issues we can trade on?
- Are we competing over one dimension while sharing interests on another?
- Who benefits from making this feel zero-sum?

## Decision Prompt

> What part of this situation is truly fixed, and what part could still create mutual value?

## Practical Examples

- Two companies bidding on a single government contract face a zero-sum award: one wins, the other loses.
- Two departments fighting over a fixed headcount allocation are in a zero-sum budget moment, though they may still share broader organizational goals.
- A courtroom verdict often has zero-sum features, but settlement design may reveal non-zero-sum options before the verdict.
