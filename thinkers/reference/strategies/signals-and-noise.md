# Signals and Noise

> Separating information that changes the decision from noise that merely feels salient.

## Description

Signals and noise is a lens for information quality. A signal is evidence that should change your understanding or decision. Noise is variation, chatter, emotion, randomness, or irrelevant detail that consumes attention without improving judgment.

The hard part is that noise often feels urgent and vivid, while signal can be slow, boring, or subtle.

## Mechanism

Human attention is drawn to novelty, intensity, recency, and social amplification. Those are not the same as relevance. A loud data point can be noise; a quiet trend can be signal.

Good analysis asks what information would change the decision, how reliable it is, and whether it is part of a stable pattern or a temporary fluctuation.

## When to Use

Use this lens whenever you are overwhelmed by data, feedback, market movement, conflict narratives, performance metrics, news, or competing interpretations.

It is especially useful when people are reacting to the latest event without asking whether it changes the underlying base case.

## When It Backfires

It backfires when you dismiss inconvenient evidence as noise because it threatens your preferred story. A weak signal can be the beginning of a real change.

It also backfires when filters are too aggressive. If your model only accepts familiar evidence, you will miss early signs of change.

## Practical Use

- Define what decision the information is supposed to improve.
- Separate trend from fluctuation, source from interpretation, and evidence from volume.
- Ask what would change your mind before looking at the data.
- Track repeated weak signals instead of overreacting to single dramatic ones.

## Decision Prompt

What information would actually change the decision, and am I giving attention to that or to what feels loud?
