# ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)

> The range of terms where both sides are better off agreeing than walking away.

## Description

ZOPA is the overlap between what one side can accept and what the other side can accept. If the overlap exists, a deal is possible. If it does not, agreement requires changing the terms, adding issues, improving alternatives, or accepting that no deal is the better outcome.

ZOPA is often described as a price range, but in real negotiation it can include timing, scope, risk, status, flexibility, support, future access, and non-monetary tradeoffs. The richer your map of what each side values, the more likely you are to find agreement that was invisible when everything looked like a single number.

## Mechanism

Each side has a reservation point: the point where it becomes better to walk away than accept the deal. Your BATNA helps define your reservation point because it tells you what you can do instead. The other side has its own reservation point, shaped by its alternatives, constraints, pressures, and goals.

The ZOPA is the overlap between those two limits. Anchors, deadlines, framing, and concessions can shape where inside that range the final deal lands, but they cannot create a real ZOPA by themselves. If there is no overlap, pressure may produce compliance, but it will not produce a durable agreement.

## When to Use

Use ZOPA before entering a negotiation to separate "what I want" from "what I can accept." It is especially useful when emotions, urgency, or social pressure make any agreement feel better than continued uncertainty.

Use it during negotiation when the conversation feels stuck. A deadlock over one issue may hide a workable agreement across several issues. Asking what each side can trade, relax, defer, or protect can reveal a broader zone of agreement.

## When It Backfires

ZOPA backfires when you treat your estimate as fact. You rarely know the other side's true constraints, and they may not know yours. False precision can cause you to reject viable deals or accept poor ones.

It also backfires when you reduce the negotiation to a single visible term. A salary negotiation, for example, may appear stuck on base pay while flexibility, title, learning budget, start date, equity, severance, or review timing still have room to move.

## Ethical Boundary

Healthy ZOPA work clarifies where agreement can benefit both sides. It becomes exploitative when you use private information, artificial urgency, or hidden constraints to push someone below a level they would reasonably accept if they understood the situation.

The ethical question is not "Can I get them to say yes?" It is "Would this agreement still be defensible if the important constraints were visible?"

## Practical Examples

- In a salary negotiation, the candidate wants $130,000, the company initially offers $118,000, and both could accept somewhere between $122,000 and $126,000 if remote flexibility and review timing are included.
- In a vendor contract, the buyer cannot increase the monthly fee, but can commit to a longer term. The vendor cares more about revenue predictability than a higher headline price, so a deal becomes possible.
- In a household disagreement, two people may be stuck between "travel" and "save money" until they discover a ZOPA around a shorter trip, a cheaper destination, or a delayed purchase.

## Decision Prompt

What terms would make agreement better than walking away for each side, and what evidence would show that no real overlap exists?
