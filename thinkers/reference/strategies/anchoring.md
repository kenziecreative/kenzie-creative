# Anchoring

> Setting or challenging an opening reference point that shapes how later offers, concessions, and judgments feel.

## Description

Anchoring is the strategic use of a first number, frame, standard, or comparison point. Once an anchor is placed, later options tend to be judged in relation to it. A high opening price can make a later discount feel generous. A low initial salary range can make a fair offer feel excessive. A narrow framing of the problem can make excluded alternatives feel unrealistic.

As a strategy, anchoring is not the same as the cognitive bias called the anchoring effect, though it relies on it. The bias describes the human tendency to over-weight the first reference point. The strategy is the deliberate act of setting, resisting, or replacing that reference point.

## Mechanism

Anchors work by creating a psychological starting line. People then adjust away from that starting line, but often not far enough. The anchor also signals what kind of conversation this is: ambitious, constrained, premium, urgent, adversarial, collaborative, or routine.

An anchor can be numeric, such as a price or deadline. It can also be conceptual: "This is a retention problem," "This is a safety issue," "This is a premium service," or "This is just a quick favor."

## When to Use

Use anchoring when the other side has little information, when there is a wide range of plausible outcomes, or when the first frame is likely to shape the rest of the discussion.

Use defensive anchoring when someone else has already set a reference point. You can slow the conversation down, ask what the number is based on, introduce market data, widen the frame, or restate the problem around a more relevant standard.

## When It Backfires

Anchoring backfires when the anchor is too extreme to be credible. An outrageous number can communicate bad faith, ignorance, or disrespect, especially in relationships where trust matters.

It also backfires when you let the anchor become the whole negotiation. If everyone argues only about the first number, richer tradeoffs such as scope, timing, risk, and future opportunity can disappear.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical anchoring gives people a starting point that you can defend. Manipulative anchoring hides the basis of the reference point or exploits someone's lack of information to pull them toward an unfair agreement.

The test is simple: if asked "Where did that number or frame come from?" you should be able to answer without inventing a story.

## Practical Examples

- A consultant proposes three pricing tiers and places the recommended option in the middle, making it easier for the client to judge value.
- A manager opens a planning discussion by saying, "We have six weeks and two people," preventing the group from designing a project that assumes resources they do not have.
- A buyer responds to a high initial quote by asking for the assumptions behind it, then introduces comparable market ranges before discussing concessions.

## Decision Prompt

What reference point will guide this conversation if I do not set, question, or replace it?
