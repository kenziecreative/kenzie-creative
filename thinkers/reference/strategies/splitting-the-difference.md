# Splitting the Difference

> A midpoint compromise that can resolve small gaps but can also reward arbitrary anchors.

## Description

Splitting the difference means meeting at the midpoint between two positions. It feels fair because both sides appear to move an equal distance. In low-stakes situations, that can be a perfectly reasonable way to finish.

The danger is that a midpoint is only as meaningful as the positions that created it. If one side starts from an inflated anchor, splitting the difference can reward the exaggeration. If the real issue is qualitative, relational, or multi-dimensional, the midpoint may avoid the hard question rather than solve it.

## Mechanism

The midpoint carries a strong fairness signal. It says, "We both gave something up." That signal can reduce conflict, preserve face, and create closure.

But the fairness is procedural, not necessarily substantive. Equal movement from unequal or arbitrary starting points does not guarantee a fair outcome. The midpoint can also narrow attention so people stop exploring better trades.

## When to Use

Use splitting the difference when the gap is small, the stakes are limited, both starting points are grounded, and the value of closure is higher than the value of further optimization.

It can also help when people are emotionally tired and need a simple, face-saving way to conclude a discussion that is already inside the ZOPA.

## When It Backfires

It backfires when used before you understand the bargaining range, the BATNAs, or the issues underneath the disagreement. A quick midpoint can leave value on the table, especially when logrolling could produce a better package.

It also backfires when one side uses an extreme anchor knowing the other side will eventually propose a compromise.

## Ethical Boundary

Splitting the difference is ethical when both sides understand what is being split and why the midpoint is acceptable. It becomes manipulative when one side creates an artificial position purely to pull the midpoint toward itself.

The ethical move is to ask whether the numbers or positions have a legitimate basis before treating the midpoint as fair.

## Practical Examples

- Two freelancers disagree over a small change fee and choose the midpoint because preserving the relationship matters more than the exact amount.
- A seller lists an item at an inflated price expecting the buyer to meet halfway. The midpoint feels like a concession but may still be above market value.
- Two colleagues disagree about a deadline and split the difference, only to discover later that neither date matched the real dependency.

## Decision Prompt

What are we splitting, and were the starting points meaningful enough for the midpoint to deserve trust?
