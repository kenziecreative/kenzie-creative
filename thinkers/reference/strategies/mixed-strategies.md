# Mixed Strategies

> Choosing randomly from several possible actions to keep your opponent from predicting your moves.

## Description

A mixed strategy is a deliberate use of probability to avoid being predictable.

Instead of always choosing the same action, a player randomizes across actions according to a chosen distribution. A soccer penalty kicker might shoot left some percentage of the time and right some percentage of the time so the goalie cannot exploit a pattern.

The point is not to be chaotic. The point is to prevent an adaptive opponent from predicting and exploiting you.

## Model Setup

- A pure strategy always chooses the same move in a given situation.
- A mixed strategy randomizes among moves.
- The opponent may know your probabilities but cannot know the specific move in advance.
- The value comes from making exploitation harder.

## Mechanism

Mixed strategies work because predictability has a cost.

If you always do the same thing, an opponent can adapt. Randomization keeps them from confidently countering your next move. In formal game theory, the right mix can make opponents indifferent among their responses because no response reliably exploits you.

## When to Use

Use mixed-strategy thinking when:

- You face a smart opponent who learns from patterns.
- No single move is always best.
- Being predictable makes you vulnerable.
- The situation is repeated enough for patterns to matter.

It appears in sports, security, negotiation timing, pricing, inspections, audits, and competitive tactics.

## When It Backfires

Mixed strategies backfire when randomness is used as a substitute for judgment.

Humans are bad at acting randomly. We create streaks, avoid streaks, over-correct, and leak patterns. Randomization also confuses allies if they need consistency from you.

It is also inappropriate in trust-based contexts where predictability is part of reliability. A partner, child, teammate, or employee should not have to guess which version of you will show up.

## Real-World Translation

Use randomization only where predictability creates exploitation risk.

- Randomize security checks.
- Vary negotiation timing if the other side exploits routine.
- Avoid a fixed play pattern in competition.
- Do not randomize commitments that others rely on for trust.

## Decision Prompt

> Is predictability helping people trust me, or helping an opponent exploit me?

## Practical Examples

- In Rock-Paper-Scissors, always playing rock is exploitable. Randomizing evenly is harder to exploit.
- A security team randomizes audit timing so bad actors cannot behave well only during predictable checks.
- A tennis player varies serve placement to prevent the opponent from cheating toward one side.
