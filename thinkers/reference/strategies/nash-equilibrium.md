# Nash Equilibrium

> A stable state in a game where no player can do better by unilaterally changing their strategy.

## Description

A Nash equilibrium is a stable state where no player can improve their outcome by changing only their own strategy, given what everyone else is doing.

It is stability, not goodness. A system can be locked into a bad equilibrium: everyone is acting rationally from their own position, and the combined result is still worse than what the group could achieve together.

The classic everyday version: everyone at a concert stands to see better. Once everyone is standing, no one sees better than before, but any one person who sits down sees worse. Standing is stable even though everyone sitting would be better.

## Mechanism

Nash equilibrium explains why "just do the better thing" often fails. A better collective outcome may require several people to change together. If changing alone makes you worse off, the bad pattern persists.

Look for three pieces:

1. **Players:** Who is making choices?
2. **Strategies:** What can each player do?
3. **Payoffs:** What does each player gain or lose depending on everyone else's choices?

The equilibrium is the place where unilateral change is unattractive. To change it, you usually have to change incentives, information, trust, enforcement, sequencing, or the available choices.

## When to Use

Use Nash equilibrium when a group or system seems stuck even though many people dislike the outcome:

- Teams that overwork because everyone fears being the first to set limits.
- Markets where companies copy bad practices because customers cannot coordinate around better ones.
- Political systems where candidates take extreme positions because moderation is punished by the current incentive structure.
- Social situations where everyone privately disagrees with a norm but no one wants to move first.

It helps you stop asking only, "Why won't people choose better?" and start asking, "What makes the current choice locally rational?"

## When It Backfires

The concept backfires when it makes bad systems feel inevitable.

An equilibrium is not destiny. It is a description of incentives under current conditions. If those conditions change, the equilibrium can change. Treating a harmful equilibrium as "just how things work" can become an excuse for passivity.

It also backfires when people assume every stable pattern is a Nash equilibrium. Some patterns persist because of coercion, misinformation, habit, law, culture, trauma, or lack of imagination. Incentives matter, but they are not the whole human story.

## Ethical Boundary

Nash analysis can become cynical if you only use it to trap others in predictable incentives. The healthier use is to understand why people are making the choices available to them and how to create better choices.

Be careful about designing systems where the "rational" move for each person creates harm for everyone. That is not strategic brilliance; it is institutionalized dysfunction.

## Practical Examples

- A team answers messages late at night. Everyone would prefer a norm of not doing that, but anyone who stops alone risks looking unresponsive. The equilibrium persists until the manager changes expectations or the group coordinates.
- Two competitors keep discounting. Each would prefer higher margins, but either one risks losing customers if it raises prices alone. A better outcome may require product differentiation, different customer segments, or a market change.
- In a meeting culture where no one challenges weak ideas, each person privately sees the flaw but fears social cost. Silence is individually safe and collectively costly.

## Decision Prompt

> What choice would be costly for one person to make alone, but beneficial if enough people changed together?
