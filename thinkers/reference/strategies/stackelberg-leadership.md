# Stackelberg Leadership

> A model of strategic leadership where one player (the leader) commits to a strategy first, and the other players (the followers) must then react.

## Description

Stackelberg leadership is a model where one player moves first and commits, forcing later players to respond to that commitment.

The first mover is the "leader" in the model, not necessarily because they are morally or organizationally superior, but because their move shapes the game. The later movers are "followers" because their best choices are made in response to the leader's committed action.

The model is useful for understanding capacity commitments, market entry, public roadmaps, pricing moves, infrastructure bets, and strategic announcements.

## Model Setup

- One player moves first.
- The first move is visible and credible.
- Later players choose after observing it.
- The first mover anticipates the followers' responses.
- Commitment changes the strategic landscape.

## Mechanism

Stackelberg leadership works through credible commitment.

A first move only matters if others believe it will hold. If the commitment is reversible, vague, or cheap talk, followers may ignore it. If it is credible, followers adapt around it.

## When to Use

Use this model when:

- Moving first changes others' options.
- Commitment can shape market expectations or competitor behavior.
- You can anticipate likely responses.
- You can survive being wrong.

It is not simply "go first." It is "commit first in a way that changes the game."

## When It Backfires

It backfires when the first move commits you to the wrong future.

The leader absorbs commitment risk. If the market, opponent, or environment behaves differently than expected, the visible commitment can become a trap. Moving first also teaches competitors where you are going.

## Real-World Translation

Ask:

- Is the commitment credible?
- What responses will it provoke?
- Can I adapt if the response is unexpected?
- Does moving first create advantage, or only reveal my plan?
- Is this a commitment or just an announcement?

## Decision Prompt

> What response am I trying to force by committing first, and what happens if they do not respond that way?

## Practical Examples

- A company builds large manufacturing capacity before competitors, signaling that it can serve demand at scale and discouraging smaller entrants.
- A platform announces and ships a new standard that partners must now integrate around.
- A political candidate stakes out a position early, forcing rivals to respond to that frame instead of setting their own.
