# The Stag Hunt

> A game that models the conflict between the safety of individual action and the rewards of social cooperation.

## Description

The Stag Hunt models the choice between a safer individual payoff and a larger cooperative payoff that requires trust.

Two hunters can cooperate to hunt a stag, which is valuable but only possible if both commit. Either hunter can instead hunt a hare alone, which gives a smaller but reliable payoff. The tension is not betrayal for advantage, as in the Prisoner's Dilemma. The tension is risk: "Will the other person really show up for the larger shared win?"

The Stag Hunt is a model of trust, assurance, and coordination.

## Model Setup

Classic structure:

- If both hunt stag, both get a large reward.
- If both hunt hare, both get a smaller but safe reward.
- If one hunts stag and the other hunts hare, the stag hunter gets nothing and the hare hunter gets the smaller reward.

There are two stable outcomes: mutual stag and mutual hare. The better outcome requires confidence that the other player will cooperate.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is assurance.

People may prefer the cooperative high-reward outcome, but only if they believe others will also choose it. When trust is low, the safer individual move becomes attractive even though everyone would prefer the higher cooperative payoff.

Unlike the Prisoner's Dilemma, the other player's cooperation does not tempt you to defect. It encourages you to cooperate. The problem is not temptation to exploit; it is fear of being left exposed.

## When to Use

Use the Stag Hunt model when:

- A better outcome requires multiple people to commit.
- Each person has a fallback option they can pursue alone.
- The fallback is worse collectively but safer individually.
- Trust, timing, or credible commitment is the main obstacle.

It appears in collaborations, coalitions, partnerships, team norms, collective action, and relationships where the larger win requires mutual vulnerability.

## When It Backfires

The model backfires when it romanticizes cooperation.

Sometimes the other party is not capable, aligned, or committed enough to hunt stag. Sometimes the fallback is not cowardice; it is correct risk management. A person choosing the hare may be seeing constraints the group is ignoring.

It also backfires when people moralize the safer choice: "If you really believed in the mission, you'd commit." Stag Hunt situations require assurance, not shame.

## Real-World Translation

The way toward the stag is credible assurance:

- Make commitments visible.
- Reduce the cost of being the first mover.
- Create staged cooperation rather than all-or-nothing exposure.
- Clarify who is doing what by when.
- Build trust through smaller successful hunts before the big one.

Trust is easier when defection is detectable and partial progress is possible.

## Decision Prompt

> What assurance would make the larger cooperative option safe enough to choose?

## Practical Examples

- Two researchers can collaborate on a difficult paper with greater impact, but each can also publish a smaller solo paper. The collaboration is worth more, but only if both invest seriously.
- A team wants to adopt a no-after-hours norm. Everyone benefits if everyone does it, but one person setting the boundary alone risks looking less committed.
- Two organizations could partner on a larger program, but each has a smaller safe program it can run alone. The partnership needs staged commitments, not just enthusiasm.
