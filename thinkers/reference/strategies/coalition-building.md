# Coalition Building

> Joining actors with partially overlapping interests so they can achieve what none could achieve alone.

## Description

Coalition building is the work of aligning people or groups who do not share everything, but do share enough to act together. A coalition can be temporary or durable, formal or informal, public or quiet.

The practical skill is finding the overlap without pretending differences do not exist. Coalitions are held together by shared interest, trust, coordination, and a believable path to impact.

## Mechanism

Coalitions create power by aggregating resources: numbers, legitimacy, expertise, access, money, relationships, attention, or veto power. They can also change the perceived norm. Once several stakeholders visibly support something, neutrality becomes harder to maintain.

The fragile part is alignment. Members may agree on the next step while disagreeing on long-term goals, priorities, credit, tactics, or acceptable compromise.

## When to Use

Use coalition building when a goal requires more influence, legitimacy, expertise, or coverage than one person or group has alone. It is useful in organizational change, politics, community action, product adoption, labor efforts, and cross-functional work.

Start by mapping stakeholders: who benefits, who blocks, who is undecided, who has credibility with whom, and what each party needs in order to participate.

## When It Backfires

Coalitions backfire when the shared goal is too vague, the costs are uneven, or members are asked to hide their real interests. They can also collapse after early success if nobody planned for what happens next.

They become dangerous when coalition maintenance matters more than the original purpose. At that point, leaders may dilute the mission, tolerate harmful allies, or punish internal dissent.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical coalitions are clear about goals, tradeoffs, representation, and who is affected. Unethical coalitions hide sponsors, manufacture consensus, exclude affected groups, or use numbers to overpower legitimate concerns.

The ethical question is whether the coalition expands responsible participation or merely concentrates pressure.

## Practical Examples

- Employees across several departments coordinate around a better internal process because each team suffers from the same bottleneck in different ways.
- Community groups with different priorities support a housing proposal because it includes affordability, transit access, and local hiring commitments.
- A product leader earns adoption by building a coalition of users, support teams, finance, and engineering rather than relying on executive mandate alone.

## Decision Prompt

Who has enough overlapping interest to act together, and what must stay explicit so the coalition does not become brittle or dishonest?
