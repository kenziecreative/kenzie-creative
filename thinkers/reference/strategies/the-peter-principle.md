# The Peter Principle

> A promotion failure where success in one role is mistaken for readiness for a different role.

## Description

The Peter Principle says people in hierarchies can be promoted until they reach a role that requires different abilities than the role where they succeeded. A great individual contributor may become a struggling manager. A strong operator may become a weak strategist.

The problem is not that successful people are incapable. The problem is that organizations often reward past performance without testing future fit.

## Mechanism

Promotions are signals. They signal status, reward, trust, and progression. If the organization has only one path upward, people may accept roles that do not match their strengths because refusing promotion looks like stagnation.

Once promoted, status and sunk cost make correction harder. The person may feel trapped, and the organization may avoid admitting the mismatch.

## What It Helps You See

This lens reveals role-skill mismatch. It asks whether the new job requires the same capabilities as the old job or merely borrows its prestige.

It also highlights systems that lack alternative growth paths, coaching, trial periods, or honest off-ramps.

## When to Use

Use this concept in promotion design, succession planning, hiring, career coaching, and performance diagnosis.

It is especially useful when someone excellent in one context begins failing after a status increase.

## When It Backfires

The label backfires when it becomes a personal insult. A person can be miscast without being generally incompetent.

It can also hide organizational responsibility. If the system promotes badly, trains poorly, or punishes lateral movement, the failure is not only individual.

## Practical Use

- Define the future role's required skills before selecting based on past performance.
- Create expert tracks that do not require management.
- Use trial assignments, coaching, and reversible promotions where possible.
- Treat role mismatch as design feedback, not shame.

## Decision Prompt

Are we promoting demonstrated fit for the next role, or rewarding success in the previous one?
