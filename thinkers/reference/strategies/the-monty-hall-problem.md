# The Monty Hall Problem

> A probability puzzle showing why new information must be interpreted through the process that produced it.

## Description

The Monty Hall Problem is useful because it punishes intuitive probability. After you make an initial choice among three doors, the host reveals a losing door and offers a switch. The correct move is to switch because the host's action is not random; it contains information.

The deeper lesson is broader than the puzzle. When new information arrives, you need to ask how it was generated. Evidence means different things depending on the process that produced it.

## Model Setup

There are three doors. One has the prize; two do not. You pick one. A host who knows where the prize is opens a losing door you did not pick. You may stay or switch.

Your first choice had a one-in-three chance of being right. The two doors you did not pick collectively had a two-in-three chance. When the host deliberately removes one losing door, that two-in-three probability concentrates on the remaining unopened door.

## Mechanism

The host's knowledge and rule matter. If the host always opens a losing door and always offers the chance to switch, the reveal is informative. If the host sometimes acts randomly, has different incentives, or only offers a switch in certain cases, the probabilities change.

This is the practical lesson: do not update only on the observed fact. Update on the observation process.

## What It Helps You See

Monty Hall trains conditional thinking. It helps you ask whether a new fact is independent, selected, filtered, strategic, biased, or produced by a known rule.

That matters in interviews, audits, research, diagnosis, hiring, dating, intelligence, and any decision where the evidence you see may not be a random sample.

## When It Backfires

It backfires when people memorize "always switch" without understanding the assumptions. If the information process changes, the conclusion changes.

It also backfires when applied too literally. Most real situations do not have clean doors and fixed host rules. The value is in the habit of asking how evidence was selected.

## Practical Use

- Ask who selected the information you are seeing.
- Ask what information was hidden, removed, or made impossible to observe.
- Ask whether the person revealing information has incentives that shape the reveal.
- Update probabilities only after understanding the selection process.

## Decision Prompt

What does this new information mean given the rule, incentive, or filter that produced it?
