# The Sunk Cost Fallacy

> A strategy-facing safeguard against letting unrecoverable past investment control the next decision.

## Description

This entry overlaps with the primary fallacy entry for [Sunk Cost Fallacy](../logical-fallacies/sunk-cost-fallacy.md). Keep this strategy version as an exit and adaptation tool: it focuses on how to stop throwing future resources after past losses.

The past investment may explain how you got here. It does not by itself justify staying.

## Mechanism

Sunk costs hurt because quitting can feel like admitting waste, failure, or foolishness. People continue projects, relationships, strategies, and purchases to preserve the meaning of what they already spent.

The fallacy appears when the unrecoverable past outweighs future costs, benefits, and alternatives.

## Strategic Use

Use this lens when deciding whether to continue, stop, pivot, or renegotiate. It is especially useful when the argument for continuing begins with "after all we've put into this."

The future-facing question is: if we were not already in this, would we choose it now?

## When It Backfires

It backfires when people use "ignore sunk costs" to avoid responsibility. Past investment can contain information: learning, obligations, relationships, and commitments may still matter.

The key is separating unrecoverable cost from future value.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical exit decisions account for commitments to people affected by the decision. Manipulative use of sunk cost keeps people trapped by shame, loyalty, or fear of waste.

The line is whether past investment is being used as context or as a cage.

## Practical Examples

- A company asks whether a failing product would receive funding today if it had no history.
- A person in a draining commitment separates "I promised" from "what responsible exit or repair looks like now."
- A team pivots after learning the original assumption was wrong, treating the past spend as tuition rather than proof.

## Decision Prompt

If this decision started today, with no need to defend the past, what would the best next move be?
