# Overconfidence Bias

> A decision risk where confidence outruns evidence, competence, or control.

## Description

Overconfidence bias appears when people overestimate what they know, how skilled they are, how much control they have, or how accurately they can predict outcomes.

Strategically, the danger is not confidence itself. Confidence helps people act. The danger is confidence that disables preparation, feedback, humility, and risk control.

## Mechanism

Overconfidence grows from success, status, selective memory, weak feedback, social reinforcement, and ego protection. People remember wins as skill and losses as circumstance.

It becomes especially risky when a person moves from a familiar domain into a new one while carrying the same confidence.

## Strategic Use

Use this lens before decisions involving uncertainty, leverage, delegation, prediction, safety, hiring, investing, negotiation, and leadership.

The goal is calibration: matching confidence to evidence and consequences.

## When It Backfires

The label backfires when it punishes justified confidence or becomes a way to keep capable people small.

It can also create analysis paralysis if every decision requires impossible certainty.

## Ethical Boundary

Leaders have an ethical duty to calibrate confidence because others often bear the cost of their certainty.

The line is crossed when confidence is used to silence warning, override evidence, or transfer risk to people without power.

## Practical Examples

- A leader asks for a pre-mortem before launching a plan they strongly believe in.
- An expert entering a new field treats their first assumptions as hypotheses, not conclusions.
- A team records confidence levels and checks calibration after outcomes are known.

## Decision Prompt

What evidence would justify this level of confidence, and who pays if I am wrong?
