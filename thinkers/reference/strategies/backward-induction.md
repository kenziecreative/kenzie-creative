# Backward Induction

> Reasoning backward in time, from the end of a game to the beginning, to determine the optimal sequence of actions.

## Description

Backward induction is reasoning from the end of a sequence back to the beginning.

Instead of asking, "What should I do first?", you ask, "What has to be true at the end?" Then you step backward: what must happen immediately before that, and before that, and before that? In formal game theory, this can identify optimal play in sequential games with known rules. In ordinary life, it helps expose dependencies, deadlines, leverage points, and impossible plans.

The practical value is that it prevents the first step from being chosen in isolation. You see whether the path you are starting can actually reach the ending you want.

## Mechanism

Backward induction works because later constraints determine earlier moves.

If the final decision depends on approval, evidence, money, trust, or timing, those conditions need to be created before the final moment arrives. Working backward reveals which earlier actions are not optional, even if they do not feel urgent yet.

The basic sequence:

1. Define the desired endpoint or final decision.
2. Identify the last required condition before that endpoint.
3. Step backward through each dependency.
4. Notice where the chain breaks.
5. Choose the first action that keeps the chain alive.

## When to Use

Use backward induction when order matters:

- Negotiations with predictable stages.
- Project plans with launch dates, approvals, or dependencies.
- Hiring, fundraising, sales, legal, or partnership processes.
- Strategic interactions where later moves depend on earlier commitments.
- Personal decisions where a desired future requires conditions that must be built now.

It is especially useful when people are over-focused on the next move and under-focused on the endgame.

## When It Backfires

Backward induction backfires when you mistake a map for certainty.

Formal backward induction assumes clear rules, known options, and rational players. Real life adds incomplete information, changing incentives, emotion, fatigue, misunderstanding, politics, and surprise. A chain that looks optimal on paper may break because another person does not behave the way the model assumes.

It can also make you too rigid. If you lock onto one end state too early, you may ignore better futures that appear along the way.

## Ethical Boundary

Backward induction can help you plan responsibly. It can also become a way to manipulate people by arranging earlier steps so their later "choice" is constrained.

Use it to reveal dependencies and avoid self-deception. Be careful when the plan depends on hiding the real endgame from people whose consent matters.

## Practical Examples

- A team wants a launch approved by October 1. Working backward shows legal review must finish by September 15, customer references by September 5, beta fixes by August 20, and beta users recruited by July 15. The first urgent task is not launch messaging; it is recruiting beta users.
- A founder wants to raise funding in six months. Working backward shows they need credible traction before investor meetings, which means instrumentation now, not a pitch deck first.
- In a negotiation, you ask what final agreement would be acceptable, what concession path could lead there, and what opening position preserves room without destroying trust.

## Decision Prompt

> If this ending has to be true, what must already be true one step before it?
