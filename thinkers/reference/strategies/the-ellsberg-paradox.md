# The Ellsberg Paradox

> A decision paradox showing that people often prefer known risks over ambiguous probabilities.

## Description

The Ellsberg Paradox reveals ambiguity aversion: people often prefer a risk with known odds over a situation where the odds are unclear, even when the ambiguous option may be equally good or better.

This matters because real decisions rarely come with clean probabilities. Careers, markets, relationships, strategy, innovation, and leadership all involve unknowns that cannot be reduced to a tidy percentage.

## Model Setup

In the classic setup, one option has a known probability and another has an unknown probability. Many people choose the known probability because it feels safer, even when the unknown option might have a better expected outcome.

The important lesson is not the specific puzzle. The lesson is that ambiguity itself carries emotional weight.

## Mechanism

Known risk feels controllable because it can be named. Ambiguous risk feels threatening because the mind cannot easily bound it. That discomfort can push people toward measurable mediocrity over uncertain upside.

Organizations often do the same thing. They choose the forecastable project, not because it is best, but because it is easier to defend.

## What It Helps You See

This lens helps separate "this is bad" from "this is hard to estimate." It also exposes a common bias in planning: overvaluing what can be quantified and undervaluing what is important but still unclear.

It is especially useful when a group treats uncertainty as evidence against an option rather than as a property to investigate.

## When It Backfires

The opposite mistake is romanticizing ambiguity. Unknown odds are not automatically attractive. Sometimes the measurable, ordinary path is the right one.

The goal is not to seek ambiguity. The goal is to price it honestly, reduce it where possible, and avoid rejecting options merely because they are harder to model.

## Practical Use

- Name whether you are facing risk, ambiguity, or ignorance.
- Ask what information would reduce the ambiguity enough to decide.
- Compare the ambiguous option against the opportunity cost of staying with the known option.
- Look for small tests that turn ambiguity into evidence.

## Decision Prompt

Am I rejecting this option because it is worse, or because its uncertainty is harder to quantify?
