# The Golden Rule

> Treat others with the consideration you would reasonably want if you were in their position.

## Description

The Golden Rule is a basic ethical lens for checking whether your behavior gives another person the dignity, honesty, and consideration you would want for yourself. It turns ethics from an abstract theory into a perspective shift: before acting, imagine being on the receiving end.

Its strength is simplicity. Its weakness is that people do not all want the same treatment. The rule works best when paired with curiosity about the other person's actual preferences, constraints, and context.

## Core Test

Would I accept being treated this way if I were in the other person's position, with their vulnerability and limited information?

This is stronger than asking, "Would I personally like this?" The point is not to project your preferences onto them. The point is to interrupt self-serving exceptions.

## What It Helps You See

The Golden Rule exposes one-sided reasoning. It catches moves like withholding information you would want disclosed, using pressure you would resent, speaking with contempt you would not tolerate, or demanding patience you would not offer.

It is especially useful when power is uneven because the more powerful person can easily forget what the interaction feels like from below.

## When to Use

Use it before giving feedback, negotiating, ending a relationship, enforcing a rule, selling, leading, or making a decision that affects someone who has less power than you.

It is also useful after conflict. Ask whether the other person's reaction would make more sense if you imagined receiving your own tone, timing, or framing.

## When It Backfires

It backfires when it becomes projection. You may value bluntness while someone else needs privacy and time. You may enjoy public praise while someone else experiences it as exposure.

It also backfires if it becomes a substitute for boundaries. Treating others well does not require accepting mistreatment.

## Practical Use

- Before a hard conversation, ask what information, tone, and timing you would want if the roles were reversed.
- Before using leverage, ask whether the pressure would still feel legitimate if used on you.
- Before judging someone's response, ask what they knew, feared, or lacked when they made the choice.

## Decision Prompt

If I were affected by this decision but did not control it, what treatment would I find fair, honest, and humane?
