# Contextualizing

> Shaping the surrounding conditions that determine how an interaction is interpreted.

## Description

Contextualizing means making the setting, purpose, constraints, roles, stakes, and norms of an interaction explicit. Context shapes what people think is happening before content even arrives.

The same message can land differently depending on timing, room, agenda, relationship, audience, medium, power difference, and stated purpose.

## Mechanism

Context tells people how to read behavior. Is this a brainstorm or a decision? Feedback or punishment? Negotiation or announcement? Exploration or evaluation? Private support or public accountability?

When context is unclear, people fill in the blanks from fear, habit, status, or prior experience. When context is shaped well, people know how to participate.

## When to Use

Use contextualizing before difficult conversations, negotiations, decisions, feedback, facilitation, conflict repair, and strategic communication.

It is especially useful when people keep reacting to the wrong conversation: defending when you are exploring, brainstorming when you are deciding, or treating a boundary as a debate.

## When It Backfires

It backfires when context-setting becomes stage management. If you control the room, agenda, timing, and definitions only to disadvantage others, people will feel manipulated.

It also backfires when context is over-explained. Too much setup can feel like a speech before a conversation.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical contextualizing helps people understand the interaction and participate well. Manipulative contextualizing hides constraints, rigs the options, or makes one outcome feel pre-decided while pretending openness.

The ethical question is whether the context gives people clearer agency or quietly reduces it.

## Practical Examples

- A manager starts feedback by saying, "This is not a performance warning; this is coaching on one pattern I want us to improve."
- A facilitator distinguishes idea generation from decision-making so people do not critique too early.
- Before negotiation, a team agrees on agenda, decision rights, and what information must be shared.

## Decision Prompt

What context are people using to interpret this interaction, and does that context help them participate honestly?
