# Tit-for-Tat

> Start with cooperation, respond proportionally to defection, and return to cooperation when the other side does.

## Description

Tit-for-Tat is a strategy for repeated interaction. It begins cooperatively, then mirrors the other party's previous behavior: cooperate when they cooperate, defend when they defect, and resume cooperation when they repair.

Its power comes from being firm without being permanently hostile. It teaches the other side that cooperation will be met with cooperation, while exploitation will not be rewarded. In human relationships, the "tat" should be proportional and clear, not punitive or theatrical.

## Mechanism

Tit-for-Tat changes the incentive structure of a repeated game. If the other party defects, they get an immediate gain but lose future cooperation. If they return to cooperation, the relationship can recover.

The strategy works best when actions are visible, rounds repeat, and both sides can understand the connection between behavior and response. It combines three signals: goodwill, self-protection, and willingness to repair.

## When to Use

Use Tit-for-Tat when you expect continued interaction and want a default that is neither naive nor needlessly aggressive. It is useful with colleagues, vendors, partners, collaborators, and even competitors who can learn from repeated consequences.

It is especially helpful when you need to protect trust without becoming a doormat. You begin open, but you do not keep offering cooperation into repeated exploitation.

## When It Backfires

Tit-for-Tat backfires in noisy environments where people misread each other's actions. A missed message, delayed payment, or ambiguous decision can look like defection and trigger unnecessary retaliation.

It also fails against people who do not care about future interaction, cannot perceive the connection between cause and consequence, or escalate every boundary into a status fight.

## Ethical Boundary

The ethical version is proportional, transparent, and repairable. It says, "I am changing my behavior because this happened, and cooperation can resume when the pattern changes."

It becomes unhealthy when it turns into scorekeeping, revenge, humiliation, or emotional punishment. The goal is to stabilize cooperation, not to win a grievance ledger.

## Practical Examples

- A freelancer delivers the first phase on time. When the client pays late, the freelancer pauses the next phase until payment clears, then resumes normally.
- A teammate repeatedly ignores shared process. You stop absorbing the cost quietly and require written commitments before depending on their work again.
- A vendor is responsive and flexible, so you offer them early visibility into future needs. If they miss agreed service levels, you reduce commitment until reliability returns.

## Decision Prompt

How can I respond in a way that protects cooperation, makes the consequence clear, and leaves room for repair?
