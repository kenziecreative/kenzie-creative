# Non-Zero-Sum Games

> A situation where the interacting parties’ aggregate gains and losses can be more or less than zero.

## Description

A non-zero-sum game is a situation where the total outcome can grow or shrink depending on how the players interact.

The pie is not fixed. Cooperation can create additional value; conflict, mistrust, or bad incentives can destroy value. A good partnership, product ecosystem, team, market, or community is usually non-zero-sum: the participants can do better together than they could by merely dividing what already exists.

Non-zero-sum does not mean everyone wins automatically. It means the structure allows mutual gain or mutual loss.

## Model Setup

- Players' choices affect the total amount of value.
- Cooperation, coordination, specialization, or trade can increase the total.
- Defection, mistrust, extraction, or poor coordination can reduce the total.
- Distribution still matters: value can be created and then fought over.

## Mechanism

Non-zero-sum games work through complementarity. One person's action can make another person's action more valuable. Trust, information-sharing, specialization, repeated interaction, and good governance can all increase the size of the shared outcome.

The hard part is that value creation and value capture are different. People may agree that cooperation creates more value and still fight over who gets how much of it.

## When to Use

Use non-zero-sum analysis when:

- There may be trades across priorities.
- Parties have different strengths, costs, risks, or preferences.
- Long-term relationship matters.
- Cooperation could increase the total value available.
- The current frame feels unnecessarily adversarial.

It is especially useful in negotiation: before arguing over division, ask whether the deal can be redesigned.

## When It Backfires

The model backfires when it turns into naive cooperation.

Some people will accept your cooperative posture while continuing to play extractively. Some systems create gains but distribute them unfairly. Some "win-win" language hides who is absorbing the cost.

Non-zero-sum thinking should widen the search for value. It should not blind you to power, exploitation, or unequal risk.

## Real-World Translation

Ask:

- What could make the total outcome larger?
- What does each party value differently?
- Where can we trade low-cost concessions for high-value gains?
- What trust or enforcement would make cooperation safe?
- How will created value be divided fairly?

## Decision Prompt

> What option would make the total pie bigger before we argue about slicing it?

## Practical Examples

- Two companies form a joint venture because each has a capability the other lacks. The combined product can create value neither could produce alone.
- A manager and employee negotiate schedule flexibility in exchange for clearer output commitments. Both sides can be better off if the design fits their real constraints.
- Two departments share data instead of hoarding it, creating better decisions for both.
