# Information Asymmetry

> A power difference created when one party has relevant information the other party lacks.

## Description

Information asymmetry appears when people are making a shared decision, exchange, or agreement with unequal knowledge. One side may know more about quality, risk, intent, constraints, alternatives, costs, or future consequences.

The asymmetry can be benign, unavoidable, strategic, or exploitative. The ethical question is what the better-informed party does with the advantage.

## Mechanism

Information changes bargaining power and judgment. The less-informed party may overpay, under-protect itself, trust the wrong signal, miss hidden risk, or agree to terms it would reject with fuller knowledge.

Markets and relationships respond with signals, screens, warranties, audits, disclosure rules, references, trials, and trusted intermediaries.

## What It Helps You See

This lens helps you ask who knows what, who benefits from the gap, and how the gap can be reduced or governed.

It also clarifies why trust is valuable: trust lowers the cost of information inequality because people believe the better-informed party will not exploit them.

## When to Use

Use this concept in negotiation, hiring, sales, investing, medicine, leadership, contracting, product decisions, and any situation where one side controls the facts.

It is especially useful when someone says, "Just trust me," but has incentives not to reveal the full picture.

## When It Backfires

It backfires when every information gap is treated as bad faith. Expertise often creates asymmetry, and not all knowledge can be fully transferred.

The goal is not perfect symmetry. The goal is enough disclosure, verification, and accountability for informed agency.

## Practical Use

- Ask what relevant facts each side has and lacks.
- Use screening, references, warranties, audits, or pilots to reduce uncertainty.
- Disclose material facts when another person's decision depends on them.
- Watch for false signals that simulate trustworthiness.

## Decision Prompt

What does one party know that the other party would reasonably need in order to choose freely?
