# The Laws of Human Nature

> A source lens for reading recurring motives, defenses, and social patterns in yourself and others.

## Description

The Laws of Human Nature is best treated as a source lens, not a scientific taxonomy. Its practical value is in prompting attention to motives, envy, status, insecurity, irrationality, projection, and self-deception.

In this corpus, it should help readers ask better questions about behavior rather than reduce people to fixed types.

## Mechanism

The lens works by making hidden motives discussable. People often explain behavior through stated reasons, while the real drivers may include identity protection, fear, status, attachment, resentment, or unmet need.

The risk is overinterpretation. A pattern lens can become a story machine if it outruns evidence.

## Strategic Use

Use this lens for self-observation, leadership, conflict interpretation, and social pattern recognition. Start with yourself before diagnosing others.

It is useful when stated reasons do not explain the emotional intensity or repeated pattern in a situation.

## When It Backfires

It backfires when it produces cynicism, mind-reading, or deterministic labels. People are patterned, but they are not reducible to a single motive.

It also backfires when "human nature" is used to excuse harm or manipulation.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical use increases humility and compassion. Manipulative use turns psychological insight into leverage or contempt.

The ethical question is whether the lens helps you respond more wisely or merely feel superior.

## Practical Examples

- A manager notices that a conflict may involve status threat, then asks better questions instead of assuming bad character.
- A person recognizes their own envy before turning it into criticism.
- A team discusses incentives and fears rather than treating recurring behavior as random.

## Decision Prompt

What motive, fear, or identity need might be operating here, and what evidence would keep that interpretation honest?
