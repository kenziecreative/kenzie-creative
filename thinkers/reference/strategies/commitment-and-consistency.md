# Commitment and Consistency

> The pressure to behave in ways that match what we have already said, chosen, or become identified with.

## Description

Commitment and consistency describes the pull to align future behavior with past commitments. Once people state a position, take an action, sign up, identify publicly, or make a small concession, inconsistency can feel uncomfortable.

This can support integrity and follow-through. It can also trap people in outdated positions because changing course feels like losing identity, face, or moral standing.

## Mechanism

Commitments create psychological and social anchors. Internally, people want to see themselves as coherent. Externally, they want others to see them as reliable.

Small commitments can grow because each later step feels like a continuation rather than a new choice. That is why this mechanism is powerful in habit formation, persuasion, escalation, and manipulation.

## When to Use

Use it ethically to support values people already endorse: public goals, implementation plans, mutual agreements, habit design, and clear commitments with informed consent.

Use it defensively when someone asks for a small agreement that seems designed mainly to make a later, larger request harder to refuse.

## When It Backfires

It backfires when consistency outranks truth. People may keep defending a bad decision, sunk cost, belief, or identity because changing would feel humiliating.

It also backfires when commitments are extracted under pressure, confusion, flattery, or incomplete information.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical commitment helps people act on chosen values. Manipulative commitment engineers small yeses to corner people into larger compliance.

The ethical question is whether the person understands what they are committing to and remains free to revise with new information.

## Practical Examples

- A person sets a public training goal and asks friends for accountability because they genuinely want support.
- A salesperson asks identity-based questions to make refusal feel inconsistent; the buyer notices and returns to the actual offer.
- A team revisits a past decision after new evidence rather than defending it only because they already announced it.

## Decision Prompt

Am I honoring a real commitment, or protecting consistency after the reasons for the commitment have changed?
