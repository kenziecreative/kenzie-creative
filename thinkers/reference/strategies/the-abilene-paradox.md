# The Abilene Paradox

> A group choosing something no individual actually wants because everyone misreads everyone else's preferences.

## Description

The Abilene Paradox is a coordination failure of honesty. People privately disagree with a course of action but go along because they assume others want it, do not want to create conflict, or fear being seen as difficult.

Unlike ordinary disagreement, the problem is not conflicting preferences. The problem is that real preferences are hidden.

## Mechanism

Each person observes public agreement and mistakes it for private support. Their own silence then becomes evidence for everyone else. The group manufactures consensus out of mutual misreading.

The paradox is most likely when politeness, hierarchy, speed, fatigue, or conflict avoidance makes direct preference-sharing feel risky.

## What It Helps You See

This lens helps identify false agreement. A group may not be committed to a plan at all; it may only be committed to not being the first person to object.

It is a useful companion to groupthink, but the emotional tone is different. Groupthink often feels like certainty. Abilene often feels like reluctant compliance.

## When to Use

Use this concept when a team agrees too easily, people seem low-energy after a decision, or support is public but enthusiasm is absent.

It is especially useful in meetings, family decisions, strategy commitments, project continuation, and any culture where dissent feels impolite.

## When It Backfires

The label backfires if it assumes people do not mean what they say. Sometimes quiet agreement is genuine.

The response should be an invitation to clarify, not an accusation that the group is lying.

## Practical Use

- Ask for private preference checks before public commitment.
- Invite "What do you not want us to do?" as a normal question.
- Separate politeness from agreement.
- Make it easy to say, "I can support this, but it is not my preference."

## Decision Prompt

What does each person actually want, and have we made it safe enough for them to say it?
