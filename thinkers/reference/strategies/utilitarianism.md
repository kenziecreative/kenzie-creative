# Utilitarianism

> An ethical lens that judges choices by their consequences for overall well-being and suffering.

## Description

Utilitarianism asks which action is likely to produce the best overall consequences. It is a consequentialist frame: the morality of a choice depends heavily on what it causes, not only on the intention behind it or the rule it follows.

This lens is powerful when decisions affect many people and tradeoffs cannot be avoided. It is also dangerous when it flattens human dignity into arithmetic or lets majorities sacrifice minorities too easily.

## Core Test

Which available option is likely to create the greatest net benefit and least net harm for everyone affected?

The test requires identifying who is affected, what counts as benefit or harm, how likely each consequence is, and how long the effects last.

## What It Helps You See

Utilitarianism makes hidden consequences visible. It pushes against decisions that feel morally pure but create avoidable suffering, and against symbolic choices that satisfy a small group while imposing large costs elsewhere.

It also forces attention to opportunity cost. Resources spent in one place cannot be spent somewhere else.

## When to Use

Use it in policy, resource allocation, public health, product safety, prioritization, crisis response, and leadership decisions where multiple people's welfare is at stake.

It is especially useful when every option harms someone and the real question is how to reduce the total harm.

## When It Backfires

It backfires when the people bearing the costs are treated as abstractions. "Greater good" language can hide coercion, exclusion, or sacrifice of people with less power.

It also backfires when consequences are too uncertain to calculate honestly, or when measurable outcomes crowd out dignity, rights, trust, and long-term legitimacy.

## Practical Use

- List affected groups, including people not in the room.
- Separate certain harms from speculative benefits.
- Ask who receives the benefit, who bears the cost, and whether the distribution is defensible.
- Pair the utilitarian answer with a rights or dignity check before acting.

## Decision Prompt

Which choice reduces total harm without quietly making the least powerful people pay for everyone else's benefit?
