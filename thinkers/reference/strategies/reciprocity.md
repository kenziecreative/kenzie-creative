# Reciprocity

> The social pull to respond in kind to help, concessions, respect, harm, or trust.

## Description

Reciprocity is the norm that what one person gives, another person should answer in some form. Help invites help. Trust invites trust. A concession invites a concession. Disrespect can invite defensiveness or retaliation.

Reciprocity is not just a transaction. It is one of the ways people read intention. A small generous action can say, "I am not only extracting value here." A fair concession can make continued cooperation feel safer.

## Mechanism

Reciprocity works through obligation, gratitude, fairness, and identity. People often want to see themselves as decent and responsive. When someone gives first, it can create an internal pressure to restore balance.

The same mechanism can support cooperation or manipulation. The difference lies in consent, proportionality, and whether the initial "gift" was genuinely helpful or merely a hook.

## When to Use

Use reciprocity to build trust by offering real value without immediately demanding repayment. In negotiation, make concessions visible and conditional enough that the other side understands cooperation is mutual.

Use it in relationships by noticing what you are inviting. Respect tends to invite respect. Calm tends to invite calm. Suspicion, contempt, or scorekeeping often invite the same.

## When It Backfires

Reciprocity backfires when it becomes a ledger. If every action is tracked as debt, generosity stops feeling generous and starts feeling like control.

It also backfires when people feel trapped by unwanted favors. A gift they did not ask for should not create an obligation they did not consent to.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical reciprocity leaves the other person free. It offers value, models cooperation, and makes fair exchange possible. Manipulative reciprocity gives in order to create pressure, guilt, or dependency.

The ethical test is whether the other person could decline repayment without punishment.

## Practical Examples

- A colleague shares useful context before asking for help, making the request feel cooperative rather than extractive.
- A negotiator says, "I can move on delivery timing if you can move on payment terms," making reciprocity explicit.
- A salesperson offers an unwanted favor and then pressures the buyer to respond; that is obligation engineering, not generosity.

## Decision Prompt

Am I creating mutual goodwill, or am I creating a debt the other person did not freely accept?
