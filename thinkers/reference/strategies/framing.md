# Framing

> Defining how an issue should be understood so people notice some options, stakes, and tradeoffs over others.

## Description

Framing is the act of shaping the meaning of a situation. The facts may stay the same, but the frame influences what people think is relevant, what they feel, what options seem available, and what kind of response feels appropriate.

"This is a cost" and "This is an investment" can point to the same spending decision. "This is a performance issue" and "This is a role-design issue" can point to the same workplace problem. The frame does not merely decorate the conversation; it organizes attention.

## Mechanism

Frames work by selecting a context. They tell people what category the situation belongs to, what standards should be used, who is responsible, what risks matter, and what outcomes count as success.

Framing can be verbal, visual, numerical, emotional, or procedural. A chart, a title, a meeting agenda, a comparison set, or the order in which options are presented can all frame the decision.

## When to Use

Use framing when a conversation is confused, emotionally reactive, or stuck in an unhelpful category. A better frame can turn blame into diagnosis, scarcity into prioritization, or conflict into tradeoff design.

Use it before persuasion, negotiation, facilitation, or decision-making. Ask what the current frame causes people to notice and what it causes them to ignore.

## When It Backfires

Framing backfires when the audience feels managed rather than oriented. If the frame hides important information or makes an unfair conclusion feel inevitable, people may experience it as spin.

It also backfires when the frame is too narrow. A leader who frames every disagreement as resistance may miss genuine risk signals. A negotiator who frames everything as a battle may destroy opportunities for mutual gain.

## Ethical Boundary

Ethical framing clarifies reality by making useful distinctions visible. Manipulative framing distorts reality by hiding relevant facts, loading the language, or forcing people into a false choice.

The ethical question is: does this frame help people think better, or does it make thinking less necessary?

## Practical Examples

- A team reframes "Who caused the outage?" as "What made this failure possible?" and gets better system learning.
- A nonprofit frames a donation as "funding one week of legal support" rather than an abstract dollar amount, making the impact easier to understand.
- A salesperson frames a product as "risk reduction" instead of "software," changing which buyer priorities become salient.

## Decision Prompt

What does this frame make visible, what does it hide, and who benefits from that selection?
