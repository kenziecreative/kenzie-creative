# Groupthink

> A group decision failure where harmony, loyalty, or consensus pressure suppresses dissent and critical thought.

## Description

Groupthink occurs when a group protects cohesion at the expense of reality. People mute doubts, avoid conflict, defer to the dominant view, or convince themselves that agreement means accuracy.

It is especially dangerous because it often feels good from inside. The group may experience confidence, unity, speed, and shared identity while its judgment is getting worse.

## Mechanism

Groupthink grows when social cost attaches to disagreement. Authority pressure, time pressure, shared identity, prior commitment, isolation from outside feedback, and fear of being disloyal all make dissent harder.

Once dissent disappears, the group loses error correction. Bad assumptions circulate unchallenged and become stronger through repetition.

## What It Helps You See

This lens helps distinguish real alignment from silence. A team may appear unified because people agree, or because disagreement has become unsafe.

It also reveals why intelligence does not prevent bad group decisions. Smart people can still conform when the group rewards belonging over accuracy.

## When to Use

Use this concept when a group is moving quickly toward consensus, dismissing outsiders, punishing doubt, or treating challenge as betrayal.

It is especially useful before irreversible decisions, launches, investments, public commitments, and crisis responses.

## When It Backfires

The label backfires when used to dismiss genuine agreement. Sometimes people align because the evidence is strong.

It can also be weaponized by someone who dislikes the decision and calls the group irrational instead of engaging the reasons.

## Practical Use

- Ask each person to name the strongest concern before final agreement.
- Separate idea generation from decision-making.
- Assign a dissent role or pre-mortem.
- Invite outside review before commitment becomes public identity.

## Decision Prompt

Are we aligned because the evidence survived challenge, or because challenge became socially expensive?
