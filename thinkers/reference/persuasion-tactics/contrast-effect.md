# Contrast effect

> Changing how an option feels by placing it next to a more extreme, inferior, or emotionally loaded comparison.

## Description

The contrast effect persuades through comparison. An option can feel cheap after an expensive one, reasonable after an outrageous one, moderate after an extreme one, or safe after a frightening one.

The option did not change. The comparison changed.

## Mechanism

Human judgment is relative. People evaluate size, price, risk, quality, fairness, and attractiveness against nearby reference points. Sequence and pairing can therefore alter perception.

Contrast can clarify real differences or manufacture a misleading sense of value.

## Where It Shows Up

- Pricing tiers and product packages.
- Negotiation offers and counteroffers.
- Political comparisons between candidates or policies.
- Performance reviews where one person is evaluated after someone unusually strong or weak.
- Media sequencing that changes emotional intensity.

## When It Persuades Legitimately

It is legitimate when the comparison is relevant and helps people see a real tradeoff. Comparisons are often necessary for judgment.

Good contrast answers: compared to what, and why is that comparison fair?

## When It Becomes Manipulative

It becomes manipulative when the comparison is chosen mainly to distort perception. A bad anchor, inflated reference price, irrelevant extreme, or fake option can make a mediocre offer feel attractive.

The risk is judging the option against the comparison instead of against your actual needs.

## Recognition Cues

- The first option feels like setup for the second.
- The comparison is vivid but not relevant.
- You feel relief at the second option rather than attraction to its merits.
- Objective criteria disappear after the comparison is introduced.

## How to Respond

Evaluate each option independently. Bring in outside standards: market rates, goals, constraints, alternatives, and your BATNA.

Ask how the option would look if presented alone.

## Decision Prompt

How would I evaluate this option without the comparison that was just placed beside it?
