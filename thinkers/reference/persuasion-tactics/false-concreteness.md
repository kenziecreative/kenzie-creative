# False concreteness

> Making an abstract, uncertain, or speculative claim feel concrete without providing real grounding.

## Description

False concreteness persuades by giving vague ideas the feel of facts. A forecast, assumption, impression, or theory is presented with enough detail, jargon, numbers, or confidence that it seems more solid than it is.

The audience feels clarity, but the claim has not earned it.

## Mechanism

People prefer concrete claims because they are easier to imagine, remember, and act on. False concreteness exploits that preference by adding surface specificity without valid measurement or evidence.

A number can anchor belief even when the number is guessed. Technical language can simulate rigor even when the underlying claim is vague.

## Where It Shows Up

- Forecasts presented as guarantees.
- Vague claims dressed in metrics.
- Hypothetical scenarios described as inevitable.
- Marketing promises with precise-sounding but unverified numbers.
- Workplace plans with confident projections but weak assumptions.

## When It Persuades Legitimately

Concrete language is legitimate when it clarifies real evidence, definitions, and measurement. Specificity is useful when it is traceable.

The honest version says what was measured, how, under what assumptions, and with what uncertainty.

## When It Becomes Manipulative

It becomes manipulative when concreteness is used to hide uncertainty. A claim should not sound more measurable, causal, or inevitable than the evidence allows.

The tactic is especially risky when people must commit resources based on the artificial clarity.

## Recognition Cues

- Precise numbers appear without sources or methods.
- Jargon creates confidence but not understanding.
- A hypothetical outcome is described as already decided.
- The explanation feels clearer than the evidence should allow.

## How to Respond

Ask what was measured, what assumptions are built in, what uncertainty remains, and what evidence would falsify the claim.

Translate jargon into plain language and see whether the claim still holds.

## Decision Prompt

What makes this claim concrete besides confident language, detail, or a number?
