# Card stacking

> Presenting true but selective information so the audience sees only the side that supports your conclusion.

## Description

Card stacking persuades by omission. The facts presented may be accurate, but the selection is biased: favorable evidence is highlighted, inconvenient evidence is hidden, and the audience receives a tilted picture.

It is harder to detect than a lie because the speaker can defend each individual claim as true.

## Mechanism

People often judge the information in front of them rather than the information that is missing. Card stacking exploits that by controlling the evidence set.

It pairs especially well with confirmation bias: people are less likely to notice missing facts when the presented facts support what they already want to believe.

## Where It Shows Up

- Political ads that list only favorable statistics.
- Product pages that show selected reviews.
- Project updates that mention wins but not blockers.
- Debates that cite anecdotes while omitting base rates.
- Historical or media narratives that exclude inconvenient context.

## When It Persuades Legitimately

Selection is not always manipulation. Every presentation must choose what to include. It is legitimate when the selection is representative, the limits are stated, and relevant counterevidence is not hidden.

Good curation helps people understand. Card stacking helps them misunderstand while feeling informed.

## When It Becomes Manipulative

It becomes manipulative when the omitted information would materially change the audience's judgment.

The more consequential the decision, the more responsibility the speaker has to include risks, limitations, and opposing evidence.

## Recognition Cues

- Only positives, only negatives, or only convenient examples appear.
- The speaker resists discussing tradeoffs.
- Numbers are shown without denominators, timeframes, or comparison groups.
- The argument feels complete but strangely effortless.

## How to Respond

Ask what evidence cuts the other way, what base rate applies, what a critic would say, and what was excluded from the selection.

Seek sources that are not rewarded for reaching the same conclusion.

## Decision Prompt

What relevant fact, risk, example, or counterargument would change this picture if it were included?
