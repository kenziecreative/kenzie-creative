# Glittering generalities

> Vague, positive, emotionally appealing language that invites approval without concrete support.

## Description

Glittering generalities use admired words such as freedom, innovation, excellence, family, courage, progress, fairness, or community without specifying what they mean in practice.

The language sounds good enough that people may approve before they understand the claim.

## Mechanism

The tactic works through positive association and ambiguity. People fill vague words with their own preferred meanings, so many different listeners can feel aligned with the same statement.

Because the terms are desirable, challenging them can make the challenger look opposed to the value itself.

## Where It Shows Up

- Campaign slogans and mission statements.
- Workplace change language.
- Brand messaging.
- Speeches that use values without tradeoffs.
- Debates where a policy is labeled virtuous instead of argued for.

## When It Persuades Legitimately

It is legitimate when broad values are followed by concrete commitments. Shared ideals matter, but they should guide specifics rather than replace them.

Good use moves from value to definition to action.

## When It Becomes Manipulative

It becomes manipulative when the pleasant word is doing all the work. If "excellence" means layoffs, "freedom" means deregulation, or "community" means conformity, the word is hiding the tradeoff.

The tactic is especially slippery because it may contain no literal falsehood.

## Recognition Cues

- Everyone can agree with the word, but no one knows the operational meaning.
- Asking for specifics is treated as cynicism.
- The value label appears before evidence.
- The same phrase could justify opposite actions.

## How to Respond

Ask for definitions, examples, metrics, and tradeoffs. Translate the abstraction into concrete commitments.

Do not argue against the value; ask what it requires here.

## Decision Prompt

What exactly does this admirable word mean in action, and what evidence supports the claim attached to it?
