# Self-Handicapping

> The strategy of creating obstacles to one's own performance, so that potential failure can be blamed on the obstacle rather than on a lack of ability.

## Description

Self-handicapping is the strategy of creating or emphasizing obstacles before performance so that possible failure can be blamed on the obstacle rather than ability.

It protects identity from a clean test. If you fail, the excuse is ready. If you succeed, success looks even more impressive.

## How It Works

Unambiguous effort creates psychological risk: if I try fully and fail, what does that say about me?

Self-handicapping reduces that threat by muddying the evidence. The person may procrastinate, overcommit, avoid preparation, announce obstacles, or choose conditions that preserve plausible deniability.

## Recognition Cues

- You create conditions that make good performance less likely.
- You mention obstacles before anyone evaluates you.
- You avoid preparation because a clean effort feels threatening.
- You prefer "I could have done well" to finding out.
- Success and failure both become ego-protective stories.

## Signature Phrasing

- "Creating excuses before failure"
- "Blaming external factors for poor performance"
- "Highlighting how success is impressive despite obstacles"
- "Protecting self-image from genuine evaluation"
- "I did not really try"

## Ask Yourself

> What would happen if you focused on your effort and preparation rather than potential excuses for failure?

## Common False Positives

Real obstacles exist. Naming constraints, illness, workload, or unfair conditions is not automatically self-handicapping.

The bias appears when the obstacle is created, amplified, or preserved because it protects identity from evaluation.

## What Helps

- Define success around controllable preparation and learning.
- Make effort visible to yourself, not just outcomes.
- Reduce identity stakes: performance is data, not a verdict.
- Remove avoidable obstacles early.
- Ask what excuse you are preserving and what it costs.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: supported in motivation and self-esteem research, especially around performance, evaluation threat, and attribution.
