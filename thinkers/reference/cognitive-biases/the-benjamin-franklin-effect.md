# The Benjamin Franklin Effect

> The tendency to like someone more after you do them a favor.

## Description

The Benjamin Franklin Effect is a strange and counterintuitive piece of psychological jujitsu. We tend to assume that we do favors for people we like. But the reality is often the other way around: we come to like people for whom we do favors. Our brain, in its constant effort to create a coherent narrative, thinks, “I just did something nice for that person. I must like them.” The action creates the feeling. Ben Franklin famously used this technique by asking a rival legislator to borrow a rare book. The rival, flattered, lent him the book, and their relationship improved dramatically.

## How It Works

Our brain seeks to maintain cognitive consistency and resolves dissonance by inferring that if we performed a kind act, we must have positive feelings toward the recipient, thus creating liking from the action itself.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I must like them because I did a favor for them"
- "Doing favors creates liking"
- "The action creates the feeling"
- "I just helped them, so I must like them"

## Ask Yourself

> Am I doing this favor because I genuinely like them, or am I convincing myself that I like them because I already did the favor?

## Applying This Knowledge

### Everyday

If you’re having trouble connecting with a new neighbor or colleague, ask them for a small, specific favor—to borrow a tool, to recommend a restaurant, or to explain a concept. This small act of asking for help can be the seed of a new friendship.

*The key is that the favor must be small and easy to grant. A large, burdensome request will have the opposite effect.*

### At Work

As a new manager, you might be tempted to try to win over your team by doing everything for them. A better strategy is to ask for their help. Ask for their input on a decision, ask for their expertise on a problem, ask for their feedback on your performance. This shows that you value their contribution, and it makes them more invested in your success.

*Frame your requests for help as a sign of respect for their expertise. “You’re the expert on this. Could you help me understand…?”*

### In Education

If you want to get a mentor, one of the best ways to start the relationship is to ask for a small, specific piece of advice. Don’t ask for a huge, open-ended commitment like “Will you be my mentor?” Instead, ask, “I’m struggling with X, and I know you have a lot of experience in this area. Could you spare 15 minutes to share your perspective?”

*Show that you have done your homework and that you respect their time. A well-formed, specific question is a gift to a busy person.*
