# Subjective Validation

> The tendency to perceive a vague, general statement as personally meaningful and accurate.

## Description

Subjective validation is the tendency to experience vague, general, or high-probability statements as personally specific and accurate.

It is also known as the Barnum effect. The statement works because the listener supplies the missing specificity from their own life.

## How It Works

Vague statements create room for self-application. People notice the parts that fit, reinterpret the ambiguous parts, and ignore the parts that do not.

Personalization cues make the effect stronger: a quiz result, horoscope, personality profile, cold reading, or "this is for you" framing.

## Recognition Cues

- The statement feels accurate but could apply to many people.
- You supply the concrete examples yourself.
- Flattering or identity-relevant language feels especially true.
- Misses are forgotten while hits are elaborated.
- The claim is hard to falsify because it is broad or elastic.

## Signature Phrasing

- "You have a great need for other people to like and admire you"
- "You have a tendency to be critical of yourself"
- "This describes me perfectly"
- "That sounds exactly like me"
- "It knows me"

## Ask Yourself

> Could this statement apply equally well to many other people, or is it truly specific to me?

## Common False Positives

General advice can still be useful. A broad statement may be valuable if it prompts reflection or action.

The bias appears when subjective fit is mistaken for external accuracy, expertise, diagnosis, or prediction.

## What Helps

- Ask what would make the statement false.
- Check whether it applies to most people.
- Look for specific, risky, testable claims.
- Count misses and non-matches.
- Be especially cautious with flattering identity language.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-supported in research on personality feedback, cold reading, and perceived personal accuracy of vague statements.
