# Placebo Effect

> People's experiences align with their expectations about what will happen.

## Description

The placebo effect is a real change in experience or symptoms that arises from expectation, context, ritual, conditioning, or belief rather than the specific active mechanism of an intervention.

It does not mean "fake." The experience can be real even when the proposed causal explanation is wrong.

## How It Works

Placebo effects can work through expectation, learned associations, attention, meaning, anxiety reduction, and bodily response.

The context around an intervention matters: authority, confidence, ritual, price, branding, setting, and prior experience can all shape what the person feels.

## Recognition Cues

- Experience changes after a credible ritual, label, or expectation.
- The same intervention feels different under different framing.
- Confidence in the treatment affects perceived benefit.
- The context seems to produce part of the effect.
- A subjective outcome improves even when the specific mechanism is uncertain.

## Signature Phrasing

- "It's all in your head"
- "Mind over matter"
- "If you believe it, it will work"
- "The power of positive thinking"
- "The ritual itself helps"

## Ask Yourself

> Is there objective evidence that this intervention is effective, or could the results be due to the power of suggestion?

## Common False Positives

Placebo effects are not proof that an intervention has no value, nor proof that it has the claimed mechanism. They also do not replace appropriate medical evidence or care.

The key distinction is between a real experienced effect and the explanation for that effect.

## What Helps

- Separate subjective improvement from mechanism claims.
- Use controls, blinding, and comparison groups when stakes are high.
- Preserve beneficial context while still asking for evidence.
- Watch for nocebo effects from negative expectation.
- Avoid dismissing people's experience just because expectation contributed.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-established, especially for subjective symptoms and expectation-sensitive outcomes. It is domain-specific and should not be overgeneralized.
