# Cognitive Dissonance

> The mental discomfort experienced by a person who holds two or more contradictory beliefs, ideas, or values, or is confronted by new information that conflicts with existing beliefs.

## Description

Cognitive dissonance is the discomfort that arises when your beliefs, values, identity, decisions, or behavior do not fit together.

The discomfort is not the bias by itself. The bias appears in how quickly the mind tries to reduce the tension: rationalizing, avoiding information, changing the story, minimizing the conflict, or attacking the source of the discomfort.

## How It Works

People are motivated to feel coherent. When a fact, action, or choice threatens that coherence, the mind searches for relief.

Relief can come from honest updating: changing behavior, revising a belief, apologizing, or making a repair. It can also come from distortion: "It was not that bad," "I had no choice," "They are just biased," or "This exception does not count."

The stronger the identity investment, public commitment, sunk cost, or moral self-image, the harder dissonance is to metabolize cleanly.

## Recognition Cues

- You feel an urgent need to explain why the contradiction is not really a contradiction.
- You avoid information that would make the conflict harder to ignore.
- You attack the messenger before considering the message.
- You reframe the standard after failing to meet it.
- You feel calmer after adopting an explanation that protects your self-image, even if it is thin.

## Signature Phrasing

- "It is not that bad."
- "I had no choice."
- "This does not count."
- "They do not understand the full context."
- "I still believe in the principle, but this situation is different."

## Ask Yourself

> What would I have to change if I admitted this conflict was real?

## Common False Positives

Not every contradiction is dissonance-driven rationalization. People can hold competing values honestly, make tradeoffs under constraint, or revise their thinking as they learn more.

The warning sign is not discomfort. It is premature relief: reaching for the explanation that protects you before examining the conflict.

## What Helps

- Pause before resolving the tension. Let the contradiction stay visible for a moment.
- Name both sides plainly: "I say I value X, and I did Y."
- Ask what repair, update, or behavior change would reduce the conflict honestly.
- Separate self-worth from consistency. Being inconsistent means you have information, not that you are irredeemable.
- Watch for explanations that arrive too fast and feel too flattering.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: robust as a broad phenomenon. Specific predictions depend on motivation, identity threat, available justifications, and whether the person has a face-saving path to update.
