# The Backfire Effect

> The tendency of some people to resist accepting evidence that conflicts with their beliefs, and to strengthen their support for those beliefs instead.

## Description

The backfire effect describes cases where corrective evidence against a belief leads someone to resist, defend, or sometimes strengthen the original belief.

It is most likely when the belief is tied to identity, group belonging, moral status, or a public commitment. The correction is not experienced as neutral information; it is experienced as a threat.

## How It Works

When evidence threatens identity, people may shift from accuracy mode to defense mode. They scrutinize the correction, search for flaws, question the source, and retrieve supporting arguments for their existing view.

The result may look like irrational stubbornness from the outside. From the inside, it often feels like protecting integrity, loyalty, dignity, or common sense from attack.

## Recognition Cues

- Corrections increase anger, certainty, or source distrust.
- The person moves quickly from evidence to identity defense.
- The conversation becomes about loyalty, respect, or contempt.
- New facts are treated as hostile weapons rather than information.
- The correction leaves the person more socially aligned with their original group.

## Signature Phrasing

- "That source is corrupt."
- "This proves they are afraid of the truth."
- "You are attacking people like me."
- "They would say anything to make us look bad."
- "Now I am even more convinced."

## Ask Yourself

> Is this evidence being received as information, or as an identity threat?

## Common False Positives

The backfire effect is often overstated. People do update their beliefs, and corrections can work, especially when they are clear, non-threatening, repeated responsibly, and come from trusted sources.

Do not assume every disagreement is backfire. The other person may have legitimate objections, different background knowledge, or distrust based on prior experience.

## What Helps

- Reduce identity threat before offering correction.
- Start with shared values or shared goals.
- Ask what evidence would matter before presenting yours.
- Allow face-saving updates: "Given new information, it makes sense to revise."
- Use trusted messengers and specific claims rather than broad attacks on identity.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: contested in strength and frequency. Motivated reasoning and defensive resistance are robust, but strong "corrections make beliefs more false" effects are not universal and depend heavily on context.
