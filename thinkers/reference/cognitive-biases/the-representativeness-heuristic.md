# The Representativeness Heuristic

> The tendency to judge the likelihood of an event by how well it matches a particular prototype or stereotype in our minds.

## Description

The representativeness heuristic is the tendency to judge probability by how much something resembles a prototype, stereotype, or familiar pattern.

If a person, event, or story "looks like" a category, the match can feel more informative than the base rate. The mind asks, "How representative is this?" before asking, "How common is this?"

## How It Works

Pattern matching is fast and often useful. But it can override statistics when the prototype is vivid.

The classic danger is base-rate neglect: a description fits a rare category well, so the rare category feels likely even though a more common category remains more probable.

## Recognition Cues

- A stereotype or prototype drives the estimate.
- You ignore the size of the category or base rate.
- A vivid description feels more diagnostic than it is.
- You mistake resemblance for likelihood.
- You overgeneralize from a case that "seems typical."

## Signature Phrasing

- "They seem like the type."
- "This has all the signs of..."
- "It just fits the pattern."
- "That is exactly what someone like that would do."
- "It looks representative."

## Ask Yourself

> What is the base rate before I consider how well this matches the prototype?

## Common False Positives

Representativeness can be useful. Similarity, symptoms, patterns, and category fit often provide real evidence.

The bias appears when resemblance overwhelms base rates, alternative explanations, or direct evidence.

## What Helps

- Start with the base rate or comparison class.
- Ask how common the category is before considering fit.
- Use structured criteria rather than prototype impressions.
- Look for disconfirming details that do not fit the story.
- Separate "similar to" from "likely to be."

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: robust in judgment and decision research, especially in tasks involving stereotypes, base rates, and probability estimates.
