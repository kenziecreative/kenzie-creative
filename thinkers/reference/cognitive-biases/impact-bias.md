# Impact Bias

> People tend to overestimate the emotional impact of future events, both in intensity and duration.

## Description

Impact bias is the tendency to overestimate how intense and long-lasting your future emotional reaction to an event will be.

People often imagine that a promotion, breakup, failure, purchase, move, or public embarrassment will change their emotional life more permanently than it does.

## How It Works

Forecasts focus too much on the focal event and too little on everything else that will still exist afterward: routines, relationships, distractions, adaptation, coping, and new concerns.

People also underestimate their psychological immune system, the mind's capacity to explain, adapt, recover, normalize, and move attention elsewhere.

## Recognition Cues

- You predict a future event will change everything emotionally.
- You focus on the event while ignoring the rest of daily life.
- You assume the first feeling will be the lasting feeling.
- You make major choices to avoid a feeling that may fade quickly.
- You forget how often you adapted after past wins and losses.

## Signature Phrasing

- "This is the best/worst thing that could ever happen to me!"
- "I'll never get over this"
- "My life will be perfect if..."
- "This changes everything"
- "I will feel this way forever"

## Ask Yourself

> How long did similar feelings actually last before, and what else will still be part of my life afterward?

## Common False Positives

Some events do have deep and lasting emotional consequences. Impact bias is not a reason to dismiss grief, trauma, joy, or meaningful life changes.

The bias appears when the forecast ignores adaptation, context, coping, and the many other experiences that will share attention with the event.

## What Helps

- Look at your own emotional history after comparable events.
- Ask people who have experienced the event what changed and what normalized.
- Forecast a day-in-the-life, not just the headline moment.
- Consider both the peak feeling and the likely adaptation curve.
- Delay irreversible choices when your forecast is dominated by immediate emotion.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-supported in affective forecasting research, though the size of the bias varies by event type, coping resources, and life circumstances.
