# In-Group Bias

> The tendency to favor members of one’s own group over out-group members.

## Description

In-group bias is the tendency to favor people we perceive as belonging to "us" over people perceived as "them."

The group can be political, professional, cultural, national, religious, educational, organizational, or informal. Even thin group boundaries can change trust, generosity, suspicion, and interpretation.

## How It Works

Group membership acts as a shortcut for safety and trust. If someone is "one of us," their motives feel more understandable and their mistakes feel more forgivable.

The same mechanism can make out-group members seem less nuanced. Their errors look more revealing, their successes look less legitimate, and their disagreement feels more threatening.

## Recognition Cues

- You explain your group's mistakes as exceptions but treat the other group's mistakes as revealing.
- You trust weak evidence from insiders more than stronger evidence from outsiders.
- You feel pressure to defend the group before understanding what happened.
- You assume critics from outside the group are hostile or ignorant.
- You notice out-group identity before individual behavior.

## Signature Phrasing

- "They do not understand us."
- "Our side would never do that."
- "They are all the same."
- "We need to stand together."
- "I know them; they are one of us."

## Ask Yourself

> Would I interpret this action the same way if it came from my group and from an out-group?

## Common False Positives

Loyalty, solidarity, and trust are not automatically biased. Groups can create real obligations, shared knowledge, and mutual support.

The warning sign is asymmetric judgment: giving your group context, mercy, and individuality while denying those same things to others.

## What Helps

- Compare like with like: would the same evidence satisfy you if the group labels were reversed?
- Evaluate claims before checking who benefits.
- Create mixed-group work where people share goals and accountability.
- Ask insiders to name the strongest out-group criticism fairly.
- Protect dissent within the group so loyalty does not require denial.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: robust across social psychology, though the strength and expression of the bias vary with identity salience, perceived threat, competition, leadership cues, and norms.
