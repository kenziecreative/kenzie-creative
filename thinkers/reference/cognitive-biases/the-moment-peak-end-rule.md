# The Moment (Peak-End Rule)

> The tendency to judge an experience based on its most intense point and its ending, rather than on the sum of all its moments.

## Description

The peak-end rule is the tendency to remember an experience disproportionately by its most intense moment and its ending, rather than by the average of every moment.

The remembered experience can differ from the lived experience. A long, mostly pleasant event with a painful ending may be remembered worse than a shorter, more difficult event that ended well.

## How It Works

Memory compresses experience into summaries. Emotional peaks and endings are easy to retrieve and become anchors for the whole episode.

This affects how people evaluate services, relationships, jobs, trips, meetings, medical procedures, learning experiences, and careers.

## Recognition Cues

- The final moment changes your evaluation of the whole experience.
- One intense high or low dominates memory.
- Ordinary duration matters less than memorable moments.
- You judge a process by how it ended rather than how it mostly felt.
- You design for average quality but neglect peak moments and endings.

## Signature Phrasing

- "Remembering the worst moment"
- "Focusing on the ending"
- "Ignoring the full experience"
- "Peak shapes memory"
- "It ended badly, so the whole thing feels bad"

## Ask Yourself

> Am I evaluating the lived experience, the remembered experience, or the ending?

## Common False Positives

Peaks and endings really do matter. They are part of the experience, and memory itself affects future choices.

The bias appears when those moments crowd out important information about duration, consistency, harm, effort, or average quality.

## What Helps

- Evaluate both lived quality and remembered quality.
- Keep notes during long experiences rather than relying only on memory afterward.
- Design humane endings, especially for difficult processes.
- Do not let a dramatic finale erase earlier evidence.
- Ask whether a peak moment is representative or merely memorable.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-supported in remembered utility research, though effects vary with experience type, duration, salience, and measurement.
