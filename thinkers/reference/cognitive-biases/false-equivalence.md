# False Equivalence

> Treating two things as meaningfully equal because they share a surface similarity, while ignoring differences in evidence, scale, context, intent, or consequence.

## Description

False equivalence creates a misleading sense of balance. It says, in effect, "both sides did something, so both sides are the same." Sometimes this is a deliberate rhetorical move. Sometimes it is a cognitive shortcut created by the desire to appear fair, neutral, or sophisticated.

The mistake is not comparison itself. Comparison is useful. The mistake is flattening important differences. A five-minute delay is not the same as a repeated no-show. A fringe claim is not equivalent to a strong expert consensus. A mistake, a pattern, and an intentional abuse are not interchangeable just because all can be described as "bad."

## How It Works

People often use symmetry as a shortcut for fairness. If two claims, groups, or actions can be placed on opposite sides of a sentence, they can feel balanced. Media formats, debate norms, and conflict avoidance can reinforce this by giving equal attention to unequal cases.

False equivalence also helps people evade judgment. If everything is equally bad, then no specific responsibility has to be weighed.

## Recognition Cues

- Two cases are treated as equal because of one shared feature.
- Differences in scale, frequency, evidence, or intent are brushed aside.
- "Both sides" language replaces actual comparison.
- Neutrality is performed by flattening distinctions.
- The comparison changes the subject away from the original responsibility.

## Signature Phrasing

- "Both sides do it."
- "It is all the same."
- "They are equally bad."
- "There is no difference, really."
- "If you criticize one, you have to criticize the other the same way."

## Ask Yourself

> Equal in what specific respect, and unequal in what respects that matter?

## Common False Positives

Sometimes two things really are meaningfully comparable. Shared incentives, shared harms, shared methods, or shared hypocrisy can matter. The problem is not saying "these are alike." The problem is saying "these are alike enough that the differences no longer matter."

False equivalence should be answered with criteria, not indignation. Name the axis of comparison.

## What Helps

Separate similarity from equivalence. Use dimensions: evidence quality, magnitude, frequency, intent, role responsibility, power, reversibility, and consequences.

In conversation, try: "I agree there is a similarity. I do not think it makes the cases equivalent. The difference that matters is..." This preserves fairness while restoring judgment.

## Evidence Status

False equivalence is best understood as an argument error and evaluation failure rather than a classic cognitive bias. It belongs in this corpus because it is powered by fairness intuitions, conflict avoidance, and pattern-matching shortcuts.
