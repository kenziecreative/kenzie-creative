# Attention

> The cognitive process of selectively concentrating on one aspect of the environment while ignoring other things.

## Description

Attention is the process of selectively focusing on some information while filtering out other information.

Strictly speaking, attention is a cognitive function rather than a bias. It belongs in the toolkit because many biases work by shaping what receives attention, what gets ignored, and what feels salient.

## How It Works

Attention is limited. Focusing on one thing increases processing depth there while reducing awareness elsewhere.

This makes attention powerful and dangerous. It enables deep work, learning, and perception, but it also creates blind spots, inattentional blindness, and vulnerability to framing.

## Recognition Cues

- You miss obvious information outside the task focus.
- What is salient feels more important than what is quiet.
- The frame of a question determines what you notice.
- Multitasking reduces depth and increases errors.
- You confuse "I did not notice it" with "it was not there."

## Signature Phrasing

- "The spotlight of your consciousness"
- "Narrower than we think"
- "Blind to things outside its beam"
- "Like a camera’s focus"
- "I was focused on something else"

## Ask Yourself

> What important information might I be missing outside my current focus?

## Common False Positives

Focused attention is not bad. Many important tasks require narrowing awareness.

The risk appears when the focus is assumed to be complete awareness, or when someone else controls the spotlight without you noticing.

## What Helps

- Decide what deserves focus before the environment decides for you.
- Use checklists for things that fall outside natural attention.
- Build deliberate zoom-out moments.
- Reduce task switching for deep work.
- Ask what the current frame makes invisible.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: foundational cognitive function with robust evidence for limits, selectivity, inattentional blindness, salience effects, and task-switching costs.
