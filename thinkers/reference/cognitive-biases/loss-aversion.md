# Loss Aversion

> People prefer avoiding losses to acquiring equivalent gains.

## Description

Loss aversion is the tendency to feel the pain of losses more strongly than the pleasure of equivalent gains.

It makes giving something up feel more urgent than acquiring the same amount would feel rewarding. This can protect people from careless risk, but it can also make them overprotect what they have.

## How It Works

People evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point: what they already have, expected, budgeted, or feel entitled to. Movement below that reference point feels like loss.

Because losses are emotionally salient, people may take bad risks to avoid accepting them, reject beneficial tradeoffs, or hold onto resources that would be better redeployed.

## Recognition Cues

- Avoiding a loss feels more important than pursuing an equal gain.
- You reject a trade because giving something up feels painful, even if the net value is positive.
- You keep an option mainly to avoid "locking in" a loss.
- You focus on what will be lost and underweight what could be gained.
- A change feels bad because it moves away from the current reference point.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I can't afford to lose"
- "Better safe than sorry"
- "Don't want to throw good money after bad"
- "I've already invested so much"
- "I just want to get back to even"

## Ask Yourself

> Am I rejecting this because the total tradeoff is bad, or because the loss side feels louder?

## Common False Positives

Loss sensitivity is not irrational by itself. Some losses are irreversible, compound, or threaten survival, trust, reputation, or optionality.

The bias appears when loss pain outweighs realistic comparison of future costs, future benefits, and alternatives.

## What Helps

- Reframe the decision around future value, not past position.
- Ask what you would choose if you did not already own the thing.
- Compare the loss to the opportunity cost of avoiding it.
- Predefine stop-loss rules before emotions rise.
- Separate "this hurts" from "this is the wrong move."

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: strongly associated with prospect theory and widely observed in decision research, though magnitude varies by context, stakes, and framing.
