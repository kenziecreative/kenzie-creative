# Survivorship Bias

> The logical error of concentrating on the people or things that “survived” some process and inadvertently overlooking those that did not because of their lack of visibility.

## Description

Survivorship bias is the error of drawing conclusions from visible successes, survivors, or remaining cases while overlooking the invisible failures, dropouts, losses, and missing cases.

The visible sample is not the full sample. If you only study what made it through the filter, you may misunderstand what caused success, what caused failure, and how risky the path actually was.

## How It Works

Survivors are easier to see, interview, celebrate, copy, and remember. Failures often disappear from view.

This creates distorted causal stories. People copy the habits of winners without asking how many non-winners had the same habits, or they underestimate danger because the harmed cases are absent.

## Recognition Cues

- Advice comes only from people who succeeded.
- Failed attempts are missing from the dataset.
- A success story is treated as a general strategy.
- The analysis ignores who did not return, finish, publish, sell, survive, or get selected.
- You hear "they did X and succeeded" without "how many did X and failed?"

## Signature Phrasing

- "Every successful person did this."
- "We copied the winners."
- "Look at the companies that made it."
- "The people who stuck with it succeeded."
- "This worked for me."

## Ask Yourself

> Who is missing from the evidence because they did not survive the selection process?

## Common False Positives

Learning from successful cases is useful. Survivors can reveal practices, constraints, and strategies worth examining.

The bias appears when success cases are treated as sufficient evidence without comparing them to failures, base rates, and selection effects.

## What Helps

- Ask for the denominator: how many started?
- Study failures and near-misses, not only winners.
- Identify selection filters that hide cases.
- Compare success traits against traits common among failures.
- Treat vivid survivor stories as hypotheses, not proof.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: robust as a sampling and selection problem. It is both a cognitive bias and a statistical error.
