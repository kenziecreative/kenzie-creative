# Hyperbolic Discounting

> People tend to prefer smaller, immediate rewards over larger, later rewards.

## Description

Hyperbolic discounting is the tendency to sharply devalue rewards or benefits that are delayed, especially when an immediate option is available.

The bias is not simply impatience. It is inconsistent time valuation: people may prefer $100 in a year over $50 in eleven months, but prefer $50 today over $100 next month.

## How It Works

Immediate rewards are vivid, certain, and emotionally available. Future rewards are abstract and easier to discount.

As delay gets close to the present, preferences can reverse. A future plan that looked sensible from a distance loses to the immediate temptation when the moment arrives.

## Recognition Cues

- You choose a smaller immediate reward over a larger delayed one.
- Your preference changes when one option becomes available now.
- You repeatedly plan well for the future and defect in the moment.
- Delayed benefits feel unreal compared with immediate comfort.
- You underestimate how often "later" becomes "not at all."

## Signature Phrasing

- "I'll do it later"
- "A bird in the hand is worth two in the bush"
- "I want it now"
- "Why wait?"
- "Future me can handle it"

## Ask Yourself

> If both options were delayed equally, which would I choose?

## Common False Positives

Immediate rewards are not automatically wrong. Sometimes sooner is genuinely better because of uncertainty, liquidity, urgency, or compounding use.

The bias appears when immediacy itself drives the decision more than real value, risk, or timing logic.

## What Helps

- Make future rewards concrete and visible.
- Precommit before temptation is present.
- Break delayed goals into near-term milestones.
- Add friction to immediate temptations and ease to future-serving actions.
- Use equal-delay comparisons to reveal preference reversals.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-supported in behavioral economics, psychology, and intertemporal choice research.
