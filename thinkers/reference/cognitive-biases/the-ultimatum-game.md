# The Ultimatum Game

> A bargaining model showing that people may reject an unfair offer even when rejection costs them.

## Description

The ultimatum game is a simple behavioral economics experiment. One person proposes how to split a resource. The other person can accept the split, in which case both get the proposed amounts, or reject it, in which case both get nothing.

If people cared only about immediate material gain, they would accept any positive offer. In practice, people often reject offers they experience as unfair. The rejection is costly, but it enforces a social message: "I will not accept treatment that violates fairness norms."

## How It Works

The game exposes the social layer inside bargaining. Offers are not evaluated only by payoff. They are evaluated by respect, fairness, status, intent, precedent, and future implications.

Rejecting an unfair offer can be a costly signal. It says that exploitation has consequences. That can be irrational in a one-time narrow payoff sense and rational in a wider social sense.

## Recognition Cues

- A technically beneficial offer is experienced as insulting.
- Someone would rather lose than validate unfair treatment.
- The conflict is about respect as much as resources.
- A party focuses on precedent: "If I accept this, it teaches them how to treat me."
- The proposer underestimates how much fairness matters to the responder.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I would rather get nothing than accept that."
- "That offer is an insult."
- "It is not about the money."
- "They need to learn they cannot treat people this way."
- "A bad deal sets a bad precedent."

## Ask Yourself

> Am I rejecting this because the offer is materially bad, because it violates fairness, or because accepting would create a harmful precedent?

## Common False Positives

Rejecting an offer is not always fairness enforcement. It may be pride, misread incentives, spite, poor calculation, or lack of information. Accepting a lopsided deal is not always weakness; it may be strategic, compassionate, or necessary.

The model is most useful when the emotional force of fairness seems larger than the immediate payoff.

## What Helps

When making offers, design for perceived fairness, not only technical value. Explain the rationale. Acknowledge constraints. Avoid making the other person feel reduced to what you can get away with.

When receiving offers, separate three questions: What do I gain? What norm does this accept? What future behavior will this teach?

## Evidence Status

The ultimatum game is a well-established experimental paradigm. Results vary by culture, stakes, relationship, framing, and norms, but the core finding is durable: fairness and punishment motives can override narrow payoff maximization.
