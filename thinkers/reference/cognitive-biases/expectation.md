# Expectation

> The powerful influence that our expectations have on our perception and our experience.

## Description

Expectation is the influence that prior belief, prediction, framing, or anticipation has on perception, interpretation, emotion, and behavior.

The mind does not passively receive reality. It predicts what it is likely to encounter, then uses incoming evidence to update or confirm that prediction.

## How It Works

Expectations shape attention: you notice what you are prepared to see. They shape interpretation: ambiguous information gets read in the expected direction. They shape behavior: you may act in ways that help produce the expected result.

This can become a loop. Expecting hostility can make you guarded, which makes others colder, which then confirms the original expectation.

## Recognition Cues

- You interpret ambiguous evidence in the direction you expected.
- The label given before an experience changes the experience.
- You behave differently because of what you expect, then treat the result as proof.
- You notice confirming details faster than disconfirming ones.
- A first impression becomes the template for later perception.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I just knew it would happen"
- "This always turns out this way"
- "I expected them to act like that"
- "It’s exactly what I thought"
- "I knew what this would be like"

## Ask Yourself

> What evidence do I have that contradicts my expectations?

## Common False Positives

Expectations are often useful. Prior knowledge helps people perceive efficiently, prepare, and interpret weak signals.

The bias appears when expectation overrules evidence, narrows attention, or creates the very outcome it claims merely to predict.

## What Helps

- Name the expectation before evaluating the evidence.
- Ask what you would notice if the opposite were true.
- Blind labels, prices, names, or identities where possible.
- Separate first impressions from later data.
- Watch for behavior changes that may make the expectation self-fulfilling.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: broad and well-supported across perception, placebo/nocebo effects, priming, self-fulfilling prophecy, and expectation effects; specific mechanisms vary by domain.
