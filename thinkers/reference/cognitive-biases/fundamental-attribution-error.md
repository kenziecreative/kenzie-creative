# Fundamental Attribution Error

> Over-explaining another person's behavior through character while under-explaining it through situation, constraint, role, or incentive.

## Description

The fundamental attribution error is the habit of turning behavior into personality too quickly. Someone is short with you, and the mind says "rude." A teammate misses a deadline, and the mind says "unreliable." A public figure makes a poor decision, and the mind says "corrupt" or "stupid." The behavior may be real, but the explanation outruns the evidence.

This matters because attribution drives response. If the problem is "their character," punishment, avoidance, or contempt can feel natural. If the problem is workload, incentives, ambiguity, fear, or missing information, a different response may work better.

## How It Works

Behavior is visible. Context is often hidden. The mind prefers simple causes, especially in emotionally charged situations, so it converts "what happened" into "what kind of person would do that."

This can be amplified by status, distance, and moral judgment. We make harsher trait inferences about strangers, opponents, low-status people, and people whose actions inconvenience us.

## Recognition Cues

- You move from a single action to a global trait.
- You ask "what is wrong with them?" before asking "what situation are they in?"
- You interpret ambiguous behavior in the most character-based way.
- You treat role behavior as personal essence.
- You feel less curious once you have a label.

## Signature Phrasing

- "That's just who they are."
- "They always do this."
- "Some people are just lazy."
- "You can tell what kind of person they are."
- "No decent person would act that way."

## Ask Yourself

> What would have to be true about their situation for this behavior to make more sense?

## Common False Positives

Sometimes the dispositional explanation is correct. Character, values, temperament, and skill do matter. The error is not noticing character; the error is treating character as obvious before checking context.

It is also possible to use context as an excuse. A good attribution separates explanation from permission: "I can understand what contributed to this, and I can still hold a boundary."

## What Helps

Run a two-cause rule. Before settling on a trait explanation, name at least one plausible situational explanation. Before settling on a situational explanation, name at least one possible personal or skill-based factor. This creates a more balanced map.

In teams, shift from blame-first questions to system questions: "What made this easier to do and harder to catch?" That question preserves accountability while making improvement possible.

## Evidence Status

The attribution literature is broad and durable, but the term "fundamental" can overstate universality. Attribution patterns vary by culture and context. The practical lesson remains strong: visible behavior often receives too much causal weight.
