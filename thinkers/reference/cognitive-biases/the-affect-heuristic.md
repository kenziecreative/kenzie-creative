# The Affect Heuristic

> The tendency to let our emotional response to a situation dictate our decisions and judgments, often in a snap.

## Description

The affect heuristic is the tendency to use immediate emotional reaction as a shortcut for judging risk, benefit, value, or truth.

If something feels good, its benefits seem larger and its risks smaller. If something feels bad, its risks seem larger and its benefits smaller.

## How It Works

Emotion provides fast information about preference, danger, attraction, trust, and meaning. That speed is useful, but it can collapse multiple questions into one feeling.

The mind may substitute "How do I feel about this?" for harder questions like "How likely is the risk?", "What evidence supports this?", or "What tradeoff am I accepting?"

## Recognition Cues

- A good feeling makes weak evidence seem stronger.
- A bad feeling makes a manageable risk feel unacceptable.
- You judge benefits and risks in opposite directions based on mood.
- You struggle to explain the decision beyond "it feels right" or "it feels wrong."
- Presentation, source, or mood changes your evaluation more than the substance changes.

## Signature Phrasing

- "It just feels right."
- "I have a bad feeling about this."
- "I love this, so the risks seem worth it."
- "I hate this idea, so it must be dangerous."
- "My gut says no."

## Ask Yourself

> What would I think about the risks and benefits if my immediate feeling were neutral?

## Common False Positives

Emotion is not irrational noise. Feelings can contain real information from experience, values, and pattern recognition.

The bias appears when affect substitutes for analysis or makes risk and benefit judgments move together without evidence.

## What Helps

- Name the emotion before making the judgment.
- Evaluate risks and benefits separately.
- Use pre-set criteria for high-stakes decisions.
- Add a cooling-off period when affect is strong.
- Ask someone less emotionally activated to review the evidence.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-supported in risk perception and decision research.
