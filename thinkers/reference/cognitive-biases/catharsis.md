# Catharsis

> The incorrect belief that venting your anger will make it go away.

## Description

Catharsis, in this bias context, is the mistaken belief that venting anger aggressively will discharge it and make it go away.

The pressure-release metaphor is intuitive, but aggressive venting can rehearse anger, strengthen grievance narratives, and keep the body activated.

## How It Works

Anger is partly sustained by attention, interpretation, bodily arousal, and rehearsal. Venting can feel relieving for a moment because it expresses energy, but it can also keep the anger story active.

The behavior teaches the body and mind to repeat the anger pattern rather than metabolize it.

## Recognition Cues

- Venting gives temporary relief but leaves you more activated later.
- The story becomes sharper and more certain with each retelling.
- You seek agreement that intensifies the anger.
- Expression becomes rehearsal rather than regulation.
- The goal shifts from understanding to discharge.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I need to vent to feel better"
- "Letting it all out will release my anger"
- "Holding in anger is harmful"
- "Expressing anger is a necessary purge"
- "I just need to blow up once"

## Ask Yourself

> Does expressing your anger actually reduce your feelings, or does it make them stronger over time?

## Common False Positives

Expression is not always harmful. Naming feelings, setting boundaries, seeking support, and assertive communication can help.

The bias is the belief that aggressive discharge itself solves anger.

## What Helps

- Downshift arousal before analysis or confrontation.
- Name the feeling without escalating the story.
- Use movement, breathing, or time-outs to regulate the body.
- Convert anger into a specific request, boundary, or repair action.
- Avoid audiences that reward escalation.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: the simple "venting anger reduces anger" model is not well-supported; constructive emotional expression and regulation are different from aggressive catharsis.
