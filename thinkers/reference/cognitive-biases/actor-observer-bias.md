# Actor-Observer Bias

> Explaining your own behavior through circumstances while explaining other people's similar behavior through character.

## Description

Actor-observer bias is the asymmetry between how we explain ourselves and how we explain others. When we are the person acting, we can feel the constraints: the deadline, the stress, the missing information, the awkward social pressure. When someone else acts, we mostly see the behavior. That makes their conduct look more like a stable trait.

The bias often shows up in conflict. "I snapped because I was exhausted" becomes "they snapped because they are hostile." "I missed that message because I was overwhelmed" becomes "they ignored me because they do not care." Both explanations might contain some truth, but the bias gives our own context more weight than theirs.

## How It Works

Your own situation is vivid from the inside. Other people's situations are usually inferred from the outside. Because visible behavior is easier to process than invisible constraint, the mind fills in missing context with personality labels.

This is not always selfishness. It is partly an information problem. You know the full story behind your own action and only a thin slice of the other person's. The trouble begins when that thin slice feels complete.

## Recognition Cues

- You excuse your own behavior with context but describe someone else's same behavior as a trait.
- You use temporary language for yourself and permanent language for others.
- You feel certain about another person's motive without having asked.
- You treat your own mistake as understandable and theirs as revealing.
- You see a pattern in the other person after one or two incidents.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I was under pressure, but they are just careless."
- "I had a reason. They have a character problem."
- "When I do it, it is different."
- "That shows what kind of person they are."
- "I know why I did it; I know who they are."

## Ask Yourself

> If I had done the same thing, what explanation would I hope someone gave me before judging?

## Common False Positives

Some behavior really does reflect stable preferences, habits, values, or incentives. The antidote is not to deny character. It is to avoid making character the first and only explanation.

Repeated behavior across different situations deserves more weight than one incident. Clear evidence of intent deserves more weight than guesswork. The question is whether you have enough information to move from "they did this" to "they are this."

## What Helps

Use symmetrical explanations. For your own behavior, ask what trait or habit might also be involved. For someone else's behavior, ask what situational pressure might also be involved.

In conversation, replace verdicts with context-seeking questions: "What was happening on your side?" or "What made that the choice in the moment?" This keeps accountability available without locking the person into a label too early.

## Evidence Status

Attribution biases are well-established in social psychology, though their size depends on culture, context, relationship, and available information. The most useful practical claim is modest: people often overweight visible behavior and underweight unseen context.
