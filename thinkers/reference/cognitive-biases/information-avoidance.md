# Information Avoidance

> Avoiding information because knowing it might create anxiety, force a decision, threaten identity, or require action.

## Description

Information avoidance is the choice not to know. It can be small, like not checking a bank balance, or large, like avoiding medical results, performance feedback, relationship truth, or evidence that a cherished belief is wrong.

Avoidance can feel calming in the short term because it postpones discomfort. But it often increases long-term cost. Reality continues moving while response is delayed.

## How It Works

Information can create obligations. Once you know, you may have to decide, grieve, apologize, change, confront, spend money, leave, or admit uncertainty. Avoidance protects the current self from emotional disruption.

It is often tied to cognitive dissonance. If new information threatens your self-image, group identity, plans, or sunk costs, not knowing can feel easier than integrating the truth.

## Recognition Cues

- You delay checking information that would help you act.
- You call something "stressful" when it is actually decision-relevant.
- You prefer vague reassurance over clear measurement.
- You avoid people who might tell you the truth.
- The cost of not knowing is growing, but the relief of avoidance keeps winning.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I do not want to know."
- "Let's not look at that yet."
- "I will deal with it later."
- "Ignorance is bliss."
- "If I ask, I might have to do something."

## Ask Yourself

> What decision, feeling, or identity change am I postponing by not knowing?

## Common False Positives

Not all information avoidance is irrational. People may reasonably avoid spoilers, graphic details, doomscrolling, intrusive updates, or information they cannot use yet. Timing and capacity matter.

The unhealthy pattern appears when avoided information is relevant, actionable, and increasingly costly to ignore.

## What Helps

Shrink the exposure. Check one number, read one page, ask one question, or schedule one appointment. Pair information with support and next steps so knowing does not feel like falling into a void.

Before seeking information, decide what you will do under different outcomes. This turns dread into a response plan.

## Evidence Status

Information avoidance is supported across health, finance, relationships, politics, and organizational behavior. The motives vary: anxiety reduction, belief protection, regret avoidance, responsibility avoidance, and identity defense.
