# The Dunning-Kruger Effect

> A cognitive bias whereby people with low ability at a task overestimate their ability, while experts tend to underestimate their own.

## Description

The Dunning-Kruger effect describes miscalibration between competence and confidence, especially when limited skill also limits the ability to recognize errors.

It is often summarized too crudely as "incompetent people think they are experts." The more useful point is that skill and self-assessment often develop together. Early learners may not yet know what good performance looks like.

## How It Works

Accurate self-assessment requires domain knowledge. Without enough examples, feedback, and standards, people may judge performance by superficial cues.

As competence grows, people often become more aware of nuance, uncertainty, and edge cases. That can reduce overconfidence, and in some cases make competent people underestimate how much they know relative to beginners.

## Recognition Cues

- Confidence is high but the person cannot explain tradeoffs, failure modes, or limits.
- Feedback is dismissed because the person lacks the criteria to understand it.
- Early knowledge produces sweeping claims.
- The person cannot distinguish simple cases from hard cases.
- Expertise brings more caution, not less.

## Signature Phrasing

- "Overconfidence despite lack of skill"
- "Unawareness of own incompetence"
- "Experts underestimate own abilities"
- "Confident but wrong"
- "I know enough to know this is easy"

## Ask Yourself

> What evidence do I have that accurately reflects my competence in this area?

## Common False Positives

Do not use this as a smug label for people you disagree with. Confidence can come from actual expertise, different assumptions, or different risk tolerance.

The bias is most useful as a self-check: where might my ability to evaluate my ability be underdeveloped?

## What Helps

- Seek feedback from people with demonstrated competence.
- Use objective tests, work samples, and outcome data.
- Ask what experts worry about that beginners ignore.
- Learn the standards of the field before judging your rank in it.
- Keep confidence proportional to demonstrated performance, not fluency.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: influential and partly supported, but often oversimplified in popular use. Confidence-competence miscalibration is real; the exact curve varies by task, measurement, and population.
