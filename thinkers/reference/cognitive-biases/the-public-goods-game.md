# The Public Goods Game

> A social dilemma where everyone benefits from a shared good, but each individual has an incentive to contribute less and rely on others.

## Description

The public goods game is a model for understanding contribution problems. A public good is something people can benefit from even if they personally do not help create or maintain it: clean shared spaces, team culture, trust, open-source software, public health, institutional legitimacy, or a well-run meeting.

The dilemma is simple: everyone is better off if enough people contribute, but each person may be tempted to free-ride. If too many people do that, the good deteriorates.

## How It Works

Each person compares the private cost of contributing with the shared benefit of the public good. If the benefit is available either way, non-contribution can look individually rational. But when many people reason that way, the collective outcome gets worse for everyone.

Trust, visibility, group identity, repeated interaction, sanctions, and norms can change the incentives.

## Recognition Cues

- Everyone values the shared good, but contribution is uneven.
- People complain about decline while withholding effort.
- Non-contributors are hard to identify or confront.
- Contributors start feeling exploited.
- The system relies on goodwill without feedback, ownership, or norms.

## Signature Phrasing

- "Why should I do it if others are not?"
- "Someone else will maintain it."
- "My contribution will not matter."
- "We all benefit, but no one owns it."
- "The culture is getting worse."

## Ask Yourself

> What would happen to this shared good if everyone contributed at my current level?

## Common False Positives

Not every low contribution is free-riding. People may lack capacity, clarity, access, authority, or trust that their contribution will be used fairly.

Before moralizing, check the design. A public goods problem often reflects weak incentives and weak structure, not only weak character.

## What Helps

Make contribution visible, meaningful, and fair. Clarify what counts as contribution. Reduce unnecessary cost. Reward contribution. Address repeated free-riding. Create smaller groups where reputation and reciprocity can work.

For yourself, do not confuse "my part is small" with "my part is irrelevant." Public goods are built from small repeated contributions.

## Evidence Status

The public goods game is a robust experimental and theoretical model in economics, game theory, and social science. Real-world outcomes depend heavily on institutions, norms, trust, enforcement, and group size.
