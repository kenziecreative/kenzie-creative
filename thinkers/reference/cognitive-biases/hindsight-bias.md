# Hindsight Bias

> The tendency to see past events as being more predictable than they actually were.

## Description

Hindsight bias is the tendency to see past events as more predictable, obvious, or inevitable after the outcome is known.

Once you know what happened, the past rearranges itself around the result. Warning signs become clearer, alternative outcomes fade, and uncertainty at the time is easy to forget.

## How It Works

Memory is reconstructive. Knowing the outcome changes which details feel important and how earlier uncertainty is remembered.

The mind also likes coherent stories. A messy decision environment becomes a clean narrative: "The signs were there." That narrative can feel like learning while actually distorting what was knowable at the time.

## Recognition Cues

- You say an outcome was obvious after it happens.
- You judge past decisions without reconstructing what people knew then.
- You remember your prior confidence as stronger than it was.
- You identify a single decisive cause after seeing the result.
- You become impatient with people who did not foresee what is now clear.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I knew it all along"
- "It was obvious in hindsight"
- "The outcome was inevitable"
- "Everyone could see it coming"
- "The signs were all there"

## Ask Yourself

> What did we actually know before the outcome, and what only became clear afterward?

## Common False Positives

Some outcomes were foreseeable. Hindsight bias does not mean nobody could have known or that warnings should be ignored.

The bias appears when outcome knowledge inflates perceived predictability without checking prior probabilities, available evidence, and competing possibilities.

## What Helps

- Keep decision journals with predictions, assumptions, confidence levels, and alternatives.
- Review what was knowable at the time, not only what happened.
- Ask what other outcomes seemed plausible before the result.
- Separate process quality from outcome quality.
- Use premortems before decisions and postmortems after decisions.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: robust across memory, judgment, and decision review contexts.
