# Dunbar's Number

> The idea that people have limited cognitive and social capacity for maintaining stable relationships, often summarized around a rough group size of 150.

## Description

Dunbar's Number is a social-capacity lens, not a hard rule. It suggests that humans can only maintain a limited number of stable social relationships because relationships require memory, attention, trust tracking, obligation tracking, and emotional upkeep.

The number most often associated with the idea is about 150, but the practical insight is broader: as groups grow, informal familiarity stops carrying the whole load. Past a certain size, people need clearer roles, rituals, norms, communication systems, and smaller subgroups.

## How It Works

Stable relationships are cognitively expensive. You have to remember who knows whom, who trusts whom, who owes what, who is in conflict, and what kind of contact each relationship needs. The larger the network, the more maintenance is required.

In organizations and communities, this means scale changes the social operating system. A small group can coordinate through memory and trust. A larger group needs structure.

## Recognition Cues

- A group grows and people start feeling anonymous.
- Informal communication stops reaching everyone who needs it.
- Trust becomes uneven because people no longer know each other directly.
- Culture splits into subgroups with different norms.
- Leaders expect "family" dynamics from a group that is too large for that style.

## Signature Phrasing

- "We used to know everyone."
- "It does not feel like the same community anymore."
- "People are falling through the cracks."
- "We need more process now."
- "I cannot keep track of all these relationships."

## Ask Yourself

> Is this problem caused by bad intent, or by a group that has outgrown informal trust and memory?

## Common False Positives

Dunbar's Number is often misused as if 150 were a precise universal limit. It is not. Relationship capacity varies by relationship depth, technology, culture, role, personality, time, and the kind of coordination required.

It also does not mean large communities cannot be healthy. It means they need nested structure and intentional maintenance.

## What Helps

Design for layers. Keep small teams, clear circles of responsibility, regular rituals, and visible norms inside the larger system. Do not rely on everyone knowing everyone.

For personal networks, distinguish close relationships, active collaborators, weak ties, and audience. Each layer needs different attention.

## Evidence Status

Dunbar's Number is influential and debated. The exact number is less important than the operational insight: social scale changes what people can track through memory, familiarity, and informal trust.
