# The Framing Effect

> Drawing different conclusions from the same information, depending on how that information is presented.

## Description

The Framing Effect is a cognitive bias where the way information is presented—the “frame”—influences our decisions more than the information itself. The same facts, framed differently, can lead to completely different conclusions. Think of a yogurt that is described as “90% fat-free” versus one that is described as “contains 10% fat.” The information is identical, but the “fat-free” frame feels much more positive and is more likely to lead to a purchase. The frame, not the fact, is what drives the decision.

## How It Works

This works psychologically because people rely on mental shortcuts and emotional responses triggered by framing, leading them to focus on how information is presented rather than its objective content.

## Signature Phrasing

- "It depends on how you look at it"
- "Framed that way, it seems better"
- "Different wording, different choice"
- "Focus on the presentation, not the data"

## Ask Yourself

> Is this information being presented differently to influence my decision?

## Applying This Knowledge

### Everyday

A doctor tells you that a surgery has a “90% survival rate.” That sounds reassuring. If another doctor tells you that the same surgery has a “10% mortality rate,” that sounds terrifying. The numbers are the same, but the emotional impact of the frame is completely different.

*When you are presented with a piece of information, especially a statistic, try to reframe it in the opposite way. If you’re told the success rate, ask for the failure rate. This simple act of reframing can reveal the emotional manipulation hidden in the initial frame.*

### At Work

A project manager can frame a project’s status in two ways. They can say, “We’ve completed 70% of the project,” which sounds great. Or they can say, “We still have 30% of the project left to go,” which might create a greater sense of urgency. The choice of frame depends on the motivation they are trying to create.

*Be aware that you are always framing information, whether you realize it or not. When you are communicating with your team, be intentional about the frame you are using. Are you trying to create a sense of optimism, urgency, or caution? Choose your frame to match your goal.*

### In Education

You can frame your learning goal in two ways. You can say, “If I study for an hour every day, I’ll pass the exam.” Or you can say, “If I *don’t* study for an hour every day, I’ll fail the exam.” The first is a “gain frame,” which is often better for motivating creative and exploratory behavior. The second is a “loss frame,” which is often better for motivating vigilance and avoiding mistakes.

*Experiment with different frames for your own goals. If you’re feeling unmotivated, try switching from a gain frame to a loss frame, or vice versa. See which one creates more energy for you.*
