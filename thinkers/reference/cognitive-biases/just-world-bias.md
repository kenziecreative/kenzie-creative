# Just-World Bias

> The tendency to believe that the world is a fair place where people get what they deserve, and deserve what they get.

## Description

Just-world bias is the tendency to believe that people generally get what they deserve and deserve what they get.

It makes outcomes feel morally legible. Success looks earned, suffering looks caused by personal failure, and random or systemic forces become easier to ignore.

## How It Works

Believing the world is fair reduces anxiety. If outcomes are deserved, then good choices should keep us safe.

That comfort has a cost. It can turn compassion into blame, privilege into proof of virtue, and misfortune into evidence of hidden wrongdoing. It can also make systems harder to see because individual deservingness becomes the default explanation.

## Recognition Cues

- You quickly search for what a suffering person did wrong.
- You assume successful people must be wiser, harder-working, or more deserving.
- You feel relief after finding a reason a bad outcome "could not happen to me."
- You underweight luck, timing, inherited advantage, discrimination, illness, or system design.
- You treat empathy as if it excuses bad choices.

## Signature Phrasing

- "They must have done something."
- "If they worked harder, they would be fine."
- "Everything happens for a reason."
- "They made their bed."
- "I earned everything I have."

## Ask Yourself

> What would I notice if I stopped assuming this outcome was deserved?

## Common False Positives

Personal responsibility is real. People's choices, habits, and character can affect outcomes.

The bias appears when responsibility becomes totalizing: when every outcome is explained by deservingness and external conditions disappear from the analysis.

## What Helps

- Separate causality from moral deservingness.
- Ask what role luck, timing, resources, constraints, and systems played.
- Imagine the same event happening to someone you love before judging it.
- Look for base rates and structural patterns, not only individual stories.
- Pair accountability with context: "What was within their control, and what was not?"

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-established as a motivational belief pattern, especially under threat, uncertainty, or exposure to undeserved suffering.
