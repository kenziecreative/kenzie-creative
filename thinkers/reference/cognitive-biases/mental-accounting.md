# Mental Accounting

> People tend to think about the world in terms of specific accounts, where value isn't interchangeable.

## Description

Mental accounting is the tendency to treat money, time, effort, attention, or resources differently depending on the mental category they are placed in.

The same dollar can feel different when labeled "bonus," "rent," "vacation," "company budget," or "found money." The same hour can feel different when labeled "work," "learning," "admin," or "wasted time."

## How It Works

Mental accounts reduce complexity and support self-control. Categories help people budget, protect priorities, and avoid constant recalculation.

The distortion appears when categories override fungibility and opportunity cost. Resources are treated as if they cannot move across accounts, even when reallocating them would create more value.

## Recognition Cues

- You treat windfalls as less real than earned money.
- You spend freely from one category while being overly strict in another.
- Budget ownership matters more than total organizational value.
- You ignore the best use of a resource because it is "for" something else.
- You justify a choice by category rather than by overall tradeoff.

## Signature Phrasing

- "That's my fun money"
- "This is a windfall"
- "It came from a different pot"
- "House money"
- "That budget cannot be used for this"

## Ask Yourself

> Would I make the same choice if this resource came from a different account or label?

## Common False Positives

Mental accounting can be useful. Budgets, envelopes, categories, and constraints can protect long-term priorities and reduce decision fatigue.

The bias appears when the category prevents better allocation or hides the true tradeoff.

## What Helps

- Translate categories back into total resources.
- Ask what the best use of the resource is now.
- Compare across accounts when the stakes are meaningful.
- Keep protective budgets where they serve a real purpose.
- Watch for "free money" language.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-supported in behavioral economics and consumer decision research.
