# Negativity Bias

> The tendency to give more weight to negative experiences or information than to positive ones, and to recall negative information more readily.

## Description

The Negativity Bias is our brain’s evolutionary tendency to be more sensitive to bad news than to good news. From a survival perspective, this makes sense: a missed opportunity for a meal is less consequential than a missed sign of a predator. Our brains are like Velcro for negative experiences and Teflon for positive ones. Think of it like a performance review. You might receive ten compliments and one piece of constructive criticism, but what do you dwell on for the rest of the day? The one negative comment. It sticks.

## How It Works

The brain prioritizes negative information as an evolutionary survival mechanism, making negative experiences more salient and memorable than positive ones.

## Signature Phrasing

- "Focuses on the negative"
- "Dwells on criticism"
- "Overweights bad news"
- "Neglects positive feedback"

## Ask Yourself

> Am I giving disproportionate weight to negative information compared to positive?

## Applying This Knowledge

### Everyday

You have a wonderful day, but a single rude comment from a stranger on your commute home can sour your entire evening. The one negative event has more emotional weight than all the positive events that preceded it.

*Practice gratitude. Deliberately taking the time to notice and appreciate the good things in your life is a conscious override of the negativity bias. It’s a way of training your brain to give more weight to the positive.*

### At Work

In a team meeting, one person points out a potential flaw in an otherwise promising idea. The team might then spend the rest of the meeting focused on that one flaw, losing sight of the idea’s potential. The negative information hijacks the conversation.

*When a negative point is raised, acknowledge it, and then consciously pivot the conversation back to the positives. A good framing is, “That’s a valid risk to consider. Now, let’s not lose sight of the potential upside here.”*

### In Education

When you’re learning a new skill, you are more likely to remember your mistakes and failures than your small wins and moments of progress. This can be discouraging and can make you want to quit, because your perception of your progress is skewed to the negative.

*Keep a “win log.” At the end of each learning session, write down one thing you did well or one small piece of progress you made. This creates a written record of the positive that you can look back on when you feel discouraged.*
