# Normalcy Bias

> The refusal to plan for, or react to, a disaster or a paradigm shift that has never happened before.

## Description

Normalcy bias is the tendency to underestimate the possibility or impact of disruption because the familiar world feels more real than a changed one.

It often appears as delayed reaction: the warning arrives, but the mind keeps modeling the future as if the current pattern will continue.

## How It Works

The mind uses recent stability as an anchor. If something has not happened before, or has not happened here, it feels less plausible.

Accepting disruption also has emotional and practical costs. It requires changing plans, spending resources, alarming people, or admitting vulnerability. Normalcy bias postpones those costs by treating the threat as unreal or exaggerated.

## Recognition Cues

- You dismiss warnings mainly because nothing like this has happened before.
- Preparation feels embarrassing, dramatic, or unnecessary.
- You wait for certainty even though delayed action raises risk.
- You assume systems will keep functioning because they usually do.
- You use past stability as proof of future safety.

## Signature Phrasing

- "It’s never happened before"
- "It won’t happen now"
- "Past stability guarantees future safety"
- "People are overreacting"
- "Let's wait and see"

## Ask Yourself

> What would I do now if I fully accepted that this disruption might be real?

## Common False Positives

Continuity is often a reasonable baseline. Most days are normal, and not every warning deserves maximal response.

The bias appears when the cost of being wrong is high and the main reason for inaction is that the disruption feels unfamiliar.

## What Helps

- Identify low-regret actions you can take before certainty arrives.
- Use scenario planning for plausible disruptions, not only likely ones.
- Predefine triggers for action.
- Ask what early signals would look like if the threat were real.
- Treat preparation as optionality, not panic.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: commonly observed in disaster preparedness, crisis response, and disruption contexts; the label is practical and descriptive rather than a single tightly bounded lab effect.
