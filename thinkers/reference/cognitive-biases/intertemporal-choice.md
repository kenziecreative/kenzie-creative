# Intertemporal Choice

> People lack empathy for their future-selves and make decisions based on current feelings.

## Description

Intertemporal choice is the problem of making decisions where costs and benefits occur at different points in time.

In everyday terms, it is the negotiation between present you and future you. Present feelings are vivid; future consequences are abstract.

## How It Works

People often have weak emotional connection to their future selves. Future needs can feel like someone else's problem.

This makes it easier to borrow from the future: delaying maintenance, taking on debt, skipping rest, accumulating technical debt, or choosing short-term relief over long-term resilience.

## Recognition Cues

- You repeatedly leave burdens for "future me."
- Current discomfort dominates future consequences.
- Long-term benefits sound good but fail to change near-term behavior.
- You make decisions that future you predictably resents.
- You talk about future capacity as if it will be larger than current capacity.

## Signature Phrasing

- "Future me will deal with it"
- "I'll worry about that tomorrow"
- "YOLO"
- "That's a problem for future me"
- "We can clean it up later"

## Ask Yourself

> What obligation am I creating for my future self, and would they consent to it?

## Common False Positives

Prioritizing the present can be reasonable. Urgent needs, scarce resources, uncertainty, and meaningful enjoyment matter.

The bias appears when the present self repeatedly spends future resources without acknowledging the future cost.

## What Helps

- Make future consequences concrete: dates, amounts, people, and maintenance burdens.
- Create commitments that protect future priorities.
- Give future stakeholders a voice in the decision.
- Convert long-term goals into near-term actions.
- Ask what pattern this choice creates if repeated.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: broad, well-established decision domain. Related mechanisms include temporal discounting, self-continuity, affective forecasting, and present bias.
