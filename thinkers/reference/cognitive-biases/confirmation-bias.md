# Confirmation Bias

> The tendency to notice, seek, interpret, and remember information that supports what one already believes or wants to believe.

## Description

Confirmation bias is the mind's tendency to notice, seek, interpret, and remember information that supports what it already believes or wants to believe.

It does not mean people only see confirming evidence. It means confirming evidence feels more natural, more relevant, more trustworthy, and easier to explain. Disconfirming evidence feels suspect, exceptional, biased, incomplete, or beside the point.

The bias is especially strong when the belief is tied to identity, belonging, moral worth, money, status, or a decision already made. In those cases, changing your mind can feel less like updating a map and more like losing part of yourself.

## How It Works

Confirmation bias works through several small filters that compound:

1. **Search filter.** You look where confirming evidence is easiest to find.
2. **Attention filter.** You notice supportive details faster than inconvenient ones.
3. **Interpretation filter.** Ambiguous evidence gets read in the direction you already lean.
4. **Memory filter.** Confirming examples are easier to recall later.
5. **Social filter.** People around you may reward agreement and punish doubt.

No single filter has to be dramatic. Together they create a world that keeps agreeing with you.

## Recognition Cues

- You can easily name evidence for your belief but struggle to name the strongest evidence against it.
- Sources that agree with you feel "obvious" while sources that disagree feel biased before you examine them.
- You keep raising the standard of proof for opposing evidence.
- You explain away every counterexample as unusual, corrupt, irrelevant, or misunderstood.
- People who disagree with you all seem stupid, malicious, naive, or bought off.

## Signature Phrasing

- "That source is obviously biased." *(before checking the claim)*
- "That doesn't count."
- "I knew it."
- "Everyone I trust agrees."
- "The evidence against this is just propaganda."
- "There must be another explanation." *(only when the evidence challenges your side)*

## Ask Yourself

> What evidence would convince me that my current belief is wrong?

## Common False Positives

Having a belief is not confirmation bias. Trusting better evidence over weaker evidence is not confirmation bias. Rejecting a bad argument from the other side is not confirmation bias.

The warning sign is asymmetric standards: accepting weak evidence when it supports you and demanding perfect evidence when it challenges you.

## What Helps

- Name the strongest counterargument before defending your view.
- Search for disconfirming evidence on purpose.
- Ask what you would think if the same evidence supported the opposite side.
- Separate identity from belief: "I was wrong about this" is not "I am bad."
- Use a decision journal so later you can see what you believed before the outcome was known.
- Invite a critic who understands your view well enough to challenge the real version, not a caricature.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: robust as a broad phenomenon. The exact mechanisms vary by domain, motivation, identity threat, and information environment.
