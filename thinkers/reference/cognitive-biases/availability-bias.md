# Availability Bias

> The tendency to overestimate the likelihood of events that are more easily recalled in memory, often because they are recent or emotionally charged.

## Description

Availability bias is the tendency to treat ease of recall as evidence of frequency, likelihood, or importance.

Events that are recent, vivid, emotional, repeated, personal, or heavily covered are easier to retrieve from memory. Because they come to mind quickly, they feel more common or more diagnostic than they may be.

## How It Works

The mind uses memory fluency as a shortcut. If examples appear quickly, the judgment feels supported.

This shortcut often works in ordinary life; common things usually are easier to remember. But media exposure, emotional intensity, novelty, and recency can make rare things feel common and common things feel invisible.

## Recognition Cues

- A vivid example weighs more than a dry statistic.
- You can easily remember cases but do not know the base rate.
- Recent events dominate your sense of risk.
- Your confidence rises because examples come to mind quickly.
- You mistake media visibility for real-world frequency.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I keep hearing about this."
- "It seems to happen all the time."
- "I can think of three examples right now."
- "After what happened last week, we have to assume..."
- "This risk feels everywhere."

## Ask Yourself

> Am I estimating likelihood from memory vividness or from base rates?

## Common False Positives

Availability is not useless. Personal experience, recent incidents, and vivid cases can signal real changes or risks worth investigating.

The bias appears when ease of recall is treated as enough. A vivid example should trigger measurement, not replace it.

## What Helps

- Ask for base rates, denominators, and comparison classes.
- Check whether your examples are recent, emotional, or media-amplified.
- Look for boring evidence, not only memorable stories.
- Keep logs where memory is likely to distort reviews or risk judgments.
- Ask what important evidence is not vivid enough to remember.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: robust as a memory-based judgment shortcut, especially in risk perception, media effects, and frequency estimation.
