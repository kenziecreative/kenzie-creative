# Confabulation

> The creation of false memories, perceptions, or beliefs about the self or the world, without the conscious intention to deceive.

## Description

Confabulation is the sincere creation of false or distorted memories, explanations, perceptions, or beliefs without conscious intent to deceive.

It is not lying. The person experiences the constructed account as real or plausible. The mind fills gaps in memory or causality with a coherent story.

## How It Works

The mind dislikes gaps. When details are missing, it draws on expectations, associations, identity, and surrounding context to complete the story.

The resulting account can feel vivid because confidence is not the same as accuracy. Fluency, coherence, and emotional fit can make a constructed memory feel true.

## Recognition Cues

- A memory contains plausible details that lack a clear source.
- The story becomes more coherent over time rather than more carefully qualified.
- Confidence is high despite weak records.
- The explanation protects identity or fills an uncomfortable gap.
- Details match stereotypes or expectations more than evidence.

## Signature Phrasing

- "Memory feels 100% real"
- "Filling in the gaps"
- "Brain abhors a vacuum"
- "Composite of reality and invention"
- "I can picture it clearly"

## Ask Yourself

> What evidence do I have to verify this memory, and could any details be reconstructed or inaccurate?

## Common False Positives

Being wrong is not automatically confabulation. People can misremember, infer, simplify, exaggerate, or lie for different reasons.

The key features are sincerity, gap-filling, and lack of intent to deceive.

## What Helps

- Treat confidence as separate from verification.
- Ask, "How do I know this detail?"
- Use records for important decisions and retrospectives.
- Allow people to revise memory without shame.
- Distinguish facts, inferences, and story.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-established in memory and neuropsychology, with everyday forms overlapping with normal reconstructive memory and narrative sense-making.
