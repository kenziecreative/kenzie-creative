# Decision Regret

> The feeling of regret after making a decision, often leading to rationalization or a change in future choices.

## Description

Decision regret is the distress that arises when you believe a different choice would have produced a better outcome.

Regret can teach. It can also distort. After the outcome is known, the rejected option may look better, the chosen option may look foolish, and uncertainty at the time may disappear from memory.

## How It Works

Regret compares reality with an imagined alternative. That alternative is often incomplete: it includes the benefits you missed but not the costs, risks, and uncertainties that would have come with it.

The stronger the identity stake, public visibility, reversibility, or sense of personal responsibility, the stronger regret tends to feel.

## Recognition Cues

- You replay the decision with perfect hindsight.
- The unchosen option becomes idealized.
- You confuse a bad outcome with a bad decision process.
- You become overly cautious after one painful choice.
- You rationalize the choice to avoid feeling regret at all.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I should have..."
- "If only I had..."
- "Buyer's remorse"
- "What was I thinking?"
- "I knew I should have chosen differently"

## Ask Yourself

> Was the decision poor given what I knew then, or does it only look poor because I know the outcome now?

## Common False Positives

Regret is not automatically bias. It can reveal a real mistake, a value conflict, or a lesson worth carrying forward.

The bias appears when regret turns outcome knowledge into self-punishment or when it idealizes the path not taken.

## What Helps

- Reconstruct what you knew and believed before the outcome.
- Separate decision quality from outcome quality.
- Ask what the unchosen option could realistically have cost.
- Turn the lesson into a rule, checklist, or future trigger.
- Let regret inform repair without becoming permanent self-indictment.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-established as a counterfactual emotion; its decision effects vary depending on responsibility, reversibility, and hindsight.
