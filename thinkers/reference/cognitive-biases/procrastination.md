# Procrastination

> The act of delaying or postponing tasks, often despite knowing there will be negative consequences.

## Description

Procrastination is delaying an intended task despite expecting that the delay will create future costs.

It is often less about laziness than emotional regulation. The task creates discomfort, and avoidance provides immediate relief.

## How It Works

The present self gets relief now; the future self inherits the cost later. Because future discomfort feels abstract, avoidance can win even when the person knows the delay is harmful.

Procrastination can be driven by anxiety, ambiguity, perfectionism, boredom, resentment, identity threat, depleted attention, or lack of a clear next action.

## Recognition Cues

- You know the delay will hurt but still avoid starting.
- You do lower-priority tasks to feel productive.
- The task feels larger when you think about it than when you touch the first step.
- You wait for the right mood or pressure.
- Avoidance reduces discomfort briefly, then increases it later.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I'll do it later"
- "Just one more episode"
- "I work better under pressure"
- "I'll feel better if I avoid it now"
- "I need to be in the right headspace"

## Ask Yourself

> What will my future self gain or lose based on the choice I make right now?

## Common False Positives

Strategic delay is not procrastination. Waiting can be wise when more information is coming, timing matters, or rest is genuinely needed.

The bias appears when delay is chosen for short-term emotional relief while knowingly increasing future cost.

## What Helps

- Define the next physical action, not the whole task.
- Make starting smaller than avoiding.
- Use implementation intentions: when, where, and for how long.
- Reduce ambiguity before demanding motivation.
- Treat discomfort as the first step, not a signal to wait.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-supported as a self-regulation and temporal-choice problem, with multiple causes rather than one single mechanism.
