# Choice Bracketing

> People tend to draw boundaries around the amount of information they consider when making a decision.

## Description

Choice bracketing is the way people draw boundaries around which choices are considered together.

Narrow bracketing evaluates a choice in isolation. Broad bracketing evaluates it as part of a pattern, portfolio, sequence, or larger life/system decision.

## How It Works

Narrow brackets reduce cognitive load. "Should I buy this today?" is easier than "What happens if I make this kind of purchase every week for a year?"

The cost is that repeated small choices, interacting decisions, and portfolio effects disappear. A decision that looks harmless alone may be costly in aggregate.

## Recognition Cues

- You justify something because "it is just this once."
- Small choices are approved one at a time without seeing the total.
- Related decisions are owned by separate people or budgets.
- You compare options inside a narrow frame that excludes better alternatives.
- Patterns are noticed only after they become expensive.

## Signature Phrasing

- "It's just one time"
- "What's another few dollars?"
- "Let's deal with that later"
- "In isolation, it's fine"
- "This decision is separate"

## Ask Yourself

> What does this choice look like when repeated, combined, or viewed as part of the larger system?

## Common False Positives

Narrow bracketing can be useful for small, independent decisions where the cost of analysis exceeds the value of optimization.

The bias appears when the bracket hides cumulative impact, interaction effects, or better alternatives.

## What Helps

- Zoom out to weekly, yearly, portfolio, or lifecycle impact.
- Group repeated choices before judging them.
- Ask what category of decision this belongs to.
- Track totals automatically where repeated choices matter.
- Use broad bracketing for strategy and narrow bracketing for execution details.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: supported in decision research on narrow versus broad framing, savings, risk, and repeated choices.
