# Self-Serving Bias

> The tendency to attribute our successes to our own character or abilities, but our failures to external factors beyond our control.

## Description

Self-serving bias is the tendency to explain successes as evidence of your own ability, character, or effort while explaining failures as the result of circumstances, bad luck, unfairness, or other people.

It protects self-esteem, but it can also block learning. If success is always "me" and failure is always "not me," feedback becomes distorted in both directions.

## How It Works

Outcomes are ambiguous. Most successes and failures contain some mix of skill, effort, timing, help, constraints, randomness, and system design.

Self-serving bias sorts that ambiguity in a flattering direction. The mind claims the parts that feel identity-enhancing and externalizes the parts that feel threatening.

## Recognition Cues

- You describe wins in terms of your qualities but losses in terms of obstacles.
- Your postmortems change standards depending on whether the outcome made you look good.
- You resent feedback after failure but enjoy praise after success.
- You notice other people's excuses more easily than your own.
- You rarely update your process after a bad result.

## Signature Phrasing

- "I earned this."
- "They set me up to fail."
- "The market was impossible."
- "That win shows what I can do."
- "The loss does not really say anything about me."

## Ask Yourself

> What mix of my choices, other people's choices, constraints, and luck produced this outcome?

## Common False Positives

Sometimes you really did create the success, and sometimes failure really was mostly outside your control. The goal is not false humility or self-blame.

The warning sign is asymmetry: using internal explanations for good outcomes and external explanations for bad outcomes without applying the same standard both ways.

## What Helps

- Use the same postmortem questions after wins and losses.
- Name at least one external factor in a success and one personal contribution to a failure.
- Ask a trusted outside observer what part of the outcome you are overclaiming or underclaiming.
- Track predictions and decisions before outcomes are known.
- Treat accountability as information, not humiliation.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-supported as an attribution pattern, with variation across culture, domain, self-esteem, accountability norms, and whether outcomes are public.
