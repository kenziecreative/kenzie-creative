# The Overjustification Effect

> The tendency for external rewards to undermine intrinsic motivation.

## Description

The overjustification effect is the tendency for external rewards to reduce intrinsic motivation for an activity that someone already found interesting or meaningful.

The issue is not that rewards are bad. The issue is that rewards can change the perceived reason for acting: "I do this because I like it" becomes "I do this because I get paid, praised, graded, or tracked."

## How It Works

Rewards provide an explanation for behavior. If the reward becomes the most visible reason, the person may infer that the activity itself is less worth doing.

Controlling, expected, or performance-contingent rewards are more likely to crowd out intrinsic motivation than informational rewards that support competence and autonomy.

## Recognition Cues

- A previously enjoyable activity becomes a chore after rewards are added.
- People optimize for the metric instead of the meaning.
- Motivation drops when the reward stops.
- Creative or exploratory behavior narrows toward rewarded outputs.
- The reward communicates control rather than appreciation.

## Signature Phrasing

- "Rewarding someone for something they already enjoy"
- "Motivation attributed to external rewards"
- "Decrease in intrinsic interest after reward removal"
- "External rewards undermine internal motivation"
- "Now it feels like work"

## Ask Yourself

> Are you valuing the activity for its own sake or just for the reward it brings?

## Common False Positives

External rewards can be useful, especially for boring, necessary, effortful, or under-valued work. Fair pay also protects dignity and motivation.

The bias is most relevant when the activity already had intrinsic motivation and the reward feels controlling or replaces the original reason.

## What Helps

- Use rewards to acknowledge value, not control every behavior.
- Preserve autonomy, mastery, and purpose.
- Avoid turning every intrinsically meaningful activity into a metric game.
- Reward quality and learning, not only volume.
- Ask what behavior the reward will teach people to optimize.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: supported, especially in motivation research around intrinsic motivation, reward type, autonomy, and task interest. Effects are context-sensitive.
