# Narrative Bias

> The tendency to interpret information as part of a larger story or pattern, even when the events are unconnected.

## Description

Narrative Bias is our brain's insatiable hunger for stories. We are not wired to process raw data; we are wired to process narratives. So we take the random, chaotic events of the world and we weave them into coherent stories with beginnings, middles, and ends, with heroes and villains, with causes and effects. This makes the world feel more understandable and predictable, but it also leads us to see patterns and connections that don't exist. Think of how we talk about the stock market. "The market fell today because of fears about inflation." In reality, millions of individual decisions were made for millions of individual reasons. But we can't process that complexity, so we create a simple narrative.

## How It Works

This bias works because humans are naturally inclined to find meaning and coherence in random events, simplifying complexity by constructing causal stories that feel more manageable and predictable.

## Signature Phrasing

- "The market fell today because of..."
- "There must be a reason behind this"
- "It all makes sense when you see the pattern"
- "This explains everything"

## Ask Yourself

> What alternative explanations or random factors might also explain this outcome?

## Applying This Knowledge

### Everyday

You look back on your career and you see a clear, logical progression—each job leading naturally to the next. In reality, it was probably a series of accidents, lucky breaks, and random opportunities. But the narrative makes it feel like it was all part of a plan.

*Be wary of the stories you tell about your own life. They are useful for making sense of the past, but they can also blind you to the role of luck and randomness. Don't mistake a good story for a good explanation.*

### At Work

A company's success is often attributed to a brilliant strategy or a visionary leader. In reality, it might have been the result of being in the right place at the right time, or a competitor making a mistake. But the narrative of the "genius CEO" is more satisfying than the narrative of "we got lucky."

*When analyzing success or failure, resist the urge to create a simple narrative. Look for the systemic factors, the luck, and the randomness that the story leaves out. The truth is usually more complex and less heroic than the narrative.*

### In Education

When you read a biography of a successful person, you're reading a narrative that has been carefully constructed to make their success seem inevitable. The failures, the dead ends, and the lucky breaks are often minimized or left out entirely.

*Read biographies with a critical eye. Ask: "What is this narrative leaving out? What role did luck play? What would have happened if one small thing had been different?" The goal is to learn from the real story, not the polished myth.*
