# The Illusion of Transparency

> The tendency to overestimate the degree to which our internal states are known by others.

## Description

The illusion of transparency is the tendency to overestimate how visible your internal thoughts, feelings, intentions, nervousness, confusion, or deception are to other people.

From the inside, your state feels loud. From the outside, others often see only a small fraction of it.

## How It Works

People anchor on their own intense inner experience and insufficiently adjust for the fact that others do not have access to that experience.

Others are also busy managing their own thoughts and impressions. They notice less than you imagine, and they interpret what they notice less precisely than you fear.

## Recognition Cues

- You assume everyone can tell you are nervous or confused.
- You overestimate how clearly your intentions came across.
- You think a small mistake revealed your whole inner state.
- You expect others to know what you meant without saying it.
- You avoid speaking because you feel already exposed.

## Signature Phrasing

- "Everyone can see how nervous I am"
- "My face must be giving me away"
- "They know exactly what I'm thinking"
- "I can't hide my emotions"
- "It should be obvious how I feel"

## Ask Yourself

> Is it possible that others are not as aware of my feelings or thoughts as I assume?

## Common False Positives

Sometimes internal states are visible. Voice, posture, facial expression, and behavior can communicate more than words.

The bias appears when the felt intensity of the inner state is treated as proof that others see it clearly.

## What Helps

- Ask for feedback instead of guessing what others noticed.
- State important intentions explicitly.
- Remember that others have their own spotlight effect.
- Treat nervousness as less visible than it feels.
- Use repair language when clarity matters: "What I meant was..."

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-supported in social cognition research on egocentric anchoring and perceived observability of internal states.
