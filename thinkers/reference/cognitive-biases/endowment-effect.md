# Endowment Effect

> People value things more highly once they own them.

## Description

The endowment effect is the tendency to value something more highly because you own it, built it, chose it, or feel attached to it.

The same object, project, idea, or role may seem more valuable once it is "mine." Selling, changing, or giving it up then feels like a loss rather than a neutral exchange.

## How It Works

Ownership changes the reference point. Before ownership, the question is "What is this worth to me?" After ownership, the question becomes "What would I lose by giving this up?"

Emotional attachment, effort, identity, and loss aversion all inflate perceived value.

## Recognition Cues

- You demand more to give something up than you would pay to acquire it.
- A project feels better because your team built it.
- You treat "mine" as evidence of higher quality.
- Letting go feels disloyal or wasteful.
- Outside evaluations seem too low because they do not include your attachment.

## Signature Phrasing

- "This has sentimental value"
- "It's not for sale"
- "I built this"
- "The IKEA effect"
- "They do not understand what this is worth"

## Ask Yourself

> If I did not already own or identify with this, how much would I value it?

## Common False Positives

Ownership can create real value. Customization, learning, history, trust, and switching costs may make something genuinely more useful to the owner than to others.

The bias appears when attachment is mistaken for transferable value or when ownership blocks comparison with better alternatives.

## What Helps

- Ask what you would pay to acquire it today.
- Get an outside valuation from someone without attachment.
- Separate sentimental value from market value or strategic value.
- Define exit criteria before ownership deepens.
- Compare the thing to the best alternative use of the same resources.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-supported in behavioral economics and valuation research, especially for owned goods and self-created work.
