# The Illusion of External Agency

> The tendency to perceive intentional action or design in random or natural events.

## Description

The illusion of external agency is the tendency to perceive intentional action, design, or hidden authorship behind events that may be random, emergent, accidental, or systemic.

It asks "who did this?" before establishing that there was a "who."

## How It Works

Detecting agents is useful. In social life, many important events are caused by people with motives.

But agency detection can overfire, especially under threat, uncertainty, surprise, or loss. The mind prefers an intentional cause to a messy system, coincidence, or randomness.

## Recognition Cues

- You assume a hidden actor before checking non-agentic causes.
- Random events feel targeted or meaningful.
- A system failure is reduced to a villain story.
- "Someone must have wanted this" appears before evidence.
- Coincidence feels like intent.

## Signature Phrasing

- "There must be someone behind this"
- "Something caused this on purpose"
- "It can’t be random or accidental"
- "This was designed to happen"
- "Who benefits?"

## Ask Yourself

> Is there evidence that this event could have happened without an intentional agent?

## Common False Positives

Sometimes there is an agent. Intentional action, coordination, manipulation, negligence, and design are real.

The bias appears when agency is assumed before comparing chance, system dynamics, incentives, incompetence, and ordinary error.

## What Helps

- Generate non-agentic explanations first: chance, system design, constraints, error, feedback loops.
- Ask what evidence would distinguish intent from accident.
- Separate harm from intent; something can harm you without being aimed at you.
- Look for mechanisms, not only motives.
- Use blameless analysis before blame assignment.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: supported by work on agency detection, attribution, superstition, and conspiracy cognition; expression varies widely by context and stakes.
