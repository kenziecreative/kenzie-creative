# Anchoring Effect

> The tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information offered (the “anchor”) when making decisions.

## Description

The anchoring effect is the tendency for an initial number, frame, estimate, offer, or reference point to pull later judgments toward it.

The anchor can be relevant, irrelevant, arbitrary, strategic, or accidental. Once it is present, people often adjust away from it too little.

## How It Works

Anchors create a comparison point. Later information is interpreted relative to that starting point rather than from scratch.

Adjustment takes mental effort, and people often stop once the new number feels plausible. This is why first offers, initial timelines, sticker prices, forecasts, and early labels can shape the entire conversation.

## Recognition Cues

- A first number keeps appearing in later discussion even after better data arrives.
- People negotiate from an opening offer rather than an independent range.
- A rough estimate becomes a commitment.
- The first diagnosis, label, or price makes alternatives feel high or low.
- You ask "compared to what?" only after the anchor has already set the frame.

## Signature Phrasing

- "The first estimate was..."
- "Compared to that, this seems reasonable."
- "We started at..."
- "It should only take a couple of weeks."
- "The list price is..."

## Ask Yourself

> What would my estimate be if I had never seen the first number or frame?

## Common False Positives

Starting points are necessary. Some anchors are valid because they come from market data, historical baselines, constraints, or prior agreements.

The bias appears when the anchor has more influence than its evidentiary value deserves.

## What Helps

- Build an independent estimate before seeing or accepting an anchor.
- Use ranges instead of single-point estimates where uncertainty is high.
- Ask where the anchor came from and whether it should count.
- Re-anchor with base rates, comparables, or decomposition.
- In groups, have people estimate privately before sharing numbers.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: robust across judgment, negotiation, pricing, estimation, and forecasting tasks.
