# Optimism Bias

> The tendency to be overly optimistic, overestimating the likelihood of positive outcomes and underestimating the likelihood of negative ones.

## Description

Optimism bias is the tendency to overestimate the likelihood of positive outcomes and underestimate the likelihood, cost, or duration of negative outcomes.

It often sounds like "it will work out" or "that will not happen to us." Optimism can motivate action, but it becomes distorting when confidence outruns evidence and contingency planning.

## How It Works

Optimism reduces anxiety and protects momentum. It helps people start hard things, tolerate uncertainty, and keep going through setbacks.

The cost is selective risk perception. People imagine success in detail but leave failure vague. They treat obstacles as exceptions, underestimate timelines, and assume their case will beat the base rate.

## Recognition Cues

- You can describe the successful path clearly but struggle to describe failure modes.
- You assume risks apply to others more than to you.
- Plans depend on best-case timing, cooperation, or execution.
- Contingency planning feels pessimistic or disloyal.
- You use confidence as evidence that the plan is sound.

## Signature Phrasing

- "It won’t happen to me"
- "I’m the exception"
- "I’m less at risk"
- "Things always work out for me"
- "We will figure it out"

## Ask Yourself

> What base rate or outside-view evidence justifies my confidence?

## Common False Positives

Optimism is not automatically bias. Hope, ambition, and confidence can be useful when paired with evidence, skill, resources, and adaptation.

The bias appears when optimism prevents risk assessment, learning, resourcing, or fallback planning.

## What Helps

- Use the outside view: compare against similar people, projects, or attempts.
- Run a premortem: imagine failure and explain how it happened.
- Set trigger points for when to reassess.
- Ask what must be true for the optimistic path to work.
- Fund the backup plan before you need it.

## Evidence Status

Evidence status: well-supported in risk perception and planning, though optimism can be adaptive depending on domain, stakes, and feedback loops.
