# Diagnosis Bias

> The tendency to lock onto an early label for a person or situation and then interpret later evidence through that label.

## Description

Diagnosis bias happens when an initial assessment becomes a filter. Once someone is labeled "difficult," "brilliant," "not strategic," "high potential," "dramatic," or "not a culture fit," later behavior is interpreted to confirm the diagnosis. Evidence that fits is remembered. Evidence that complicates the label is minimized or explained away.

This is especially dangerous because labels feel like understanding. A label compresses complexity into something manageable, but it can also stop inquiry. The person is no longer being observed. They are being sorted.

## How It Works

Diagnosis bias combines anchoring, confirmation bias, and social attribution. The first impression supplies the anchor. Later perception searches for confirmation. Social systems then repeat the label, making it feel more objective than it is.

Once a label becomes shared, it can become self-fulfilling. People who are treated as unreliable, threatening, gifted, or fragile may begin to receive different opportunities, scrutiny, patience, and feedback.

## Recognition Cues

- A person is discussed through a fixed label more often than through recent behavior.
- New evidence is treated as "typical" when negative and "an exception" when positive.
- The same action is interpreted differently depending on who did it.
- Feedback sounds like identity judgment rather than behavior description.
- The label survives long after the original evidence has faded.

## Signature Phrasing

- "That's just who they are."
- "I knew it from the start."
- "They are not a serious person."
- "Classic them."
- "They have always been the problem."

## Ask Yourself

> What evidence would make me update this label, and am I actually looking for it?

## Common False Positives

Not every label is unfair. Pattern recognition is useful. Some people repeatedly behave in ways that justify caution, trust, promotion, or boundaries. The risk is when the label becomes immune to new evidence.

A working hypothesis should stay revisable. A diagnosis bias turns the hypothesis into a verdict.

## What Helps

Convert labels back into observable claims. Instead of "she is defensive," write "in the last two reviews, she interrupted before the feedback was complete." Instead of "he is high potential," write what outcomes, judgment, or learning speed support that claim.

Build review points into important judgments. Hiring, performance, conflict, and trust decisions benefit from asking: "What have we learned since the first impression?"

## Evidence Status

The specific phrase "diagnosis bias" is commonly used in applied psychology, management, and clinical reasoning rather than as one narrow experimental effect. Its components - anchoring, confirmation bias, labeling, and attribution - are well-supported.
