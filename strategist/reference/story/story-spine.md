# Story Spine

> A six-sentence narrative skeleton — from stable world to disruption to resolution — that structures any business story in the same arc that makes fictional stories compelling.

## What It Is

The Story Spine is a narrative template built from six sentence-starters that force a complete story arc. Each sentence-starter corresponds to a structural beat:

1. **Once upon a time…** — the stable world before the problem. (Beginning: context.)
2. **Every day…** — the normal pattern of life in that world. (Beginning: ongoing context.)
3. **But one day…** — the disruption that breaks the pattern. (Event: the inciting incident.)
4. **Because of that…** — the first consequence of the disruption. (Middle: response and action.)
5. **Because of that…** — the next consequence; the causal chain continues. The "Because of that" beat can repeat as many times as needed to walk through the sequence of strategic moves. (Middle: continued.)
6. **Until finally…** — the resolution: the goal achieved, the new state reached. (Climax: endpoint.)

The spine can carry an optional seventh beat — **And ever since then…** — to describe the steady state after the resolution.

## Why It Works

Humans process information more readily when it arrives as a story than as a list of facts, because stories are how the brain encodes cause and effect. The Story Spine works for strategy communication because it uses the same structure — stable world, disruption, response, resolution — that underlies every compelling narrative from folk tales to case studies.

The key mechanism is the **causal chain**. Each "Because of that" beat is not just "next, we did X" but "because the previous thing happened, we did X." This forces the presenter to articulate why each action follows from the prior situation, which is exactly the logic of strategy. A strategy that cannot be told as a causal chain — where actions follow from each other rather than being listed arbitrarily — is probably not a coherent strategy.

The opening beats also do important work: "Once upon a time" and "Every day" establish the baseline so that "But one day" lands with appropriate weight. The disruption is only dramatic if the audience first understands what it disrupted.

## How To Use It

1. **Write "Once upon a time…"** Describe the business, product, or team in its stable state. Who is involved, what does the world look like, what is normal? Keep it grounded and factual — this is context, not marketing.
2. **Write "Every day…"** Describe the recurring pattern that defines this world — what the business does routinely, how it grows, what its customers expect.
3. **Write "But one day…"** Introduce the disruption: a market shift, a data signal, a customer behaviour change, a competitive move. Be specific. This is the inciting incident that makes the strategy necessary.
4. **Write one or more "Because of that…" beats.** Each one describes an action or strategic decision made in response to the previous beat. Chain them causally: "because of the disruption, we did X; because we did X, Y happened; because Y happened, we did Z."
5. **Write "Until finally…"** Describe the resolution — the goal achieved, the metric hit, the new state reached. Be concrete: a number, a milestone, a measurable outcome.
6. **Optionally write "And ever since then…"** Describe the sustained new normal if it adds useful context about the lasting impact.

## Worked Example

Acme Design wants to communicate the story of a strategic pivot to a live instructor-led offering, structured as a case study for a potential partnership deck:

**Once upon a time,** Acme Design offered a subscription library of on-demand graphic-design courses, taught by industry veterans, to a growing base of self-directed learners.

**Every day,** thousands of subscribers completed modules at their own pace, with new content released monthly and the library expanding steadily across illustration, typography, motion design, and brand identity.

**But one day,** net subscriber growth turned negative. Exit surveys revealed that 41% of churned subscribers had left not because of content quality but because they lacked structure and accountability — they were self-directing themselves out the door.

**Because of that,** Acme piloted a live cohort format with 30 subscribers in Q2 — eight weeks of weekly live sessions with a senior instructor, structured assignments, and peer review.

**Because of that,** completion rates in the live cohort reached 84%, compared to 31% for equivalent self-paced modules, and 90-day churn in the cohort group dropped to 4% against a platform-wide rate of 7.5%.

**Until finally,** Acme launched a live instructor-led tier at $149/month in Q3, returning net subscriber growth to positive within six weeks and increasing average revenue per user by 38%.

**And ever since then,** the live tier has accounted for 22% of total revenue while representing only 14% of subscribers — and the accountability model has become the core of Acme's brand positioning in a crowded market.

## When To Use It

The Story Spine works best for **retrospective narratives** — telling the story of what has already happened: a case study, a product launch post-mortem, a lessons-learned presentation, a brand story. It is particularly strong in pitches and partnership decks where you want to demonstrate that you have navigated real challenges and arrived somewhere credible.

For **forward-looking strategy** — a plan that hasn't happened yet — the spine still works, but the Climax needs to be stated explicitly near the beginning rather than reserved for the end, so the audience knows what success looks like before following you through the causal chain. In a forward-looking context, consider opening with the resolution and then using the spine structure to explain how you will get there. For a tighter fit with forward-looking arguments, the [5 ACT](./5-act.md) framework is designed specifically for that direction.

Use the Story Spine rather than SCQA when the communication is external-facing (a pitch, a case study, a brand narrative) or when the emotional arc of the journey matters as much as the logic of the recommendation.

## Things To Watch Out For

- "Because of that" must be genuinely causal — not just temporal. "Because of that, we also decided to refresh the brand" is not a causal consequence of the previous beat; it is a separate decision being squeezed in. Either connect it causally or leave it out.
- The beginning beats ("Once upon a time" / "Every day") are often written too long. Their job is to establish context quickly, not to document history. Two to three sentences each is usually enough.
- A spine where the Climax is vague ("things improved") is not a complete story. The resolution must be concrete enough that the audience knows whether the strategy succeeded. A number or a named outcome is almost always better than a general description.
- The spine implies a single protagonist and a single causal chain. Complex strategies with parallel workstreams may need multiple spines or a different structure (the [5 ACT](./5-act.md) framework handles multi-stream narratives somewhat better).
