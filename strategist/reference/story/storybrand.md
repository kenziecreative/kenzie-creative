# StoryBrand

> A seven-part framework that structures any brand or marketing message around a simple story: the customer is the hero, your brand is the guide, and the plot is the customer's journey from problem to success.

## What It Is

StoryBrand is a brand messaging framework built on the logic of the hero's journey. It holds that the most common failure in marketing communication is positioning the brand as the hero — talking about the company's capabilities, history, and achievements — when customers only pay attention to stories in which *they* are the hero. The framework provides seven narrative elements that, taken together, produce a clear and compelling brand message:

1. **A Character:** The customer — their identity, their aspirations, their desires.
2. **Has A Problem:** The pain point the customer is experiencing — at three levels: external (the practical problem), internal (how it makes them feel), and philosophical (why it is unjust or wrong that they face this).
3. **And Meets A Guide:** Your brand — not as a hero, but as the experienced mentor who has solved this problem before and can show the customer how. The guide demonstrates empathy (understanding the customer's problem) and authority (credibility to help).
4. **Who Gives Them A Plan:** A clear, simple set of steps that shows the customer what to do — the path from where they are to where they want to be. Three steps is the standard; more than four makes the plan feel overwhelming.
5. **And Calls Them To Action:** A direct, explicit invitation to act — both a direct call to action (buy now, start a trial, book a call) and a transitional call to action (download a guide, watch a demo) for customers who are not yet ready to commit.
6. **That Ends In Success:** A vivid description of the customer's life after the problem is solved. Specific, aspirational, and grounded in outcomes the customer actually wants.
7. **And Helps Them Avoid Failure:** The cost of not acting — what the customer loses or continues to suffer if they do not take the path the guide offers. This is not a threat; it is a reminder of what is genuinely at stake.

## Why It Works

Most brand messaging fails not because it is inaccurate, but because it is organised around the wrong protagonist. Companies naturally want to talk about themselves — their story, their innovation, their awards — but no customer wakes up interested in a company's story. Customers wake up interested in their own story, their own problem, their own possibility of a better life. StoryBrand works because it reorganises the entire message around the customer's story, with the brand playing the role that makes the hero's success possible.

The guide-not-hero distinction is the core insight. A guide's role in any story is to give the hero what they need to succeed — wisdom, tools, a plan, encouragement — without upstaging them. The guide's credibility comes not from boasting but from a demonstrated track record of having been here before. Yoda does not talk about how good he is at the Force; he shows Luke what is possible and then gets out of the way.

The three-level problem structure in element 2 is also important. Most brands address only the external problem (we fix X). But customers experience the internal and philosophical dimensions of their problems most acutely: the frustration, the self-doubt, the sense of injustice. A brand that names those internal dimensions resonates in a way that a purely functional message cannot.

## How To Use It

1. **Identify the Character.** Define your customer with specificity. Not "small business owners" but "solo founders managing their first team, who want to look competent but feel overwhelmed." The more specific the character, the more resonant the message.
2. **Name the Problem.** External: what is the practical problem they need to solve? Internal: how does it make them feel (frustrated, embarrassed, stuck)? Philosophical: why is it wrong that they are in this position?
3. **Position the Brand as Guide.** Show empathy first — demonstrate that you understand the problem from the inside, not just as an observer. Then show authority — a credential, a track record, a number, or a testimony that establishes you have solved this before.
4. **Offer a Plan.** Write out three steps that take the customer from problem to solution. Steps should be action-oriented and described from the customer's perspective. Keep them simple enough to remember.
5. **Write the Call To Action.** Decide your primary CTA (the direct invitation to buy or engage) and a secondary transitional CTA (for those not yet ready). Both must be explicit — "learn more" is not a call to action, it is a hedge.
6. **Describe Success.** Paint a concrete picture of the customer's life after. Avoid generic aspiration ("live your best life") and go specific: what do they have, do, feel, or experience that they don't now?
7. **Name the Failure Stake.** State clearly — briefly, without catastrophising — what continues to go wrong if the customer does not act. This is the cost of inaction, not a fear campaign.

## Worked Example

Acme Design is writing the homepage messaging for its platform, using StoryBrand to structure the brand narrative:

**A Character:** You're a self-taught designer — or a designer by title who knows your craft could go further. You have the eye. You're missing the technical range.

**Has A Problem:**
- External: You don't have a structured way to learn advanced motion design, brand systems, or typography — and YouTube tutorials only take you so far.
- Internal: You start courses and don't finish them. You feel stuck at the same level while the work around you keeps raising the bar.
- Philosophical: You shouldn't have to choose between learning on your own (slow, lonely, haphazard) and paying for expensive in-person programmes that don't fit your schedule.

**And Meets A Guide:** Acme Design has trained over 22,000 working designers, with instructors who are currently working in top studios and agencies. We built the live cohort model because we watched hundreds of talented designers buy courses they never finished — and we knew the problem wasn't motivation, it was structure.

**Who Gives Them A Plan:**
1. Choose a live cohort in your focus area — motion design, brand identity, or typography.
2. Join an eight-week cohort with 20 peers, weekly live sessions, and structured assignments reviewed by a working professional.
3. Graduate with a portfolio project you built during the cohort, a completion certificate, and a peer network in your discipline.

**And Calls Them To Action:**
- Primary: Start your cohort — next intake begins June 23.
- Transitional: Download our free curriculum guide to see what a full cohort covers.

**That Ends In Success:** Eight weeks from now, you have a motion design reel you built under live instruction from a director who works at Pentagram. Your completion rate was 84%. You know what to do next, and you know people doing the same work.

**And Helps Them Avoid Failure:** If you keep learning the way you have been — tutorials when you find time, courses you don't finish, skills gaps you paper over — the ceiling stays where it is. The designers who are outpacing you aren't more talented. They're better trained.

## When To Use It

StoryBrand is a marketing and brand communications framework — use it when you are writing for an external audience whose attention you have to earn: homepage copy, sales decks, email campaigns, brand positioning documents, pitch narratives.

It is strongest when the buyer is an individual with a personal stake in the outcome: a consumer, a professional buying for their own development, or a small-business owner making decisions that feel personal. In enterprise B2B contexts where buying decisions are made by committees, the framework still applies but the "character" becomes more complex — you may need to map the framework to the different individuals in the buying group separately.

StoryBrand is complementary to, not a replacement for, [5 ACT](./5-act.md) in pitch contexts. 5 ACT leads with market Trends and builds to Differentiators; StoryBrand leads with the customer and builds to stakes. Use 5 ACT when you are presenting to a business audience that wants strategic context; use StoryBrand when you are writing to a customer who wants to know whether you understand their problem.

## Things To Watch Out For

- The most common mistake is writing the guide section in a way that makes the brand the hero again: long company history, awards, founding story. Keep the guide section short and credibility-focused — empathy first, authority second, brevity always.
- The Plan must be simple enough that a customer can hold it in their head after reading it once. If the plan has seven steps or requires significant explanation, it will not function as a reassurance signal. Simplify, even at the cost of accuracy.
- Success and Failure must be specific. Vague success ("transform your career") and vague failure ("miss out on opportunities") do not move people. Ground both in concrete, recognisable details.
- StoryBrand does not produce a strategy — it produces a message. It tells you how to communicate what you offer, not whether what you offer is right, differentiated, or financially viable. It sits downstream of strategy, not in place of it.
