# Creating Conditions

*The posture underneath the practice.*

---

Everyone has something you can't teach them. Call it spark, instinct, taste, judgment. Whatever it is, it's theirs. No course gives it to them. No tool replaces it. No process manufactures it.

But here's what I keep seeing: that thing only shows up when the conditions are right. Put the same person in a classroom that punishes imagination and a living room where nobody's watching, and you get two completely different people. The talent didn't change. The environment did.

Most of us inherited an industrial mental model of how work is supposed to go. People are "resources." Teams are "managed." Organizations are machines you tune. Nobody chose that lens on purpose. It just came with the furniture. And it quietly suppresses the thing that actually makes people valuable — the stuff that can't be automated or copied.

Leaders aren't mechanics. They're closer to farmers. You don't make things grow. You create the conditions where growth is possible, and then you get out of the way.

Everything else in my work comes from this foundation.

---

## The two movements

Creating Conditions is two distinct moves, and both are required.

**Create the conditions.** This is active work. You arrange the environment. You manage the complexity that gets in the way. You establish the trust, clarify the purpose, set the boundaries, and put the right tools within reach. None of that happens by accident. It happens because someone designed it carefully.

The first time this came together for me was years ago, working as a consultant at ChaiOne. A mentor told me my job was to be a lead blocker — to facilitate making the environment one where an employee could succeed, because by definition if they succeeded, I'd reach my own goals. That reframed what management was actually for. It's what you see with Yoda and Dumbledore. The hero of the story isn't them. The hero is Harry. The hero is Luke. The wiser figure in the room creates the conditions where the hero finds out who they are, then gets out of the way long enough for them to do it.

**Get out of the way.** This is active restraint. Once the conditions are in place, the work isn't to stand over the result and steer. It's to step back and let what's growing take its own shape. Crowding what you made room for breaks the same conditions you just built. The discipline is knowing when your job changes from doing to letting be.

Stepping back isn't abdication. It's a calibration. You're measuring whether the conditions you put in place are doing what they were set up to do, and whether the person you trusted now has the room to use the trust. The discipline is doing it before it feels comfortable — earlier than the part of you that wants to keep steering says it's safe.

Mechanical thinking does only the first move, and often does it badly — piling on tools and processes that constrain rather than enable. Laissez-faire does only the second, which is abdication, not posture. The practice is both: deliberate condition-setting *plus* deliberate stepping back.

The Ken Robinson quote that originally seeded this for me captures both moves in a single sentence: "Farmers know that you cannot make a plant grow. The plant grows itself. What you do is create the conditions under which it will do that."

---

## What it rejects

The default mental model in most organizations is industrial. Work is treated like manufacturing. People are inputs. Process is the lever. If something isn't working, you add more tools, more layers, more oversight. The instinct is to mechanize the human parts so they become predictable.

What I keep watching is the same shape. A team struggles with something that's actually a human problem — a trust gap, an unclear decision, an emotional intelligence skill someone needs to develop. The mechanical response is to add a form. A new process. Another meeting. Another tool. The human problem doesn't go away; it just gets papered over with artifacts that everyone now has to navigate. Over enough cycles, the artifacts become the thing. The work the artifacts were supposed to lead to disappears under the weight of producing them.

That's where I came to "manage the process, lead the people." When managers avoid the human things by trying to install a tool or a process where a human skill is needed, what they're actually doing is choosing the easier failure mode. The process gives them something to point at. The leadership work — the trust-building, the direct conversation, the emotional intelligence — gives them no visible deliverable. So the deliverable wins, and the leadership doesn't happen.

The cost of that instinct is the thing it tries to optimize. Spark, instinct, taste, judgment — none of that mechanizes. Try to make it predictable and you make it disappear. The industrial model produces compliance, not creativity. Throughput, not care. Efficiency, not the kind of attention that makes products people love or teams people stay for.

What I keep arguing for is the opposite move. Stop trying to install what isn't there. Start attending to what is. The thing you actually want — the work that can't be automated or copied — is already inside the people you're working with. The job is to create the conditions where it shows up, then make sure you're not the thing in the way.

## Where it shows up

The six frameworks in my work are not separate philosophies. Each one is Creating Conditions applied to a different dimension of the work. They share the same posture and the same disciplines; they differ in what they're attending to.

**Organic Systems** (`frameworks/organic-systems.md`) is Creating Conditions at the organizational scale. "Organizations are living systems, not machines. The best leaders aren't mechanics who optimize — they're farmers who create the conditions where growth happens naturally, then get out of the way." That's the posture stated almost verbatim. The seven principles — manage complexity, design for natural growth, balance technology and humanity, focus on context, build trust through design, drive decisions with purpose, learn through evolution — are seven different ways of doing the conditions work at the level of teams and companies.

**Metaskills** (`frameworks/metaskills.md`) is Creating Conditions at the individual scale. The six metaskills — Exploring, Creating, Feeling, Imagining, Innovating, Adapting — are the capacities that develop in people when the conditions are right. They are not what you know; they are how well you can know. The posture says: don't try to install hard skills that will be obsolete in three years. Attend to the operating system underneath, which compounds rather than decays.

**Learning and Teaching** (`frameworks/learning-and-teaching.md`) is Creating Conditions in the transmission dimension. The teaching model (Theory → Method → Application) creates the conditions for understanding to take. The learning model (Deconstruct → Compare → Rebuild) creates the conditions for the learner to reassemble a working mental model in a wicked environment. Both models work by scaffolding what the learner does, not by transferring what the teacher knows.

**Legible and Memorable** (`frameworks/legible-and-memorable.md`) is Creating Conditions for the agentic-era market. The agent reads structure, specificity, and evidence; the human carries memory through story, identity, and unmediated experience. Both evaluators need conditions to recognize what you actually are. Truth, Memory, and Proof are three tracks of conditions work — what makes the brand legible to the agent and what shapes the human's recall after the moment of search has passed.

**Durable AI Practice** (`frameworks/durable-ai-practice.md`) is Creating Conditions applied to AI work specifically. Tools change faster than the work; the durability tests and the harness create the conditions for a practice that survives the next model release. Frame → Run → Trust → Learn is a loop for noticing where the work fell apart and restoring the conditions that make it not break next time. AI is one of the tools the conditions are arranged around, not the subject of the work.

**Minimum Lovable Products** (`frameworks/minimum-lovable-products.md`) is Creating Conditions for the user's experience of a product. The distinction MLP makes explicit — between *designing* the user experience and *shaping* the user's experience — is the conditions move applied to product. Emotional connection isn't a layer you add at the end. It is the result of a thousand small decisions made by people who were paying the right kind of attention. That kind of attention is what conditions work looks like in product design.

The posture is the through-line. Each framework is what the work looks like when you attend to a particular dimension of it.

## The integration

The reason these six frameworks belong to one body of work, rather than six separate ones, is the integration of the dimensions they cover. Design. Product. Technical. Human. Most practitioners hold one or two of those dimensions well. Few hold all four at once, and fewer still treat the integration itself as the work.

That integration is where the posture lives. You can be a strong designer who doesn't think about teams or technical durability and still ship things that work. You can be a strong product manager who doesn't think about how learning actually happens and still make a viable product. But you can't create the right conditions at the level of a company, a team, a brand, a product, or a practice without holding all four dimensions in view simultaneously. The dimensions reinforce each other or undermine each other; they don't sit politely in separate rooms.

The body of work in this project is what Creating Conditions looks like when those four dimensions are held together. Each framework attends to one piece of the integration: Organic Systems holds organizational design; Metaskills holds individual capacity; Learning and Teaching holds knowledge transmission; Legible and Memorable holds brand and market; Durable AI Practice holds technical practice in a changing tooling environment; Minimum Lovable Products holds artifact and user experience. None of them stands alone, and none of them is sufficient by itself.

The posture is what you arrive at when you've worked across all of them long enough that the integration starts to look like a single instinct.

## What this asks of the practitioner

The practical demand of Creating Conditions is uncomfortable. It asks you to do less of what looks like work. Fewer interventions. Fewer additions. Less direct steering. More attention to the surrounding environment than to the output you want.

It also asks you to trust people. Not because they always earn it, but because the posture only functions if the default assumption is that the spark is already there. If your default assumption is that you have to install what isn't, you'll spend your time adding things that wouldn't be needed if the conditions were right in the first place.

Trust isn't enough on its own, though. The mechanism for trust is safety — the kind where the room itself signals that the person in it can say what they actually think, make a decision that might not work out, and bring a perspective that differs from yours. That kind of safety doesn't show up by itself. It has to be installed deliberately, usually on day one, before someone has formed their assumptions about who you are and what kind of room you run.

The way I do this in practice is by breaking the frame people bring with them. New employees walk in with a script for what a boss is supposed to be, what kind of pushback is acceptable, how the power between us is supposed to work. New students walk in with the same kind of script for what a teacher is supposed to be. The hierarchy in both relationships is real — you need it for the relationship to function — but the preconceived notions around it are usually getting in the way of the work. Calling them out, naming them, and explicitly correcting the power balance is what makes safety operational. Without that, the room is technically open but the person in it is still performing the script they came in with.

And it asks you to accept slower compounding returns over visible short-term wins. The work shows up in what people produced, not in what you can point to as having done. Done well, Creating Conditions makes you almost invisible. The team grew. The product got lovable. The learner kept learning after the course ended. The brand stayed memorable a year later. Someone else gets to say they did that. Your job was to make sure the room was right.

The frameworks are six different shapes of the same discipline. The philosophy isn't the frameworks themselves; it sits underneath them. The name for it is Creating Conditions.
