# Strategy Spine

A strategy moves through seven stages, from the question worth answering to the move worth making, and then it loops. The spine names the sequence. The metaskills name why each stage demands the capacity it does.

## Origin

Most strategy frameworks hand you a set of tools and trust you to know when to reach for each one. That is the part they get wrong. The tools are rarely the problem. The order is.

A brilliant analysis run before the question is framed answers the wrong thing precisely. A story told before the synthesis is settled is persuasion with nothing underneath it. A decision made before the insight has surfaced is just a preference wearing a suit. The discipline that separates strategy that lands from strategy that merely impresses is sequence: doing the right kind of thinking at the right moment, and refusing to skip ahead because the answer feels obvious.

The Strategy Spine is that sequence. Seven stages, each one a distinct mode of thought, ordered so that every stage produces exactly what the next one needs and nothing it cannot yet support. A spine does not generate the answer. It holds the work upright while the answer emerges, then gets out of the way so the answer can move. That posture is the parent of this framework (see `creating-conditions.md`): you create the conditions for the right strategy to surface rather than forcing a predetermined one.

What makes the sequence more than an arrangement is what drives it. Each stage runs on one of the six metaskills (see `metaskills.md`). The spine is the visible shape of those capacities applied, in order, to a problem.

## The Spine

Define. Frame. Analyse. Insight. Synthesise. Story. Move.

The front of the spine perceives. The back of the spine generates. The two perceiving capacities, Exploring and Imagining, alternate across the first four stages: investigate, then see; investigate deeper, then see deeper. The three generating capacities, Creating, Feeling, and Innovating, run in sequence across the last three: make the meaning, make it felt, make it real.

The seventh capacity, Adapting, is not a stage. It is the loop. Move surfaces what no plan could predict, and Adapting is the return arrow that re-opens Define rather than defending the answer you already gave. A spine that does not loop is a waterfall, and strategy is never a waterfall.

Between Synthesise and Story sits a gate. Commitment locks there, before the narrative is built. You decide the through-line is the one you will stand behind, and only then do you spend the effort to make it land. Story is expensive, and storytelling makes a strategy harder to walk back. Committing first keeps narrative from quietly becoming the decision.

## Perceiving and Generating

The spine has a hinge in the middle, and it is worth naming because it is where most strategy work goes wrong.

The first four stages perceive. Define and Analyse run on Exploring: they investigate, one before the frame and one after it. Frame and Insight run on Imagining: they see, one constructing the lens and one reading what the lens revealed. The rhythm is deliberate. Investigation without a frame produces noise. A frame without prior investigation produces bias. So they alternate, each correcting the other, until perception is honest.

The last three stages generate. Synthesise builds the coherent whole (Creating). Story makes it felt (Feeling). Move makes it real (Innovating). These cannot be reordered. You cannot make people feel a meaning you have not yet built, and you cannot operationalize a story you have not yet told.

The failure mode of weak strategy is generating before perceiving is done: reaching Synthesise on the strength of one good Analyse, or jumping to Move because the Story was persuasive. The hinge is the discipline. Finish perceiving before you start generating.

## Stage Detail

### Define

Strategy begins by refusing the question you were handed. The problem as stated is almost never the problem worth solving. Define is where you establish the objective the strategy actually serves, what success would look like if you got there, and the single question the rest of the spine exists to answer. Get this wrong and every later stage is precise about the wrong thing.

Produces: the framed objective and the real question, stated plainly enough that anyone could check the final strategy against it.

### Frame

Before you look, decide how you will look. Frame constructs the lens: the dimensions, perspectives, and angles you will examine the problem through. This is a constructive act, not an analytical one. You are not finding the structure that is already there, you are choosing the structure to impose, and two strategists will choose differently. The frame determines the entire field of view for Analyse, which is why it comes first and why a lazy frame is so expensive: you can only find what your dimensions let you see.

Produces: the set of dimensions, perspectives, or hypotheses the analysis will work through.

### Analyse

Now look, hard, through the frame you built. Analyse interrogates each dimension for what is actually true: the gaps, the strengths, the cause-and-effect relationships, the numbers that change the picture rather than decorate it. The frame keeps this honest, so analysis answers the question instead of wandering. Discipline here is refusing to stop at the first finding that confirms what you hoped.

Produces: evidence per dimension, with the cause-and-effect relationships made explicit.

### Insight

Analysis tells you what is true. Insight tells you what it means. This is the step most often skipped, because it requires stepping back from the data you just worked so hard to gather and reading the pattern it makes. The non-obvious truth, the tension between two findings, the thing that is true but that nobody has said. Insight is where evidence becomes understanding. Without it, you carry a pile of facts into Synthesise and call it a strategy.

Produces: the small number of load-bearing insights, the truths the strategy will be built on.

### Synthesise

Insights arrive as fragments, and some of them disagree. Synthesise pulls them into a coherent whole: reconciling the tensions, prioritizing what matters most, and building the through-line that connects everything into one argument. This is the generative core of the spine, the act of making meaning rather than finding it. A synthesis that does not resolve its own tensions is a list, not a strategy.

Produces: the coherent strategy and its through-line. The commitment gate closes here.

### Story

A decided strategy that no one can feel will not survive contact with the people who have to carry it. Story shapes the strategy into a narrative that can be communicated, visualized, and understood, the form others can experience and act on. The map itself becomes the artifact: the document, the deck, the picture that holds the whole argument in a form a person can absorb and repeat. Story does not change the strategy. It makes it transmissible.

Produces: the narrative artifact, the strategy in a form built to be carried.

### Move

Strategy that does not change anything was a thought experiment. Move translates the story into action: assigning ownership, setting priorities, and creating the conditions under which execution can actually happen. It is the bridge from a decided direction to a different reality. The work of Move is not making the plan, it is making the plan real to the people who will run it.

Produces: owned, prioritized actions and the conditions for them to be executed.

## The Loop

Move always surfaces something the plan could not predict. The market moves, the assumption breaks, the execution teaches you what the analysis could not. Adapting is the metaskill that meets this without panic and without ego: you change direction in response to what is now true, and that change re-opens Define with a sharper question than the one you started with.

This is why the spine loops rather than ends. A strategy is not a document you finish. It is a position you hold and keep correcting. The teams that compound are not the ones that found the right answer once. They are the ones that kept running the spine.

## Relationship to the Body of Work

The Strategy Spine is a domain derivation of Creating Conditions (`creating-conditions.md`), applied to the strategy and decision dimension. Its facilitation posture, advising rather than answering, pushing on soft logic but deferring the call, keeping its own frames provisional, is Creating Conditions at the scale of a single strategic problem.

It draws its engine from Metaskills (`metaskills.md`). Each stage is one of the six capacities applied in sequence, and the loop is the seventh. The spine is what Metaskills looks like when pointed at a strategic problem rather than at a person's general capacity to learn.

Its analytical core is the Learning Model (`learning-and-teaching.md`) applied to strategy. Frame, Insight, and Synthesise are the strategic form of Deconstruct, Draw Comparisons, and Rebuild: break the problem into components, find the pattern beneath them, reconstruct a coherent whole. The moves rhyme because the cognitive engine is the same.

The metaskills differ by design, because the work differs. Deconstruct finds the structure already inside a thing, so it runs on Exploring; Frame constructs a structure to impose on a problem not yet examined, so it runs on Imagining. Rebuild reassembles new knowledge into your existing models to understand it, so it runs on Adapting; Synthesise generates a new strategy built to act on, so it runs on Creating. Learning ends in absorption, and strategy ends in a move. The stages the spine adds, Define and Analyse before the core and Story and Move after it, are where strategy parts from learning.
