# Tabular

> A structured table or grid that organises multiple insights into rows and categories so an audience can scan, compare, and absorb takeaways without reading everything linearly.

## What It Is

The Tabular layout is an Insight-stage presentation format. It arranges information into a grid of rows and columns: categories on the left, with sub-rows beneath each, and columns that carry descriptive detail, supporting commentary, and a rating or impact score. A single Tabular page can hold six to twelve distinct findings without feeling crowded, because the grid structure does the organisational work that prose would otherwise have to carry.

## Why It Works

A bulleted list forces the reader to hold context in their head — by the time they reach the fifth point, they've forgotten how the first one was framed. A Tabular layout removes that burden by making structure visible. The eye navigates to the group it cares about, finds the relevant sub-row, and reads across. That's a fundamentally different cognitive experience than linear prose.

The column architecture also forces the author to be disciplined. Every finding has to fit into the same template: what it is, what it means, and how significant it is. That discipline surfaces gaps — if you can't fill the "Impact" column for a finding, the finding probably isn't as clear as you thought. The grid thus works as both a communication tool and a quality-control mechanism for the analysis behind it.

## How To Use It

1. **Define your categories.** Group findings into two to four logical clusters (e.g. Acquisition, Retention, Revenue). These become your row-group headers.
2. **Identify sub-rows.** Within each category, list the individual findings or items (typically two to four per group). Each is its own row.
3. **Fill the description column.** Write the factual setup for each sub-row — what is true, what was observed, or what the data shows.
4. **Write the commentary column.** State the implication or "so what" — why this matters for the question at hand.
5. **Assign an impact rating.** Score each finding (High / Medium / Low, or a traffic light) to signal which items drive the most consequence.
6. **Write a headline takeaway.** Above or beside each category group, state the single most important insight the group of rows collectively supports.

## Worked Example

Acme Design is reviewing its subscription product performance heading into a board meeting. Rather than a slide of bullets, the team builds a Tabular page:

| Category | Sub-item | Description | Commentary | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Acquisition | Paid search | CPA rose from $18 to $27 over 6 months | Rising keyword competition is eroding efficiency | HIGH |
| Acquisition | Organic / SEO | Blog traffic +40% YoY | Untapped low-cost channel; only 8% of new subs | MED |
| Retention | Month-1 churn | 22% of new subscribers cancel in month 1 | Onboarding failure is the primary retention risk | HIGH |
| Retention | Power users | Top 15% of subscribers complete 4+ courses | Identify and clone the profile for acquisition targeting | MED |
| Revenue | ARPU | $29/month average, flat for 12 months | Price sensitivity limiting upsell; test annual plan | LOW |

The board can scan the "Impact" column in three seconds to know where to focus, then read across for context. They don't need to read the whole thing linearly.

## When To Use It

Use a Tabular layout when you have six or more distinct findings that belong to two or more logical groups, and your audience needs to be able to navigate them selectively rather than read them in sequence. It is the default insight page format precisely because it works for almost any type of content — analysis findings, initiative assessments, feature comparisons, risk reviews.

Prefer a **Tri-Column** layout when your findings fall naturally into three parallel themes rather than grouped rows. Prefer a **Heat Map** when the dominant communication is a rating across many items, with less emphasis on explanatory text.

## Things To Watch Out For

- Tables with more than five or six columns become unreadable on a single slide. Merge or cut columns ruthlessly — description and commentary can often be combined.
- Impact ratings lose meaning if everything is rated "High." Force a distribution: if more than half of your items are High, you haven't prioritised.
- The category groups should be mutually exclusive. If a finding could reasonably sit in two groups, the categories aren't well-defined — and the audience will notice the ambiguity even if they can't name it.
- A Tabular page with no headline statement at the top is a data dump. Always lead with the single most important conclusion; the table then proves it.
