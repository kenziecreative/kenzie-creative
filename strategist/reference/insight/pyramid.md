# Pyramid

> A layered triangle that organises ideas from foundational to pinnacle — showing that the higher levels are only possible because the lower levels exist and support them.

## What It Is

A Pyramid chart arranges ideas or components in stacked horizontal tiers, with the widest tier at the bottom and the narrowest at the top. The bottom layer holds the broadest, most foundational elements — the many things that make everything else possible. Each layer above it is smaller and more concentrated, representing a higher order of abstraction, aspiration, or rarity. The apex holds the single, defining idea that the whole structure supports.

A common version is the Brand Pyramid with three tiers: Idea at the bottom (the rational foundation), Tangible in the middle (what the brand delivers in practice), and Emotional at the top (how it makes people feel). But the structure is general-purpose: it works for capability hierarchies, value propositions, strategic priorities, or any set of ideas where the upper tiers depend on the lower ones being in place.

## Why It Works

A list of ideas is flat — every item looks equally important. A pyramid makes hierarchy visual. The narrowing shape communicates scarcity and elevation: there are many foundational elements, fewer mid-tier ones, and only one thing at the top. An audience grasps this hierarchy the moment they see the shape, without needing to read the labels.

The structural logic also communicates dependency. A pyramid is stable because its base is wide. If you remove a lower layer, the upper tiers collapse. That isn't just visual metaphor — it's the actual claim: you cannot have the pinnacle without the foundation. This makes the pyramid especially effective for communicating that strategic priorities must be sequenced, or that premium positioning requires operational capability first.

## How To Use It

1. **Identify your apex.** Decide what the single, highest-level idea is — the vision, the brand promise, the defining outcome. This sits at the top and is the point the whole diagram exists to make.
2. **Work downward.** For each level below the apex, ask: "What must be true, built, or delivered for the level above to be achievable?" Name each tier with a short, distinct label.
3. **Limit your tiers.** Three is the natural number; four is acceptable; five begins to look like a list in a triangle. If you have more than five things, consolidate or reconsider whether a pyramid is the right structure.
4. **Size the tiers deliberately.** The bottom tier should be wide, visually dominant, and labelled to signal its foundational role. The apex should feel small and singular.
5. **Add a short annotation for each tier** if the label alone doesn't convey what it contains. Keep annotations brief.

## Worked Example

Acme Design wants to articulate its brand to a new wave of potential instructors it's trying to recruit. The team builds a three-tier Brand Pyramid:

- **Apex — Emotional:** "You become the designer you were meant to be." This is the feeling Acme's best students describe: not that they learned software, but that they found their creative voice and professional identity.
- **Middle — Tangible:** Portfolio-quality project work, industry-standard tools (Figma, Illustrator), instructor feedback on real briefs, and a completion certificate recognised by hiring managers.
- **Base — Idea:** The belief that design skill is learnable by anyone willing to do the work — that talent is not the gate, practice is. This is the rational foundation that makes the rest credible.

The pyramid tells recruiting instructors the story in three moves: we believe anyone can learn (base) → we deliver that through rigorous, project-based practice (middle) → we give students a genuine transformation, not just a credential (top). An instructor who shares that emotional apex will teach differently than one who's just looking for a platform.

## When To Use It

Use a Pyramid when you need to communicate hierarchy and dependency — that the top is only possible because the bottom is solid. It works well for brand and value proposition communication, for showing the relationship between strategic enablers and strategic outcomes, and for sequencing an argument where each layer unlocks the next.

Use a **Driver Tree** instead when you need to show how multiple inputs combine to produce an output (the logic is additive, not hierarchical). Use the **Minto Pyramid** (in the Story stage) when the context is the structure of an argument in a document or presentation, not a visual framework for presenting an insight. Use a **Hub n' Spoke** when the relationship is radial — a central idea with equal-weight components — rather than tiered.

## Things To Watch Out For

- The shape implies a stable, load-bearing structure. If the lower tiers of your pyramid are aspirational rather than already in place, the diagram over-states your position. Be clear (in accompanying prose, not the diagram itself) about what exists today versus what you are building toward.
- Pyramids can be read bottom-up (foundation enables apex) or top-down (apex is supported by foundation). Decide which reading you intend and frame the page accordingly.
- Three tiers is almost always the right number. Four tiers is workable. Five looks like a parody of bureaucracy. If you find yourself at five, the bottom two tiers are probably the same tier under different names.
- A pyramid with only two tiers is just a box with a triangle on top. Use a simpler visual if you only have two levels.
