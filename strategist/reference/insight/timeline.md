# Timeline

> A chronological diagram that places events, milestones, or decisions on a line — showing when things happened, in what order, and how much time separates them.

## What It Is

A Timeline arranges milestones along a horizontal (or occasionally vertical) axis calibrated to time. Each milestone is anchored to a date, labelled with a short name, and accompanied by a brief description of what the event was and why it mattered. The axis makes sequence and duration visible at a glance: which event preceded which, how much time elapsed between them, and how far back or forward the story stretches.

Timelines are most commonly used to trace historical events — showing how an organisation, market, or situation evolved to its current state — but they can also project forward, marking planned milestones and decision points on a future axis.

## Why It Works

Sequence and timing are facts that prose buries. A paragraph can describe six milestones in six sentences, but a reader who finishes that paragraph cannot easily picture whether the events were clustered in a single year or spread across a decade, or which of them came first and forced the others. A Timeline makes those facts impossible to miss: the physical spacing of the markers communicates duration, and the left-to-right ordering makes sequence non-negotiable.

This is especially valuable in strategy work when the goal is to establish how a problem developed — or to show that a proposed plan is realistic given how long similar initiatives have taken in the past. A slide that says "we will launch in Q3" is a claim; a Timeline that shows the last three comparable launches each took 18 months, and Q3 is 9 months away, is a stress test.

## How To Use It

1. **Choose your time horizon.** Decide whether the timeline runs backward (historical), forward (planned), or both (current state at the midpoint). Set the axis to cover the relevant span.
2. **Identify your milestones.** Select the events that belong on the timeline — typically the moments that changed the situation, unlocked a next step, or marked a clear before/after. Be selective: five to eight milestones is the practical maximum before the diagram becomes a list.
3. **Anchor each milestone to a date.** The date doesn't need to be precise (a quarter or a year is often enough), but it must be specific enough to fix the milestone's position on the axis meaningfully.
4. **Write a short label and description for each.** The label is the name of the event (one to four words). The description provides the context a reader needs to understand why this milestone mattered.
5. **Consider grouping or colour-coding.** If milestones fall into distinct categories (e.g., product launches vs. competitor events vs. regulatory changes), use visual differentiation to make the categories legible without cluttering the timeline.

## Worked Example

Acme Design's leadership team is presenting to the board, and they need to explain why subscriber growth has stalled. Rather than opening with a financial slide, they open with a Timeline showing the company's history:

- **Jan 2019 — Founded:** Acme Design launched as a YouTube channel offering free Illustrator tutorials; built an audience of 40,000 subscribers within 18 months.
- **Sep 2020 — First paid course:** Released "Illustrator for Beginners" as a one-time purchase at $79. Sold 2,100 copies in the first three months.
- **Mar 2021 — Platform launch:** Moved from single-course sales to a subscription platform ($15/month). First 1,000 subscribers signed up within 60 days.
- **Oct 2021 — Peak growth:** Subscription base reached 8,500 — the fastest three-month growth period in company history — following a viral Instagram post by a student showcasing her portfolio.
- **Feb 2022 — Competitor entry:** DesignStack, a well-funded competitor, launched a directly competing subscription with free 30-day trials and aggressive Instagram advertising.
- **Jun 2022 — Growth stalls:** Monthly new subscriber additions dropped from ~400 to ~120. Churn ticked up from 3% to 5.5% as existing subscribers compared the two products.
- **Now (Dec 2022) — Strategy review:** Subscription base at 9,200; growth effectively flat for six months.

This timeline does more work than a revenue slide: it shows the board that Acme's growth problem is not a product failure but a competitive response problem that arrived at a specific moment (Feb 2022), and that the company was healthy before that point. The narrative frame — and the strategic question it sets up — follows naturally from the Timeline.

## When To Use It

Use a Timeline when the story you are telling is about sequence and timing — when the audience needs to understand how a situation developed over time, or when a planned sequence of events needs to be presented as a credible, time-anchored roadmap.

Distinguish it from related layouts: use a **Gantt** when you need to show overlapping initiatives across multiple pillars simultaneously (the Gantt handles parallel workstreams; the Timeline handles sequential milestones). Use a **Chevron** when the focus is on the phases of a process rather than the dates when milestones were reached. Use a **Horizon** when the communication need is strategic sequencing at a high level (what we do in years 1, 2, and 3) rather than a milestone-by-milestone historical or planned record.

## Things To Watch Out For

- A Timeline with too many milestones stops being a timeline and becomes an annotated calendar. If you have more than eight or nine events, cut the least consequential or group them into a single period with a brief label.
- The spacing of milestones on the axis should be proportional to the time between them. If two milestones are five years apart and another two are three months apart, the diagram should show that. Evenly spaced milestones falsely imply equal intervals.
- Selective inclusion is a form of argument. A Timeline that leaves out inconvenient events — a product failure, a regulatory setback — is doing rhetorical work the audience may not realise. If you are presenting to a board or external audience, include the milestones that are unflattering but relevant; omitting them will undermine trust if they are later noticed.
- A forward-looking Timeline is a plan, not a prediction. Label future milestones clearly as targets, and consider annotating the assumptions behind the dates — especially for plans that depend on external factors (regulatory approvals, partner launches, funding rounds).

## Related Frameworks

- [Gantt](./gantt.md) — shows overlapping initiatives across multiple strategic pillars over a shared time axis; use when you have parallel workstreams rather than a single sequence of milestones.
- [Chevron](./chevron.md) — shows the phases of a process or approach; use when the focus is on stages rather than dates.
- [Horizon](./horizon.md) — strategic sequencing at a thematic level; use when the communication need is "here's what we tackle in each of three phases" rather than a specific milestone record.
- [Journey](./journey.md) — shows the customer experience across phases; use when the story is about what the customer does, not when organisational events occurred.
