# Horizon

> A three-horizon time chart that organises strategic initiatives into near-term execution, medium-term capability-building, and long-term transformation — so a team can manage today's performance while investing in the future simultaneously.

## What It Is

The Horizon is an Insight-stage layout that divides a strategy's timeline into three sequential buckets — Horizon 1, Horizon 2, and Horizon 3 — each representing a different time range and a different type of strategic work. Each horizon carries a theme (the headline objective for that period) and a list of the specific initiatives or activities that fill it. The three horizons are typically displayed as an ascending staircase or as stacked bands running left to right, with H1 on the left (nearest in time) and H3 on the right (furthest out).

The time ranges are relative rather than fixed: H1 might be the next 12 months, H2 the following 1–3 years, and H3 the 3–5 year horizon. What matters is the type of work each horizon represents, not the exact calendar length.

## Why It Works

Most strategy conversations collapse into a false choice: do we focus on this year's numbers or invest for the future? The Horizon model resolves this by making the answer "both — but differently, and at the same time." H1 is where you protect and optimise what already works. H2 is where you build the new capabilities that will become your next core business. H3 is where you explore the options that might matter in five years. By giving each type of work its own named bucket, the framework makes it legitimate to pursue all three simultaneously without confusion about which work belongs where.

It also changes the conversation about resource allocation. Without explicit horizon thinking, long-term investments get systematically starved because they can't demonstrate near-term returns. The Horizon model gives leaders a vocabulary to defend H2 and H3 investments: "this initiative is a Horizon 2 build — it won't contribute to this year's numbers and it's not supposed to."

Think of it as a high-altitude Gantt: not "what will we build in which sprint" but "what are we betting on, and over what timeframe."

## How To Use It

1. **Define your three time bands.** Name the time ranges in a way that is honest for your organisation. For a startup, H1 might be 6 months; for a large enterprise, 18 months. Be explicit about the dates so "medium-term" isn't ambiguous.
2. **Name a theme for each horizon.** The theme is the headline objective — the common thread or dominant strategic priority for that period. It should be a phrase, not a list. ("Stabilise and grow our core subscription business" for H1, not a bullet list of activities.)
3. **List the initiatives within each horizon.** Two to five specific actions or projects per horizon. These should be concrete enough that someone could own them.
4. **Check the causal logic across horizons.** H2 initiatives should build toward the capabilities H3 assumes. H1's performance should fund H2 investment. If there is no logical handoff between horizons, the strategy has a gap.
5. **Use it in context.** The Horizon is an abstracted communication tool, not an operational plan. Follow it with a Gantt or Chevron that shows H1 in operational detail.

## Worked Example

Acme Design's three-horizon plan:

**Horizon 1 — "Protect and optimise the core subscription" (Now–12 months)**
Theme: Reduce churn and improve unit economics on the existing subscriber base.
Initiatives:
- Launch onboarding redesign to reduce month-1 churn from 22% to under 10%
- Introduce annual billing as the default sign-up option
- Produce 6 new intermediate-level courses to close the content gap
- Improve SEO content engine to reduce paid acquisition dependency

**Horizon 2 — "Build the B2B and certificate business" (12–30 months)**
Theme: Diversify revenue by opening corporate and credentialing channels.
Initiatives:
- Launch a team licence product for design studios (5–20 seats)
- Develop three industry-recognised design certificates with partner institutions
- Build a dedicated B2B sales motion (one account executive, outbound pilot)
- Create an instructor revenue-sharing model to attract higher-profile talent

**Horizon 3 — "Become the platform, not just the course library" (30–60 months)**
Theme: Transform from a content provider into a professional community and hiring platform.
Initiatives:
- Build a designer portfolio and credentialling passport
- Launch a talent marketplace connecting Acme certificate holders with employers
- Explore white-label licensing of the Acme curriculum to design schools

Reading across the three horizons: H1's improved unit economics fund H2's B2B build. H2's certificate programme creates the credentialling infrastructure H3's talent marketplace depends on. The causal logic holds.

## When To Use It

Use the Horizon when you need to communicate how near-term and long-term investments coexist within the same strategy — particularly to audiences who default to asking "when will we see a return?" It is especially valuable for boards and investors who need to understand that not all strategic investments are expected to return in the current fiscal year.

Use **Gantt** when the audience needs specific timelines and workstream-level detail for H1 execution. Use **Chevron** when the three periods are better understood as sequential phases where one must complete before the next begins.

## Things To Watch Out For

- Horizons are relative time bands, not fixed calendar years. Mapping all three rigidly onto single fiscal years collapses the essential "type of work" distinction that makes the framework useful.
- H3 initiatives that are just vague aspirations ("explore AI opportunities") do not belong in a strategic document. H3 should name specific bets, even if they carry high uncertainty.
- The framework can become a dumping ground: anything that doesn't fit H1's operational plan gets called "H3 exploration." If H3 has twelve items, you're not making choices.
- H2 is the horizon that most organisations actually fail to resource. Watch for H2 initiatives being deprioritised every planning cycle in favour of H1 urgency — that is the failure mode the framework exists to prevent.
