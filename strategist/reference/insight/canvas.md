# Canvas

> A grid of named building blocks that maps all the components of a business model — or any complex topic — onto a single page so the parts and their relationships are visible simultaneously.

## What It Is

The Canvas is an Insight-stage layout that arranges the key components of a business model (or any multi-part topic) as labelled blocks on a single page. In its best-known form — the Business Model Canvas — nine blocks cover the full commercial logic of a business: customer segments, value propositions, channels, customer relationships, revenue streams, key resources, key activities, key partnerships, and cost structure. Each block is filled in with a few concise bullet points rather than paragraphs.

The format generalises beyond business models. Any topic that can be decomposed into a fixed set of building blocks — a product launch plan, a technology platform, a team operating model — can be presented as a canvas. The defining feature is that all blocks appear on the same page at equal visual weight, making the relationships between them visible without having to flip between slides.

## Why It Works

Complex topics resist sequential explanation. If you walk an audience through a business model one component at a time, they cannot see how the pieces fit together until you've finished — and by then they've already started forming incomplete impressions. The Canvas solves this by showing everything at once. Because every block is visible simultaneously, the audience can trace dependencies: value proposition → customer segments → channels → revenue, or key activities → cost structure. Those connections are the strategy; the Canvas makes them inspectable.

The uniform block structure also creates a natural discipline for workshops. Each block is a contained problem with a clear scope. A team can split up, fill in blocks independently, then reconvene to see where their assumptions align and where they conflict. The page catches disagreements that would otherwise stay hidden.

## How To Use It

1. **Choose your canvas template.** For a business model, the nine-block Business Model Canvas is the standard starting point. For other topics, define your blocks in advance — typically five to nine named areas that collectively cover the full topic without overlapping.
2. **Fill in the highest-stakes blocks first.** For a business model canvas, that usually means Value Proposition and Customer Segments — because every other block flows from those two.
3. **Write in bullet form.** Each block holds three to five brief points, not paragraphs. The constraint forces prioritisation.
4. **Check the connections.** Trace the logical links between blocks. Does the Revenue Streams block match what your Customer Segments would actually pay for? Do your Key Activities align with what your Value Proposition requires?
5. **Use it to run workshops.** Print it large (A1 or bigger) and use sticky notes so teams can add, move, and remove items in real time.
6. **Iterate.** Canvases are living tools. Fill in what you know now, mark what's uncertain, and return to update as you learn more.

## Worked Example

Acme Design's business model mapped onto a nine-block canvas:

- **Customer Segments:** Independent graphic designers (2–5 years experience); aspiring freelancers in career transition; design students supplementing formal education.
- **Value Proposition:** High-quality, practitioner-taught video courses; structured learning paths with certificates; community access to working professionals.
- **Channels:** SEO-driven blog and YouTube; Instagram and design community forums; email newsletter (42k subscribers).
- **Customer Relationships:** Self-serve subscription; monthly live Q&A sessions; peer community forum.
- **Revenue Streams:** Monthly subscription ($29/mo); annual plan ($249/yr); corporate team licences ($500/seat/yr).
- **Key Resources:** Course library (180 videos); instructor network (22 active instructors); learning platform (custom LMS).
- **Key Activities:** Course production; instructor sourcing and management; content marketing; platform development.
- **Key Partnerships:** Design software vendors (co-marketing); design schools (distribution); freelance platforms (referral arrangements).
- **Cost Structure:** Instructor fees (largest cost); platform hosting and development; content production; paid acquisition.

Seeing these nine blocks together reveals, for example, that Acme's Corporate Revenue stream is new and under-supported — there are no Key Activities dedicated to B2B sales and no Customer Relationship mechanism designed for team buyers.

## When To Use It

Use the Canvas at the Insight stage when you need to present the complete logic of a business model, product, or operational topic in one place — especially to an audience that includes people from different functional areas who each know only their own piece.

It is particularly valuable at the start of a strategy process (to map the current state before deciding what to change) and at the end (to show how the strategy reshapes the business model). For showing the current-to-future-state shift explicitly, pair it with **From:To**.

## Things To Watch Out For

- A canvas filled with aspirational bullet points rather than current-reality observations is a wish list, not a diagnosis. Fill in what is actually true before filling in what you hope will be true.
- Blocks filled with three-word labels ("great service," "best content") carry no information. Push for specifics: which service, what content, for whom.
- The Business Model Canvas was designed for business models. Forcing unrelated topics into its nine-block structure because it's familiar usually produces awkward fits. Define your own blocks when the topic genuinely requires it.
- In a workshop, dominant voices tend to fill blocks fast. Give everyone sticky notes and a time limit before the group discussion, or the canvas ends up reflecting the most confident voice rather than the team's full knowledge.
