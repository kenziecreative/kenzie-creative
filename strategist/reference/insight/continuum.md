# Continuum

> A set of horizontal sliders — one per strategic dimension — that shows where a product, brand, or organisation sits today on each spectrum and where the strategy is moving it, so the directional choices of the strategy become visible at a glance.

## What It Is

The Continuum is an Insight-stage layout that represents strategic positioning as a series of horizontal sliders. Each slider spans a dimension with a named pole at each end (e.g. "Generalist" ↔ "Specialist," or "Low-touch" ↔ "High-touch"). Two markers appear on each slider: one for the **current** position and one for the **target** position after the strategy is executed. By stacking three to six sliders on a single page, the layout lets an audience see the full set of strategic positioning choices simultaneously.

## Why It Works

Strategy is often framed as a list of things to do. But what strategy fundamentally requires is making choices about *where to sit* on a set of competing dimensions — and those choices are inherently about direction, not just activity. The Continuum makes direction visible. A slider that moves left tells a different story than a slider that moves right, and an audience can read that directional signal in seconds without parsing prose.

The format is also honest about trade-offs. Every dimension has two ends, and sitting at one end means not sitting at the other. A Continuum that shows a brand moving from "mass market" toward "premium niche" immediately surfaces the implication: the brand is giving up reach to gain margin. That trade-off is often hidden in bullet-point strategy documents and only becomes apparent in the Continuum because the pole you are moving away from is explicitly named.

Stacking multiple sliders also reveals strategic consistency — or its absence. If one slider says "move toward premium" and another says "move toward high volume," the inconsistency is visible because both sliders are on the same page.

## How To Use It

1. **Identify three to six strategic dimensions.** Choose the attributes that define your competitive positioning and are genuinely subject to strategic choice. Avoid dimensions where you have no real trade-off (if you're always going to be "high quality," that slider adds no information).
2. **Name both poles.** For each dimension, write a concise label for the left pole and the right pole. The poles should be genuine opposites, not a good-bad pair — both ends should be valid strategic positions that different organisations might occupy.
3. **Plot the current position.** Mark where the organisation sits today on each slider with a circle or dot labelled "Current." Be honest — this is a diagnostic.
4. **Plot the target position.** Mark where the strategy is moving the organisation with a different coloured marker labelled "Target."
5. **Draw an arrow connecting the two.** The arrow makes the direction and magnitude of the strategic move visible.
6. **Write a brief annotation.** For any slider where the directional choice is not self-evident, add a one-line note explaining the reasoning.

## Worked Example

Acme Design's strategic repositioning, presented as a Continuum with four dimensions:

**Dimension 1 — Content Breadth**
Left: Generalist (all design topics) ← ← Current | Target → Right: Specialist (graphic design depth)
Note: Acme is narrowing its curriculum focus to establish clear authority rather than competing on breadth.

**Dimension 2 — Audience**
Left: Beginner-first ← Current | Target → → Right: Professional-first
Note: Moving from entry-level acquisition toward mid-career professionals, who have higher willingness to pay and lower churn.

**Dimension 3 — Business Model**
Left: Consumer (B2C) → Current | Target → Right: Mixed (B2C + B2B)
Note: Adding a corporate licensing channel alongside the existing consumer subscription.

**Dimension 4 — Learning Experience**
Left: Self-paced, asynchronous ← ← Current | Target → Right: Community and live components
Note: Adding monthly live Q&As and peer forums to increase engagement and differentiation.

Reading across the four sliders, the strategy has a clear shape: Acme is moving toward depth, professionals, B2B revenue, and a richer experience. A reader can assess whether those four moves are consistent with each other — and they are: deeper content for professionals, sold to teams, with community components that justify a premium price.

## When To Use It

Use the Continuum when the core strategic choices can be expressed as positioning decisions along meaningful dimensions — particularly when the most important message is the *direction* of movement rather than the destination. It is especially effective in repositioning strategies, brand strategy documents, and product strategy reviews where you are explicitly choosing to move away from something.

Use **From:To** instead when the current and future states are complex enough to require descriptive paragraphs or diagrams rather than a single slider position. Use **Positioning** (the competitor map) when the comparison you need to make is against named competitors rather than along your own strategic dimensions.

## Stage Boundary (Insight)

The Target marker *is* the strategy — plotting it is a decision, not an observation. At Insight, run steps 1–3 only: choose the dimensions, name the poles, and plot the **Current** positions honestly. That diagnostic alone is real insight — it shows where you actually sit and which dimensions are genuinely in play. The Target markers, arrows, and annotations (steps 4–6) are Synthesise-onward work: return to this layout after the commitment gate to plot where the chosen strategy moves you, and present the finished set at Story.

## Things To Watch Out For

- Poles that are good-versus-bad ("high quality" ↔ "low quality") create a Continuum that only states the obvious. Both poles must be legitimate strategic positions that a real competitor might intentionally occupy.
- Too many sliders (more than six) produce a chart that looks comprehensive but communicates nothing — the audience can't hold six separate directional decisions in their head simultaneously. Prioritise the three to four dimensions that are actually consequential.
- A Continuum where all sliders move in the same direction (all rightward) may indicate that only one strategic thesis is being expressed, just restated multiple times. Check whether the dimensions are truly independent.
- The gap between Current and Target is a claim about what the strategy will change. If a slider moves only one increment, it probably shouldn't be on the Continuum at all — that isn't a strategic choice, it's incremental drift.
