# Image Column

> A three-column layout that leads each column with a visual or diagram, follows it with explanatory text, and unites all three columns under a single synthesising takeaway.

## What It Is

An Image Column page divides the page into three vertical columns, each representing a distinct component of the argument. At the top of each column sits a visual — an icon, a small chart, a diagram, or an illustration — that anchors the component visually before the text explains it. Below the visual, a short block of text describes the key points, evidence, or steps for that component. Below all three columns, a single "Big Takeaway" statement synthesises the three components into one headline insight that tells the audience what to conclude.

The structure is a variation of the Tri-Column layout, with the deliberate addition of visuals at the top of each column to make the components more memorable and more scannable.

## Why It Works

Reading a dense text-only slide requires a reader to process words, build a mental model, and extract the point — all at the same time. An Image Column breaks that sequence. The visual at the top of each column gives the brain something concrete to anchor to before the text arrives. By the time a reader reaches the explanatory text, they've already formed a rough mental image of what the column is about. The text then confirms and extends that image rather than constructing it from scratch.

The Big Takeaway below all three columns does the work of synthesis explicitly, so the audience doesn't have to. Three separate columns can read as three separate arguments. The Big Takeaway re-unites them: "these three things together mean one thing, and here it is." This structure makes it nearly impossible for an audience to finish the page without knowing the conclusion.

The format is particularly effective when the three components are best understood by seeing something — a before/after, a process diagram, a chart — rather than by reading a description of it. The visual column exploits the natural superiority of images over text for spatial, relational, and procedural information.

## How To Use It

1. **Identify your three components.** These should be the three elements, reasons, or pillars that together support the Big Takeaway. They should be parallel in nature — three reasons, three features, three phases — so the three-column structure feels logical rather than arbitrary.
2. **Create or select a visual for each.** The visual should communicate the essence of the component at a glance. Small charts, icons, annotated screenshots, simple diagrams, or even photographs can work. The visual should be legible at presentation size.
3. **Write the column text.** Under each visual, write the key points or evidence for that component. Keep it brief — three to five bullet points or two to three sentences. The visual carries the weight; the text supports and extends it.
4. **Write the Big Takeaway.** The takeaway at the bottom unifies the three components into a single, actionable conclusion. Write it in plain language, and make it strong enough to stand alone as the one thing someone remembers from the page.
5. **Keep visuals consistent in style.** Three columns with three visuals in three different visual registers (one photo, one hand-drawn diagram, one screenshot) look unplanned. Align the visual style across all three columns.

## Worked Example

Acme Design's product team needs to present the case for launching a certificate programme to the executive team. They build an Image Column slide titled "Three reasons to launch a certificate now."

**Column 1 — Market Signal**
*Visual: A bar chart showing that "design certificate" search volume grew 140% over 18 months.*
Key points: Demand for credentials that signal job-readiness is accelerating, especially among career-changers entering design. Employers increasingly use certificates as a first-pass filter when reviewing portfolios.

**Column 2 — Competitive Gap**
*Visual: A simplified Positioning Map showing that no direct competitor offers a project-based portfolio certificate in Figma specifically.*
Key points: Skillshare and Domestika offer courses but no certificates. LinkedIn Learning offers a Figma certificate, but it is assessment-based (multiple choice) with no portfolio output. Acme's project-based format is genuinely differentiated.

**Column 3 — Retention Impact**
*Visual: A line chart showing that students who complete three or more projects have a 12-month retention rate of 74%, vs. 28% for those who complete fewer than three.*
Key points: Project completion is Acme's strongest predictor of retention. A certificate programme creates a structured path that encourages students to complete the projects. Formalising that path is expected to lift the cohort retention rate from 31% to approximately 55%.

**Big Takeaway:** There is a real market demand for a project-based Figma certificate, no direct competitor currently offers one, and Acme's existing data shows that the students most likely to complete a certificate are also the students most likely to retain and refer others. Launch is strategically justified.

## When To Use It

Use an Image Column when you have three distinct components that are easier to understand with a visual than with text alone, and when those three components together support a single synthesising conclusion. It is the right format when you are trying to move an audience from "I see the evidence" to "I understand the implication" in a single page.

Use a **Tri-Column** instead when the components can be communicated effectively through text and data points alone, without needing a visual anchor. Use a **Hub n' Spoke** when the three to five components radiate from a central big idea rather than running in parallel columns. Use a **Pyramid** when the relationship between the components is hierarchical (lower tiers enable upper tiers) rather than parallel.

## Things To Watch Out For

- The visual must be genuinely informative, not decorative. A generic stock icon of a lightbulb above a column about "insight" adds nothing — it may actually slow reading because the reader spends time looking at the icon expecting it to communicate something it doesn't. Every visual should either compress information (a chart, a diagram, a process flow) or clarify a spatial or relational concept that would take several sentences to explain in words.
- The Big Takeaway must actually synthesise — not just summarise. "These three things are important" is not a takeaway. "These three things together mean we should launch in Q2 rather than wait" is.
- Three columns is the natural number for this layout. Two columns work if you have only two components and they are substantial; four columns become cramped at standard slide widths and force the text too small. Resist the urge to add a fourth.
- Visual consistency matters more here than in text-only layouts, because the eye is drawn to the images first and registers inconsistency immediately. Use a consistent icon set, a consistent chart style, or a consistent illustration approach across all three columns.
