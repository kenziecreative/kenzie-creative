# Positioning

> A two-axis map that places your product and your competitors in the same visual space, so you can see where the market is crowded, where it's empty, and where you currently sit.

## What It Is

A Positioning map (sometimes called a perceptual map) is a 2×2 coordinate grid. Each axis represents a dimension that matters to customers — price, quality, speed, accessibility, modernity, or any other attribute that drives choice. Every competitor in the market gets plotted as a point. Your product gets plotted too, often highlighted. The resulting scatter of points reveals the competitive landscape: where players cluster, where gaps exist, and where the white space is.

## Why It Works

Strategy without a map of the competitive terrain is navigation without geography. A list of competitors tells you who is in the market; a Positioning map tells you *where* they are relative to each other and relative to you. The spatial layout does something a table cannot: it makes clustering and gap-finding immediate and obvious. When six competitors are stacked in the top-right quadrant, you see in one glance that high-price, high-quality is saturated — and you can see at a glance whether the bottom-left is empty or equally crowded.

The map also externalises assumptions. When you plot your competitors, you are forced to commit to a reading of where they sit on two chosen axes. Those choices surface disagreement fast: if two team members place the same competitor in different quadrants, that's a real strategic disagreement about what customers value, surfaced before you've built a slide deck around a false assumption.

## How To Use It

1. **Choose your two axes.** Select the two attributes that most drive customer choice in your market. Price vs. quality is the default, but almost any two meaningful dimensions work. The axes should be independent of each other (not perfectly correlated) and genuinely important to customers.
2. **Label the poles.** Name the high and low ends of each axis explicitly — e.g., "High Price / Expensive" and "Low Price / Affordable" on the vertical axis. This removes ambiguity when you present the map.
3. **Plot your competitors.** Place each competitor as a point (or logo). Be honest — plot them where the evidence suggests they sit, not where you'd like them to be.
4. **Plot yourself.** Add your own product, clearly distinguished (typically a different colour or symbol).
5. **Read the map.** Look for clusters (where competition is intense), gaps (underserved positioning territory), and your own position relative to both.
6. **Add movement arrows if needed.** If your strategy involves repositioning, draw an arrow from your current position to your intended position to show the strategic direction.

## Worked Example

Acme Design wants to understand how it sits in the online design-education market. It chooses two axes: **price** (vertical, from low to high) and **learning depth** (horizontal, from surface-level to mastery-focused). It plots six players:

- **YouTube tutorials:** bottom-left (free, surface-level).
- **LinkedIn Learning:** lower-middle (low subscription price, broad but shallow courses).
- **Skillshare:** middle-left (low price, project-based but introductory).
- **RISD Online / SVA:** top-right (high price, deep mastery focus).
- **Domestika:** lower-middle (low price, project-based, mid depth).
- **Acme Design:** middle-centre (mid price, moderate depth).

The map shows that the bottom-left is crowded with free and near-free content, the top-right is occupied by expensive academic programmes, and the middle-right — mid-to-high price, genuine mastery depth — is almost empty. That gap becomes the strategic target: reposition Acme toward mid-high price with a mastery-level curriculum, occupying territory none of the current players can easily move into.

## When To Use It

Use a Positioning map when the core question is competitive: where do we sit in the market, and is there a better place to be? It is most useful in the Insight stage, after you have gathered Analyse-stage data on competitors, and before you commit to a strategic direction in Synthesise.

Use a **Venn** instead when the question is about overlap between attributes rather than placement on a spectrum. Use a **Segmentation** map when you need to show the size of customer groups rather than their preferences. Use a **From:To** when you've already decided on repositioning and want to show the before/after, rather than exploring the landscape.

## Things To Watch Out For

- Axis choice determines everything. Pick the wrong two dimensions and the map tells a story that doesn't match what customers actually care about. Validate your axis choice against customer research, not internal intuition alone.
- The temptation to draw the map so your product sits in a visually obvious gap is strong and corrupting. If you backfill the competitor positions to make your product look well-placed, the map is marketing, not strategy.
- Two dimensions can never capture the full competitive picture. A Positioning map is a simplification by design — but that means you may be hiding a third dimension that actually matters most. If after plotting you feel the map misrepresents the market, consider whether you've picked the right axes or whether you need a second map with different axes.
- Clustering in one quadrant isn't automatically bad — it may mean that's where the customers are. Before concluding a gap is an opportunity, check whether there's genuine demand in that territory.
