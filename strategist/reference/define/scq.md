# SCQ

> A three-part frame — Situation, Complication, Question — that turns a messy problem into a single, answerable question before you try to solve it.

## What It Is

SCQ is the lightest way to define a problem. It frames the problem in three moves. The
**Situation** sets the stage with the context the audience needs to reach a baseline
understanding. The **Complication** names the issue you face — what changed, and why it
demands decisive action. The **Question** asks the next logical question to solve the
problem: the one that, fully answered, becomes your proposed solution. The problem
statement itself is then built by combining and summarising the Situation and the
Complication.

## Why It Works

People skip problem definition because it feels obvious — they jump to solutions and
discover too late that everyone was solving a slightly different problem. SCQ works
because it forces agreement on the problem *before* anyone argues about answers, and it
does so in the order a listener's mind actually moves: first orient me (Situation), then
tell me what's wrong (Complication), then the question lands as something I already feel
the need to answer rather than a demand dropped on me cold.

Its real power is the **Question**. By ending on a single, well-formed question, SCQ
constrains the entire effort that follows: a sharp question admits only a few real
answers, while a vague one lets the work sprawl in every direction. Getting the Question
right is most of the work of getting the strategy right.

## How To Use It

1. **Set the stage (Situation).** State the stable, agreed-upon context — what's true and
   not in dispute — to bring everyone to a shared baseline.
2. **Introduce the problem (Complication).** Describe what changed or what's now at stake,
   and why it requires action now.
3. **Ask the question (Question).** Pose the single logical question the strategy must
   answer. Keep it open enough to admit more than one possible solution.
4. **State the problem.** Combine and summarise the Situation and Complication into a
   crisp problem statement that the Question points toward solving.

## Worked Example

Acme Design sells subscriptions to graphic-design courses:

- **Situation:** Acme Design launched a subscription learning platform for its suite of
  graphic-design courses, specialising in high-quality courses taught by industry
  veterans.
- **Complication:** After a period of steady growth, the trend has reversed — new
  subscribers are declining by 5% a month for the past three months.
- **Question:** How can Acme Design turn around its subscriber sales?
- **Problem statement:** Return subscriber growth to a sustainable run rate of >1,000
  subs per month by 2024.

Notice the Question is open — it doesn't presume the fix is pricing, or marketing, or
retention. That's deliberate: it keeps every real option on the table for the analysis
that follows.

## When To Use It

Reach for SCQ at the start of the Define stage when you need to frame a problem fast and
communicate it to others. It's the lightest of the three Define frameworks — use it when
the problem is reasonably well understood and the job is to state it clearly rather than
to interrogate it. Step up to **HTDQ** when you want a more vivid, narrative frame, or
**Outcome** when the problem is high-stakes and needs a rigorous, checklist-driven
definition.

## Things To Watch Out For

- The frame is only as honest as its Situation. Loading the Situation with disputed claims
  smuggles your conclusion in before the Question is even asked.
- A Question that's secretly a pre-baked answer ("How do we launch the referral
  programme?") collapses the analysis before it starts. Keep it open enough to admit more
  than one solution.
- A Complication with no real stakes produces a problem no one feels the need to solve. If
  you can't say why it demands action now, you may not have a problem worth a strategy.
