# HTDQ

> A four-part narrative frame — Hero, Treasure, Dragon, Quest — that defines a problem as a story so the urgency and direction are immediately clear.

## What It Is

HTDQ is a problem-definition frame drawn from narrative logic. It identifies four elements: the **Hero** (who faces the problem), the **Treasure** (what they are trying to achieve), the **Dragon** (the single obstacle standing between them and the treasure), and the **Quest** (the overarching question the work must answer, stated as *"How should [the Hero] get [the Treasure], given [the Dragon]?"*). Together, the four elements define the problem not as a technical description but as a situation with a protagonist, a goal, an obstacle, and a question — in that order.

## Why It Works

The key insight is that a problem with a protagonist and a concrete obstacle is far more visceral than a problem stated as an abstract gap between current state and desired state. When you describe the hero and what they want, and then name the one dragon in the way, listeners feel the problem — and that matters because felt problems generate action, while abstract ones generate deferral.

The Dragon is the pivot of the entire frame. HTDQ forces you to identify *one* dominant obstacle, not a list of contributing factors. That discipline is deliberate and valuable: a problem with five dragons has no strategic answer, only a programme of work. A problem with one dragon has a clear target. If you cannot name a single dragon, the problem is either not yet real or not yet properly bounded.

The Quest's phrasing — "How should [Hero] get [Treasure], given [Dragon]?" — matters structurally. It acknowledges the obstacle without accepting it as permanent, and it keeps the question open to multiple answers. That openness is what makes the Quest a good brief for the analysis that follows.

## How To Use It

1. **Name the Hero.** State the facts about who you are working on behalf of — the organization, team, or person whose situation defines the problem's boundaries. Include just enough context to make the situation legible.
2. **State the Treasure.** Describe the aspiration or outcome the Hero is trying to reach. Be specific: a direction ("grow") is not a treasure; a destination ("grow subscribers 20% per year") is.
3. **Identify the Dragon.** Name the one problem that stands between the Hero and the Treasure. Resist listing multiple dragons. If there are several candidates, pick the most foundational one — the one that, if removed, would do the most to clear the path.
4. **Write the Quest.** Combine the three elements into a single question: "How should [Hero] get [Treasure], given [Dragon]?" This becomes the brief that governs your analysis.

## Worked Example

Acme Design sells subscriptions to graphic-design courses and has hit a growth ceiling:

- **Hero:** Acme Design launched its learning platform offering subscriptions to its suite of graphic-design courses. Subscriber growth started strong but has plateaued for the last six months.
- **Treasure:** Acme Design wants to grow its subscriber base by 20% per year.
- **Dragon:** Not many people know that Acme now offers learning courses — brand awareness among the target audience is low.
- **Quest:** How should Acme Design grow subscribers at 20% per year, given that people are unaware of its new offer?

The Quest immediately focuses the analysis on the awareness problem. Without HTDQ, the team might have run experiments on pricing or course quality — both legitimate concerns, but neither addresses the foundational obstacle. The Dragon forces the team to confront the right question first.

## When To Use It

HTDQ belongs at the start of the Define stage when the problem needs to be communicated to others who don't yet feel its urgency. It is particularly strong in stakeholder settings — a narrative frame lands faster and holds attention longer than a structured checklist. Use it when you need buy-in as well as clarity.

If the job is quick orientation rather than stakeholder alignment, **SCQ** is lighter and faster. If the problem is high-stakes or contested and demands a rigorous, criterion-by-criterion definition, step up to **Outcome**, which adds layers like timeframe, constraints, accuracy requirements, and decision-maker criteria that HTDQ deliberately omits.

## Things To Watch Out For

- The Dragon is the hardest part to get right. Groups naturally list several obstacles rather than commit to one. If you leave with multiple dragons, you haven't completed the frame — you've deferred the hardest thinking. Push until one dominant obstacle emerges.
- The Treasure must be genuinely specific. "Improve performance" or "grow the business" are aspirations, not treasures — they don't constrain the Quest. A vague treasure produces a vague Quest, and a vague Quest produces unfocused analysis.
- The Hero's description can smuggle in assumptions. If the Hero section quietly presupposes a solution ("Acme Design, which needs to run paid campaigns, launched..."), you've poisoned the Quest before it begins. Keep the Hero section factual and neutral.
- HTDQ is a frame for defining problems, not for solving them. A compelling HTDQ doesn't mean you've found the right answer — it means you've asked the right question. The analysis still has to happen.
