# Eisenhower

> A 2×2 matrix that sorts every task by urgency and importance so you can see at a glance what to do yourself, what to delegate, what to schedule, and what to drop.

## What It Is

The Eisenhower Matrix places tasks on two axes — how time-critical the issue is (urgent vs. not urgent) and how consequential it is (important vs. not important) — producing four quadrants, each with a prescribed action:

- **Quadrant 1 — Do (Urgent + Important):** Tactical work, fires, and quick wins that demand your personal attention right now. If left undone, short-term consequences follow.
- **Quadrant 2 — Schedule (Not Urgent + Important):** Strategic work with longer timeframes — the things that actually drive results but never feel pressing. This is where leverage lives.
- **Quadrant 3 — Delegate (Urgent + Not Important):** Work that needs to get done but doesn't require your specific skills or authority. Pass it to someone else.
- **Quadrant 4 — Drop (Not Urgent + Not Important):** Tasks that deliver no real value. Eliminate them with little consequence.

## Why It Works

The trap most people fall into is treating urgency as a proxy for importance. A ringing phone, a meeting request, an incoming email — all feel urgent and therefore important. But urgency is just social pressure dressed up as priority. A decision made under time pressure isn't automatically a high-stakes one.

The Eisenhower Matrix breaks the two axes apart and forces you to assess them independently. The structural insight is that Quadrant 2 — the not-urgent-but-important box — is the one that almost never gets attention in a reactive week, yet it contains the decisions and work that most determine long-run outcomes. By making Q2 visible as a category, the matrix creates permission to protect time for it rather than letting Q1 and Q3 crowd it out.

The framework also makes delegation legible. Q3 items feel urgent enough that many people hold onto them; surfacing them as a category with a "delegate" label makes it obvious they should move to someone else.

## How To Use It

1. **List everything on your plate.** Tasks, projects, decisions, requests — get them out of your head and onto paper.
2. **Score each item on urgency.** Is there a real, near-term deadline with meaningful consequences if missed? Urgent = yes; not urgent = no.
3. **Score each item on importance.** Does it contribute meaningfully to a goal that matters — your own or the organisation's? Important = yes; not important = no.
4. **Place each item in the correct quadrant** and apply the quadrant's action: Do, Schedule, Delegate, or Drop.
5. **Act.** Clear Q1 now. Block protected time for Q2. Assign Q3 items to the right people. Remove Q4 items from the list entirely.

## Worked Example

Acme Design's Head of Product sits down on a Monday with a pile of demands:

- A key enterprise customer emailed over the weekend threatening to cancel unless a billing bug is fixed by Tuesday. → **Q1: Do.** Urgent and important — revenue is at risk.
- Acme's pricing model hasn't been reviewed in two years; a new competitor is undercutting on entry-tier plans. → **Q2: Schedule.** Not pressing today, but strategically consequential. Block three hours Thursday.
- The CEO's assistant asked for slides summarising last month's product metrics for an investor update. → **Q3: Delegate.** Urgent (needed by Wednesday) but doesn't require the Head of Product specifically — a data analyst can draft them.
- A vendor sent a demo request for a project management tool Acme is not evaluating. → **Q4: Drop.** Not urgent, not important.

By the end of the exercise, the Head of Product has one personal action item for today, one calendar block for strategic work, one delegated task, and one deleted email — instead of a four-item to-do list that would have crowded out the pricing review for another month.

## When To Use It

Use Eisenhower when you or your team are overwhelmed and need to sort demands quickly — especially when everything *feels* urgent. It's a triage tool, best used at the start of a week or sprint, or any time the inbox has taken over the calendar.

It's less suited to comparing *options* for a single decision (where **Evaluation** or **Pros & Cons** fit better) and less suited to complex branching decisions with uncertain outcomes (where **Decision Tree** applies). Its natural companion is **ABCD**, which provides a finer-grained four-letter categorisation for decisions rather than tasks.

## Things To Watch Out For

- **Urgency creep.** Other people's urgency doesn't make something important. A request that arrives with a tight deadline is still a Q3 or Q4 item if it doesn't advance your goals. Asserting the matrix means sometimes disappointing people who have conflated the two.
- **Q2 starvation.** The most common failure mode: Q1 and Q3 fill every day and Q2 strategic work never happens. Protecting Q2 time requires blocking it *before* the week starts, not after.
- **False urgency in Q1.** Some items feel like fires but aren't — the deadline is self-imposed or the consequence of missing it is smaller than it appears. Running the "what actually happens if this waits 48 hours?" check saves Q1 from becoming a dumping ground.
- **The matrix doesn't weigh effort.** A Q1 item that takes five minutes and one that takes five days both land in Q1. You still need to sequence and resource them.
