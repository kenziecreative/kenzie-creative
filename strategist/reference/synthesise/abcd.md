# ABCD

> A four-category system that sorts decisions by their scope and impact versus your familiarity with them — so you can match the right level of process and involvement to each decision.

## What It Is

ABCD places decisions on two axes: **Scope & Impact** (how widely the decision affects the organisation and how consequential it is) and **Level of Familiarity** (how well-understood the problem and solution space is — whether it's a known pattern or genuinely new territory). The four categories that result each call for a different decision-making approach:

- **A — Big and Novel:** High impact, low familiarity. These are the hardest decisions — consequential and without a clear playbook. They need careful deliberation, senior involvement, and often structured frameworks (SPADE, Evaluation) to work through.
- **B — Big and Known:** High impact, high familiarity. Consequential but well-understood — the organisation has done this before. These can move faster because the pattern is recognised, but still require senior sign-off.
- **C — Small and Novel:** Lower impact, low familiarity. Not catastrophic if wrong, but unfamiliar. Often best handled by an empowered owner who experiments, learns, and adjusts rather than over-analysing.
- **D — Small and Known:** Low impact, high familiarity. Routine decisions. These should be delegated entirely or systematised so they consume no senior attention at all.

## Why It Works

Most organisations apply the same decision-making process to every decision — long approval chains, committee meetings, status updates — regardless of what the decision actually warrants. The result is that small, routine decisions waste senior time while genuinely novel and high-stakes decisions get crowded into the same over-loaded process and don't receive enough careful thought.

ABCD works because it disaggregates two things that are commonly conflated: how *big* a decision is and how *hard* it is. A big decision the organisation has made dozens of times (B) is easier than a small one in genuinely unfamiliar territory (C). By separating them, the framework lets you right-size the process — move fast on D and B decisions, bring real rigor to A decisions, and empower teams to learn their way through C decisions rather than escalating them unnecessarily.

The framework also makes the cost of misclassification visible. Treating a D decision as an A wastes time; treating an A decision as a D risks a consequential mistake. Naming the categories creates a shared vocabulary for pushing back: "This is a D — let the team decide" or "This is an A — we need to slow down."

## How To Use It

1. **Name the decision clearly.** Write it as a specific question, not a topic. "Should we discontinue the Starter tier?" rather than "Pricing."
2. **Assess Scope & Impact.** If this decision goes wrong (or right), how many people, business units, customers, or dollars are affected? Is the consequence recoverable in months, or permanent?
3. **Assess Level of Familiarity.** Has the organisation (or you personally) navigated this exact type of decision successfully before? Is the causal chain well understood? High familiarity = known playbook; low familiarity = genuinely new terrain.
4. **Assign a letter: A, B, C, or D.**
5. **Match the process to the category.** A = deliberate, structured, senior. B = efficient, using established pattern, senior sign-off. C = empower an owner, experiment, review. D = delegate entirely or systematise.

## Worked Example

Acme Design faces four simultaneous decisions on a Tuesday:

- **Should Acme enter the enterprise market with a new B2B tier?** High impact (new segment, pricing restructure, new sales motion) and low familiarity (Acme has only ever sold direct-to-consumer). → **A.** This needs a structured analysis process, the founding team's involvement, and probably a framework like SPADE or Evaluation to work through seriously.
- **Should Acme renew its email marketing platform contract for another year?** High cost ($40k/yr) but a well-understood vendor relationship the team has managed twice. → **B.** Important enough for a decision owner to sign off, but the process can move quickly because it's familiar ground.
- **Should Acme try a new format — short "micro-courses" of 15 minutes or less — for one topic area?** Low stakes (one content line) but genuinely unfamiliar (Acme has never produced this format). → **C.** Assign the content lead as owner, run a small test, measure completion rate, and decide whether to expand based on evidence.
- **What font size should body text use in Acme's new email templates?** Low stakes, well within the design team's existing standards. → **D.** The design lead decides. No escalation needed.

In a single pass, Acme has routed four decisions to four different processes rather than booking a two-hour leadership meeting to discuss all four together.

## When To Use It

Use ABCD at the start of a decision-heavy period — a quarterly planning cycle, a strategic review, a product launch — to triage the decision backlog before choosing how to approach each one. It's also useful as a recurring team norm: if your organisation tends to over-escalate or under-escalate, making ABCD explicit gives everyone shared language for pushing decisions to the right level.

It pairs naturally with **Bezos** (which makes a similar but simpler two-way distinction between reversible and irreversible decisions) and with **Eisenhower** (which sorts tasks by urgency and importance rather than familiarity and scope). For A-category decisions, graduate to **SPADE** or **Evaluation** to work through the substance.

## Things To Watch Out For

- **Familiarity is easy to overestimate.** Decisions that *look* like familiar territory often hide a materially different variable — a new market, a different scale, a changed competitive environment. A B decision that's actually an A, handled with a B-level process, is one of the most common sources of expensive strategic errors.
- **The categories aren't stable.** A decision that's a C today (novel, low stakes) becomes a B tomorrow once the organisation has learned the pattern. Periodically recalibrating what counts as "known" is worth doing.
- **Scope and impact need to be assessed together.** A decision that affects many people but is easily reversible is different from one that affects fewer people but locks in a direction for years. The axis captures both — don't reduce it to headcount or budget alone.
- **D decisions still need an owner.** "Delegate or systematise" doesn't mean "ignore." Unowned D decisions accumulate and create noise; they just don't need senior time to resolve.

## Related Frameworks

- [Eisenhower](./eisenhower.md) — sorts tasks by urgency and importance; a complementary triage tool for managing workload rather than categorising decision type.
- [Bezos](./bezos.md) — a simpler two-way split (reversible vs. irreversible) that maps roughly to the familiarity axis; useful as a quick first filter before ABCD.
- [SPADE](./spade.md) — a structured process for working through A-category decisions: novel, high-stakes, requiring deliberation and stakeholder alignment.
