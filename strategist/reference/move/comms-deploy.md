# Comms Deploy

> A staged communications plan that maps message types and intensity across the phases of a deployment — so the right people hear the right thing at the right moment.

## What It Is

Comms Deploy is a visual planning tool for rolling out communications across a strategic initiative or product launch. It divides the deployment into sequential stages (typically four or five), assigns a communications intensity level to each stage, and specifies the actions, message types, and milestones that belong in each. The horizontal axis is time; a curve above it shows whether comms volume is high or low at each point; and within each stage, the team details the specific channels and messages being deployed.

## Why It Works

Most execution failures are, at root, communication failures. Either the message went out too early (before the audience could act on it), too late (after confusion had already set in), or at the same volume throughout (burying critical moments in noise). Comms Deploy works because it forces the team to think about communications as a sequenced campaign rather than a one-time announcement.

The intensity curve is the key insight the framework adds. Some stages genuinely warrant a communications blitz — launch day, a policy change taking effect, a leadership transition. Others warrant quiet preparation that happens mostly behind the scenes. Making that variation explicit lets the team allocate effort accurately and prevents both under-communication at critical moments and fatigue-inducing over-communication during routine phases.

## How To Use It

1. **Define the stages.** Break the initiative's timeline into four or five meaningful stages — for example, Prepare, Pre-launch, Launch, Stabilise, Embed. Each stage should represent a distinct chapter in the story you're telling stakeholders.
2. **Set the intensity.** For each stage, indicate how much communications activity is needed. This is often drawn as a wave or curve — low during preparation, peaking at launch, tapering during stabilisation.
3. **Detail the comms actions.** Within each stage, list the specific communication actions: what type of message (email, townhall, manager briefing, FAQ release, in-app notification), who it goes to, and who owns it.
4. **Mark milestones.** Call out the key milestone events in each stage — dates or triggers that anchor the comms to real moments in the deployment.
5. **Sequence for dependencies.** Check that later stages assume earlier comms have landed. If the launch message assumes audiences already know the backstory, the pre-launch stage needs to deliver that backstory first.

## Worked Example

Acme Design is launching its new cohort product to its existing subscriber base. The Comms Deploy plan spans five stages:

**Stage 1 — Internal Prepare (Weeks 1–4, low intensity).**
Actions: brief the customer success team on the new product and prepare an internal FAQ. Milestone: all CS staff have attended the briefing session.

**Stage 2 — Subscriber Warm-Up (Weeks 5–8, medium intensity).**
Actions: send a "something exciting is coming" teaser email to the full subscriber list; publish a blog post from the founder explaining the vision for live learning; share behind-the-scenes content on social. Milestone: teaser email delivered with open rate confirmed.

**Stage 3 — Launch (Weeks 9–10, high intensity).**
Actions: early-access enrolment email to top-tier subscribers (Day 1); full-list launch announcement email (Day 3); founder webinar (Day 5); paid social campaign activated; PR outreach to design education press. Milestone: both cohorts reach 80% enrolment capacity.

**Stage 4 — In-Cohort (Weeks 11–18, low intensity).**
Actions: weekly progress check-in emails to enrolled students; instructor communication templates active; one mid-cohort social post featuring a student story. Milestone: mid-cohort NPS survey sent and responses collected.

**Stage 5 — Post-Cohort & Re-Enrolment (Weeks 19–20, medium intensity).**
Actions: completion certificates sent; post-cohort testimonial campaign on social; "Cohort 2 now open" re-enrolment email to waitlist and completers. Milestone: re-enrolment email sent; Cohort 3 dates announced.

## When To Use It

Use Comms Deploy whenever an initiative will generate sustained audience impact over multiple weeks or months — a product launch, an organisational change, a pricing update, a system migration. It is especially valuable when multiple audiences need different messages at different times: the framework lets you plot one stage-based map and then differentiate the audience and message within each stage's detail row.

For one-off announcements, a simple message brief is enough. Comms Deploy earns its overhead when the rollout has enough stages that communications risks becoming uncoordinated or inconsistent without an explicit plan. Pair it with the **Execution Plan** to keep comms work tracked alongside the rest of the programme's activity.

## Things To Watch Out For

- The intensity curve is only useful if it drives real resourcing decisions. If every stage is labelled "high intensity," the curve is decorative and the team will exhaust itself or let things slip.
- Stages that are too broad ("Launch") can collapse weeks of distinct activity into one undifferentiated block. Split a stage if it contains more than one meaningful shift in audience, message, or channel.
- The plan is only as good as the owner assignment within it. A beautifully staged comms plan with no named person responsible for each action produces no communications at all.
- Communications has a lead time. Copy needs to be written, approvals obtained, designs completed. Build enough runway into early stages that Stage 3's launch-day email is approved before Stage 2 begins.
