# Decision Grid

> A structured grid that maps each workstream's key decisions against the weeks of a programme — so a steering committee knows exactly what it needs to decide, and when, to keep the work moving.

## What It Is

The Decision Grid is an execution planning tool for programmes that require regular governance decisions from a leadership body. It organises the programme into workstreams (rows) and time periods (columns — typically weeks), with a named lead for each workstream and a numbered list of key decisions that need to be made at specific points. The grid surfaces decisions before they become blockers, gives the steering committee a forward view of its agenda, and gives workstream leads certainty about when they need a call.

## Why It Works

In large or complex programmes, execution stalls most predictably at decision points — not because people are slow to act, but because decisions arrive at the wrong moment: either before the team has enough information to make them well, or long after they were needed and work has been blocked waiting. The Decision Grid works because it inverts that pattern by working backwards from the programme's target outcome to derive the set of decisions that must be made, and then locating each decision in the week where it genuinely needs to occur.

This "work back from the outcome" logic is the grid's most important structural move. Instead of scheduling the steering committee when it's convenient, the grid tells the steering committee when it needs to show up. That shifts governance from a meeting cadence into a decision cadence — a meaningfully different thing.

## How To Use It

1. **Define the workstreams.** Identify the major workstreams required to deliver the initiative (typically three to five). Assign a named lead to each.
2. **Set the time horizon.** Lay out the columns as weeks (or fortnights, or months, depending on programme length). A four- to eight-week look-ahead is typical for a governance grid.
3. **Work back from the outcome.** Starting from the end state or launch date, identify what decisions must be made for the programme to get there. For each workstream, list the key decisions in the order they need to be made.
4. **Place decisions in time.** Assign each decision to the week in which it needs to be made — not the week in which it would be convenient to make it.
5. **Number and track.** Number the decisions sequentially (1, 2, 3…) so the steering committee can track which are resolved and which are still open.
6. **Review at each governance meeting.** Use the grid as the live agenda: decisions due this week are on the table; decisions coming next week are flagged for preparation.

## Worked Example

Acme Design is running a three-workstream programme to launch its cohort product: Platform (engineering build), Curriculum (content and instructor), and Go-to-Market (marketing and CS). The programme lead is the Head of Product.

**Stream 1 — Platform. Lead: CTO.**
- Decision 1 (Week 1): Confirm the live-session video provider (build vs. integrate Zoom/Meet).
- Decision 2 (Week 3): Approve final technical spec for cohort booking and payments module.
- Decision 3 (Week 6): Sign off QA test results and confirm platform is launch-ready.

**Stream 2 — Curriculum. Lead: Head of Content.**
- Decision 1 (Week 1): Select the two launch cohort subjects (UX Fundamentals and Brand Identity confirmed).
- Decision 2 (Week 2): Approve first instructor contracts.
- Decision 3 (Week 5): Final curriculum sign-off for both launch cohorts.

**Stream 3 — Go-to-Market. Lead: Head of Marketing.**
- Decision 1 (Week 2): Confirm pricing for cohort tier ($499 per cohort).
- Decision 2 (Week 4): Approve launch campaign concept and channel plan.
- Decision 3 (Week 7): Go/no-go call on full launch (based on early-access enrolment data).

The steering committee — CEO, CTO, Head of Product, Head of Content, Head of Marketing — meets weekly for 30 minutes. The Decision Grid is the standing agenda: each week's decisions are tabled, resolved or escalated, and next week's decisions are flagged so leads know what to prepare.

## When To Use It

The Decision Grid is designed for larger organisations or programmes with multiple parallel workstreams where a central body (a steering committee, an executive sponsor, or a programme board) holds decision authority. It is most valuable when decisions are time-sensitive — when a delayed call in Week 3 creates a knock-on delay in Week 5. If the programme is small enough that one person can make all the decisions informally, the overhead isn't justified.

Use the **Execution Plan** to manage the activity-level roadmap within each workstream. The Decision Grid sits above it: not tracking tasks, but tracking the governance decisions that allow tasks to proceed.

## Things To Watch Out For

- A grid that lists every minor choice becomes unreadable. Restrict it to decisions that genuinely require steering-committee authority — not decisions the workstream lead can make independently.
- Working forward ("let's schedule the pricing decision for Week 4 because that's when we meet") produces a slower programme than working back from the outcome ("the campaign needs pricing signed off by Week 4 to brief the agency in Week 5"). Start at the end.
- Decisions without named owners stall. Each row in the grid should have a lead who is responsible for arriving at the meeting with a recommendation, not just a question.
- The grid is only useful if it is live. A Decision Grid that is updated once and then filed is a historical record, not a governance tool. Assign someone to update it weekly.
