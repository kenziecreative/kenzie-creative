# 3. Delivery

*Anchor area chapter — companion to the [Goal Setting Playbook](../playbook.md). For the body-systems metaphor, the scorecard mechanics, and how to choose where to focus, see the [Anchor Areas README](README.md).*

> **In the body:** Delivery is the circulatory system that carries value to the muscles.

A great recipe is just a piece of paper until you cook the meal. Delivery is the act of creating genuine customer value. It's the quality of your product, the effectiveness of your service, and the overall experience you provide. It's the promise you made in your marketing, fulfilled.

**The Belief:** As long as the product works, customers will be happy.

**The Reality:** The product is only one part of the experience. Delivery is about the entire journey, from the moment a customer signs up to the moment they achieve their desired outcome. It's about the quality of your support, the clarity of your documentation, and the thoughtfulness of your design. It's about how you make your customers feel at every touchpoint.

**The Practice:** Obsess over the details of the customer experience. Use your own product regularly. Talk to your customers and understand their frustrations. Invest in high-quality support and documentation. Your goal is not just to deliver a working product, but to deliver an experience that makes your customers feel seen, understood, and cared for.

**What It Means:** You stop thinking about your product as a collection of features and start thinking about it as a solution to a problem. You understand that the best products are not just functional, but also emotional. You build a product that people don't just use, but love.

**Signals of Strength:**

- You have a high customer satisfaction rate.
- Your customers are achieving their desired outcomes with your product.
- You have a low churn rate.

**Common Failure Modes:**

- Focusing on shipping new features at the expense of fixing bugs and improving existing ones.
- Treating customer support as a cost center instead of a value creator.
- Assuming you know what your customers want without actually talking to them.
