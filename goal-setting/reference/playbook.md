# The Goal Setting Playbook

*A canonical methodology for setting and achieving business goals — for small business owners working on individual professional goals or organizational goals.*

---

## Why this playbook exists

Most business goal-setting fails for the same reason. The frameworks were designed for corporations with dedicated strategy teams, not for small businesses where the owner wears seventeen hats and makes decisions between customer calls. Traditional goal-setting treats businesses like machines you can program: set the inputs, get the outputs. But businesses aren't machines. They're living systems made of people, and people are organic — unpredictable, adaptive, sometimes brilliantly irrational.

Force human systems into mechanical frameworks and one of two things happens. The goals get quietly abandoned because they don't account for how work actually gets done. Or the team burns out trying to fit organic reality into rigid targets that ignore how humans actually operate.

This playbook is a different approach. It's a six-stage **Setup Arc** for constructing goals that survive contact with reality, paired with a five-cadence **Ongoing Arc** for operating against them. It draws on Objectives and Key Results, the Three Time Horizons, the Four Laws of Behavior Change, and the pre-mortem — but it sequences them in a specific order, with specific constraints, that small businesses can actually execute.

It's not a template. It's a method. Work the stages, in order, the first time. Loop through them annually thereafter.

---

## The model underneath

Before the playbook itself, a short detour into the model of motivation it leans on. Andrew Huberman's podcast has covered goal-setting across several episodes; the account he popularizes describes goal-related behavior — across goals of different sizes, so the same machinery that helps you ship a wholesale channel helps you take a vacation — as a circuit of four brain systems working together:

- The **amygdala** processes fear and anxiety — including the anxiety of falling short.
- The **basal ganglia** runs the go/no-go action pathway — what you actually do.
- The **lateral prefrontal cortex** plans across timescales — bridging today's action and next year's outcome.
- The **orbitofrontal cortex** orients your emotional response around the goal.

In this model, **dopamine** ties the system together — the reward chemistry of visible progress, what makes pursuit feel worth continuing. The practical lever doesn't need the chemistry to be exactly right: structure your goals and systems so progress is visible and frequent, and pursuit stays rewarding.

A note on the four-region model: it's a practical summary, not a strict statement of neuroanatomy. The brain is more interconnected than any clean four-region story implies, and the research literature uses more careful language. Treat it as a working model — what it earns is four levers (anxiety, action, planning, emotion) the playbook actually pulls, and every one of them is verifiable from an operator's own experience without a brain scan: fear of falling short is real fuel; you do what's triggered, not what's intended; attention drifts to the urgent; motivation follows visible progress.

That's why the playbook works the way it does. The Setup Arc front-loads clarity and difficulty calibration so there's something specific to chase. The Ongoing Arc provides high-frequency feedback so the sense of progress arrives when it's earned. The pre-mortem routinely rehearses failure — research associated with the Balcetis lab found that people who visualized the obstacles substantially outperformed those who visualized only success. And the daily writing ritual makes the goal a daily act of attention and recommitment instead of a decoration.

Same machinery, different framework. The playbook is a way to use the habits and attention you already have.

## Foundational principles

These six principles run through every stage of the playbook. They're the worldview underneath the method.

**1. Organic, not mechanical.** Businesses are living systems made of people, not machines you can program. Every framework in this playbook accounts for that. If a framework assumes consistent energy, perfect information, or linear progress, it's lying to you. And the people *inside* the business are themselves organic — your employees aren't coming to work to make you successful, they're coming to build the life they want. The goals you set, and how you manage execution against them, only work when you remember that.

**2. Goals must account for how humans actually work.** Energy fluctuates. Sick days happen. Family emergencies don't check your calendar. Goals that assume away these realities aren't ambitious — they're naive.

**3. Finite vs. infinite games.** Finite games have winners, losers, and a finish line. Infinite games have no endpoint — the goal is to keep playing well. Your business is an infinite game. Stop optimizing for short-term wins that damage long-term sustainability.

**4. You fall to your systems, not rise to your goals.** Motivation is unreliable. Discipline is exhaustible. Design is durable. The businesses that win consistently don't want it more — they've built systems that make winning the default.

**5. Goal-setting is iterative.** The plan you set in January won't survive untouched until December. Your business is organic; your goals should be too. The playbook builds in feedback loops at five different cadences specifically so you can adapt.

**6. Never more than three goals at once.** Three goals can spawn an infinite number of Key Results to track, an infinite number of systems to maintain, and an infinite number of decisions to make. More than three is how people get overwhelmed, lose the thread, and abandon the system entirely. This is the hardest rule to follow and the most important one in the playbook.

> **Foundational reference:** These principles emerge from a deeper diagnosis of why traditional productivity systems fail. See `three-tyrants.md` for the full framework — it's optional reading, but it's the "why" underneath the rules.

# Part One: The Setup Arc

The Setup Arc is the construction sequence. You work through it once at launch and again at each annual vision check. It has six stages, and they go in order — each stage's output is the input to the next.

The stages are:

1. **Orient** — what game you're playing and what you actually want
2. **Horizon Mapping** — your now, next, and future time frames
3. **Anchor Areas** — the domains where you'll focus
4. **Goal Construction** — Objectives and Key Results
5. **System Design** — the weekly habits that make the goals inevitable
6. **Pre-mortem** — stress-testing before you launch

Resist the urge to skip ahead. The most common failure mode is starting at Stage 4 (writing OKRs) without doing Stages 1-3 first. That's how you end up with technically correct goals that produce terrible results.

---

## Stage 1 — Orient

Before you set a single goal, answer two questions: *What game are you playing?* and *What do you actually want?* Skip this stage and you'll spend the year executing brilliantly against the wrong target.

### The framework: Finite vs. Infinite Games

Finite games have winners, losers, and a finish line. Infinite games have no endpoint — the goal is to keep playing well. Most goal-setting advice was built for finite games: beat the competition, hit the number, dominate the market.

But your business isn't football. It's more like marriage, or parenting, or gardening. You're not trying to win. You're trying to build something that adapts when conditions change, doesn't collapse at the first obstacle, and creates value over time rather than extracting it all at once.

When you set finite-game goals inside an infinite game, you optimize for short-term wins at the expense of long-term sustainability. You burn out your team chasing quarterly targets. You damage customer relationships hitting revenue goals. You sacrifice what makes your business special for metrics that don't actually matter.

### The diagnostics: three tests every goal must pass

Pull out whatever goal you're considering. Run it through these:

**The 30-second test.** Can you explain this goal to someone — your team, your spouse, a stranger at a coffee shop — in 30 seconds or less? If it requires a PowerPoint to understand, it's too complicated to drive behavior. Goals should be clear enough that anyone can remember them and make decisions aligned with them.

**The human-aware test.** Does this goal assume superhuman conditions? Constant energy every day. No sick days or family emergencies. Instant process adoption. Perfect information. Linear progress with no setbacks. If yes to any of these, you're using machine logic on a human system.

**The should-want vs. actually-matters test.** Are you chasing this because it'd make you look successful, or because achieving it would actually move your business forward? "Should-want" goals are the ones you set because everyone else in your industry has them. They sound impressive. They produce no joy. And they almost never get achieved, because the underlying motivation isn't real.

### Difficulty calibration

A goal that's too easy generates no pull. A goal that's impossible generates anxiety and paralysis instead of action. Goals should be **challenging but possible**. If you'd bet money on hitting it without changing anything, it's too easy. If you can't imagine a path to it even with effort, it's too hard.

### The warning: vision boards are not goals

Writing down what you want and putting it on a corkboard *feels* like progress. It isn't. People who create vision boards and wish-lists tend to feel accomplished from the act of creating them — and that satisfaction takes the edge off actually doing the work. You feel like you've already won.

Vision boards are useful for clarifying what you want. They are not effective for achieving it. Don't confuse the two.

### Worked example

Maria came to a Boost Camp workshop with perfect SMART goals: revenue targets, customer acquisition numbers, social media metrics. Six months later, she'd hit some of them — and her team was burned out, she was working more hours than ever, and she wasn't sure any of it mattered. Her goals were perfect. Her business was struggling. The goals failed the should-want test. What she actually wanted was freedom — which meant building systems that let the business run without her, not just hitting bigger numbers. When she rebuilt her goals around what actually mattered, she stopped tracking revenue and started tracking *weeks without firefighting* and *systems that run without me*. The business grew faster after the rebuild — because the goals finally accounted for how humans actually work.

### Deliverable

A one-sentence direction that survives all three tests. Plain language. No jargon. This is the input to Stage 2.

## Stage 2 — Horizon Mapping

With direction in hand, you now need to set the time frames the goal will live inside. Without this, you'll either burn out chasing today's emergencies or drift forever toward a vision that never arrives.

### The framework: Three Time Horizons

Time in your business runs along three horizons. Most businesses get trapped in just one.

**Horizon 1 — Operational.** Execution mode. What needs to happen this week, this month, this quarter to keep the business running and moving forward. This is your daily task list, your weekly team meetings, your monthly financial review. The systems that keep the engine running.

**Horizon 2 — Strategic.** Building mode. What needs to change over the coming months or year to transform how the business works. This is where you build systems, launch offerings, hire key people, eliminate bottlenecks. You're not just running the business — you're improving it.

**Horizon 3 — Visionary.** Direction mode. Where the business is headed over the longer term. What are you building toward? What does your life look like if everything goes right?

Skip Horizon 1 and nothing gets done. Skip Horizon 2 and you stay stuck. Skip Horizon 3 and you build something you don't actually want.

### Calibrate horizons to your industry's velocity

The specific time periods matter less than the relationship between them. Pick horizons that match how fast your world changes:

- **AI startup in 2025:** this sprint (now), this quarter (next), 18-24 months out (future)
- **Marketing agency:** this month (now), this quarter (next), 3 years out (future)
- **Professional services firm:** this quarter (now), this year (next), 5 years out (future)

What matters is that you can clearly answer: *What am I executing today? What am I building toward in the medium term? Where does this all lead?*

### The time distribution principle: 70 / 25 / 5

You don't spend equal time on each horizon. You spend appropriate time on each:

- **70% Horizon 1** — most of your time is execution. This is right. This is your job.
- **25% Horizon 2** — a meaningful chunk is building. Improving systems. Working on the business.
- **5% Horizon 3** — small but critical. Checking direction. Making sure you're building what you actually want.

Most small business owners are at 90 / 9 / 1 when they start. Even moving to 80 / 15 / 5 transforms a business in a year.

### The one-week time audit

Before you can rebalance, you need to see where your time actually goes. For the next five business days, categorize your time at end of day:

- Horizon 1 work (executing today's commitments)
- Horizon 2 work (building systems and improvements)
- Horizon 3 work (thinking about direction)
- Reactive firefighting (unexpected problems that shouldn't exist with better systems)

Be honest. Most people discover they're 85% Horizon 1, 10% reactive firefighting, and 5% combined Horizon 2 and 3.

### The horizons are connected, not separate

Don't think of the horizons as separate buckets that get separate time blocks. Think of them as nested and feeding each other:

- Your Horizon 3 vision informs your Horizon 2 strategy. If your vision is "run the business from anywhere," your strategic projects are "document every process" and "hire a COO," not "open a second location."
- Your Horizon 2 strategy determines your Horizon 1 operations. If your quarterly goal is "launch the new service," your daily tasks build toward that, not just maintain status quo.
- Your Horizon 1 execution reveals what needs to change in Horizon 2. If you're drowning in customer-service requests every day, that's not just operational — it's a strategic signal.

### Worked example

David ran a 40-person marketing agency, working 70-hour weeks, no vacations. His Horizon 3 vision: manage operations 25 hours a week and take three weeks of vacation annually. That reframed his Horizon 2 (this year): hire a director of production, document every core process, implement a CRM. Which reframed his Horizon 1 (this quarter): document the client onboarding process two hours per week, interview candidates three hours per week, track team capacity one hour per week. Six months in, he was down to 45-hour weeks and had taken a real vacation.

### Deliverable

Named time periods for each of your three horizons, and a Horizon 3 vision statement. This vision is the input to the rest of the Setup Arc.

## Stage 3 — Anchor Areas

You can't set a goal without knowing which domain of the business it serves. Without Anchor Areas, "improve the business" becomes a goal — and you've already seen what happens to goals that vague.

### What Anchor Areas are

Anchor Areas are the small, fixed set of domains every business must actively manage. Picking your set forces you to look at the business honestly — instead of always working on the area that's screaming the loudest, you grade yourself across all of them and choose where to invest.

### The business anchor set (ships canonical)

For small businesses, there are seven — six functional areas of the business itself, plus the leader who runs it. The first six come from the GROWTH Framework; the seventh recognizes that the operator is part of the system.

**1. Demand Generation.** The work of getting noticed by the right people — content, partnerships, channel presence. Becoming the beacon, not chasing boats.

**2. Conversion.** Turning interest into action — the bridge between awareness and customer. Friction-removal more than persuasion.

**3. Delivery.** Creating genuine customer value — the quality of the product, the experience, the promise fulfilled.

**4. Operations.** The systems, processes, and workflows that let your team work together effectively. The sheet music of the business.

**5. Nurturing.** The ongoing work of customer relationships — retention, advocacy, community. The gardener tending what's already growing.

**6. Profit.** The financial sustainability that lets everything else continue. The root system, not the goal — but without it, nothing else survives.

**7. Personal Leadership.** Your own development, health, and energy as the leader. The driver of the system; you can't grow the business past your own capacity.

The first six are *functional areas of the business*. The seventh is *the leader who runs it*. The asymmetry is deliberate — you are qualitatively different from the functions you manage, and treating yourself as a peer anchor (rather than tucking your own development inside Operations or Strategic Planning) is the only way to keep it on the radar.

> **Companion reference:** Each anchor area has a full chapter — beliefs, realities, practices, signals of strength, and common failure modes — in `anchor-areas.md`. The playbook uses the names; the companion explains them.

### The Anchor Areas Scorecard

Rate yourself honestly on a 1-10 scale in each area:

- **1-3:** Completely reactive, no systems.
- **4-6:** Inconsistent, happens when you remember.
- **7-8:** Pretty good, occasional slip-ups.
- **9-10:** Rock solid, automatic.

Most owners are surprised by what they find. The area you assume is fine because it's not on fire is often the one that's quietly atrophying.

### Selection logic: pick by weakness, urgency, or impact

Three legitimate ways to choose where to focus:

- **Weakest score.** If Financial Management is a 3, that's vulnerability. Fix it.
- **Most urgent.** If Revenue Generation is a 4 and you're three months from a cash crunch, urgency wins.
- **Highest impact.** Sometimes a 6 in Customer Experience is your highest-leverage move because retention will compound for years.

Different starting points, same constraint: pick few.

### The constraint: max three anchor areas active at once

This is the second appearance of the three-goal rule, and it's the most enforceable place to apply it. You cannot meaningfully build systems in seven anchor areas simultaneously. You probably can't in four. Three is the ceiling. One is fine — sometimes the right move is to fix one thing well before touching anything else.

### Worked example

Marcus ran a 15-person digital agency. His scorecard: Demand Generation 3, Conversion 5, Delivery 8, Operations 6, Nurturing 5, Profit 4, Personal Leadership 5. His two weakest areas were Demand Generation and Profit. He chose Demand Generation first — most urgent, pipeline was thin. Profit would get attention next quarter. He explicitly did *not* try to fix all seven at once.

### Deliverable

Your full Anchor Areas Scorecard, plus the one to three areas you'll set goals in. These chosen areas feed Stage 4.

## Stage 4 — Goal Construction

Now you turn direction into testable goals. This is where most goal-setting advice starts — and it's the fourth stage here for a reason. Without Stages 1-3, you don't know what game you're playing, what time frames you're working in, or which domain you're improving. Constructing goals before that is gambling.

### The framework: OKRs

OKRs — Objectives and Key Results — has two parts working together.

**The Objective.** Qualitative. Aspirational. Clear enough to guide decisions but inspirational enough to get you out of bed on hard days. The Objective answers: *If we succeed this quarter or year, what will be different?*

**The Key Results.** Two to four specific, quantifiable outcomes that prove the Objective is happening. Key Results answer: *What metrics would prove we're succeeding?*

### Wishes vs. goals

A wish is something you want to happen. A goal is something you're going to make happen. The difference isn't motivation — it's specificity.

- "Grow my business" — wish.
- "Become the go-to consulting firm for mid-size tech companies in the Southeast" — goal.

- "Improve customer service" — wish.
- "Create a customer experience so good that clients refer us without being asked" — goal.

- "Make more money" — wish.
- "Build a business that generates $500K in annual revenue while I work 35-hour weeks" — goal.

The good versions are specific enough to guide decisions and inspirational enough to motivate work.

### Directions vs. destinations

Most people set goals that function like compass headings rather than destinations:

- "Increase revenue"
- "Get more customers"
- "Build a better team"

These tell you which way to walk, but not where you're going or how you'll know you've arrived. Six months later, you can't tell if you've made progress or just made motion.

A real goal has a destination (the Objective) and proof of arrival (the Key Results).

### Specificity is non-negotiable

Specificity is where the work happens. You cannot just set a state ("I want to be fit," "I want to be rich"). You need specificity built on verbs and actions, defined by *how much* and *by when*. Attention needs something concrete to track.

### Examples of objective + KR pairs

For a consulting firm:

- **Objective:** Become the go-to consulting firm for mid-size tech companies in the Southeast.
- **KR 1:** Sign 3 clients with annual contracts over $75K each.
- **KR 2:** Increase average project fee from $15K to $25K.
- **KR 3:** Achieve 50% of new business from referrals (not cold outreach).

For a product business:

- **Objective:** Build a sustainable business that doesn't depend on me being available 24/7.
- **KR 1:** Hire and train a customer service lead who handles 80% of inquiries.
- **KR 2:** Document all core processes so any team member can execute them.
- **KR 3:** Take a 2-week vacation without checking email or Slack.

### The constraint warning: max three objectives total

Third and final appearance of the three-goal rule. One objective per active anchor area, and never more than three objectives total across all anchor areas. If you've selected three anchor areas in Stage 3 and you're tempted to set two objectives per area, stop. You'll have six objectives and twelve to twenty-four Key Results to track. You will abandon the system within a quarter.

One objective per anchor area. Three total maximum. This is non-negotiable.

### Deliverable

One objective per active anchor area, each with 2-4 Key Results. Maximum three objectives total. These feed Stage 5.

## Stage 5 — System Design

Key Results are inert without systems. This is where the playbook diverges most sharply from standard goal-setting advice. Most frameworks stop at the goal. This one assumes the goal is meaningless until you've built the weekly habits that make it inevitable.

### The principle: you fall to your systems

You don't rise to your goals. You fall to your systems. If your system requires motivation, discipline, and perfect conditions, it will fail — not because you're weak, but because that's not how humans work.

The businesses and people that win consistently aren't unusually disciplined. They've designed systems that work even when motivation is gone.

Think about college football. Every year, 120+ programs set the exact same goal: win the championship. Only one wins. The teams in the championship conversation year after year aren't there because they want it more — every team wants to win. They're there because they have systems to win. They've built the culture, the recruiting process, the training regimen, and the playbook that puts them in position to compete every single year. The goal matters. But the system is what makes the goal possible. Your business works the same way.

### A system needs a trigger or it's a hope

This is the practical test that separates real systems from wishes. A real system has one of three things attached to it:

- **A time trigger:** "Every Monday at 9am, I will..."
- **A location trigger:** "When I sit at my desk in the morning, I will..."
- **A habit-stack trigger:** "Right after my morning coffee, I will..."

If your system requires you to remember to do it, it's not a system. It's a hope. And hopes get displaced by whatever's urgent.

### The framework: the Four Laws of Behavior Change

Adapted from James Clear's *Atomic Habits*, these four laws determine whether a habit sticks. Apply all four to every system you build.

**Law 1: Make it Obvious.** You can't do a habit you don't remember to do. Don't rely on memory — build triggers into your environment.

- Instead of "I should do business development," set a recurring calendar block every Monday at 9am titled "Outreach Hour."
- Instead of "I need to review our numbers," configure automatic monthly reports that hit your inbox on the 1st.
- Instead of "I should check in with my team," schedule recurring one-on-ones directly in their calendars.

**Law 2: Make it Attractive.** You're more likely to do things that feel rewarding. Pair habits you need to do with things you want to do.

- Do your weekly planning session at your favorite coffee shop, not at your desk.
- Reserve a specific high-quality coffee or tea for the start of the habit.
- Listen to a favorite podcast only during exercise or commute time.

**Law 3: Make it Easy.** The harder something is to do, the less likely you'll do it. Remove friction.

- Pre-write email templates for common outreach scenarios — don't start from scratch every time.
- Create a one-page financial dashboard, not a 47-tab spreadsheet you have to rebuild monthly.
- Set up a shared document for one-on-ones with standing questions — don't reinvent the meeting structure every time.

**Law 4: Make it Satisfying.** You repeat behaviors that feel rewarding in the moment. Build in immediate feedback.

- Track weekly habit execution on a simple checklist — checking the box is a small, immediate win.
- Report completed execution to one trusted accountability partner *after* you've done the work.
- Keep a "done" list, not just a to-do list.

### The tactical tool: narrow your visual focus

When you're actually doing the work, physically narrow your visual field. Close extra browser tabs. Turn your monitor to face away from the window. Use a single-tasking app. Narrowing your literal field of view narrows your attention with it. It's a small thing that compounds.

### The accountability nuance — get this right

There's a popular myth, partly correct, that you shouldn't tell anyone your goals. The research — covered in the Huberman Lab podcast and originally surfaced in work by Peter Gollwitzer — is actually more specific than that.

**Don't:** broadcast your goals to your social circle for encouragement *before* you've executed. The validation lands like an accomplishment before anything has been done — announcing the goal satisfies some of the drive that should have gone into pursuing it.

**Do:** report completed execution to one trusted accountability partner *after* the work is done. This is satisfaction (Law 4), not premature reward. Marcus texting his coach "5/5 this week" works because it celebrates execution, not intention.

The difference matters. One pattern undermines goal pursuit. The other reinforces it.

### The constraint: one system per anchor area at first

You cannot design six systems at once. You probably can't sustain three. Start with one per active anchor area. Get it working consistently for a month before adding the second.

### Worked example

Marcus chose Revenue Generation as his weakest area. His system: every Monday, 9-11am blocked as "CEO Monday" for business development. Made it Obvious with a recurring calendar block his assistant could not override. Made it Attractive by working from his favorite coffee shop with the good espresso. Made it Easy by pre-writing five outreach templates. Made it Satisfying by texting his coach "5/5 this week" when he hit his outreach number. His first version blocked four hours; it lasted three weeks. Version 3 was two hours, earlier in the day, with a permission rule that he could move it to Tuesday if Monday was on fire — but only if he actually moved it. Version 3 works.

### Deliverable

One weekly system per active anchor area, designed against all four laws, each with an explicit trigger. These systems are the inputs to Stage 6.

## Stage 6 — Pre-mortem (Launch version)

Your goals are set. Your systems are designed. Before you launch, you do one more thing: you assume the whole thing fails, and figure out what would have killed it.

### Why this works

This is the most counterintuitive piece of the playbook, and it has the strongest evidence behind it. Research from the Balcetis lab — covered extensively by Andrew Huberman — shows that **foreshadowing failure nearly doubles the probability of achieving a goal** compared to focusing only on success. Exact effect sizes vary across studies, and the pre-mortem protocol below is an adaptation of the underlying research rather than a direct replication of any single experiment. But the direction and magnitude of the finding is well-established.

The working explanation is practical. Visualizing success feels like winning, and the satisfaction fades fast while blunting the urgency to act — you've already enjoyed the ending. Mentally rehearsing what could go wrong, and how you'd respond, does the opposite: it sharpens alertness, and it leaves you holding responses you've already thought through *before* the problem arrives.

This isn't pessimism. It's preparation.

### The framework: the Pre-mortem

A post-mortem analyzes what went wrong after failure. A pre-mortem assumes failure before you start.

**Step 1: Set the failure scene.** Fast-forward to the end of the period. Write this down: *"It's [end date]. We failed. We didn't achieve [objective]."* Make it specific and visceral.

**Step 2: Brainstorm external causes.** What outside the business could kill this? Markets, competitors, suppliers, customers, regulatory changes, partner failures.

**Step 3: Brainstorm internal causes.** What inside the business could kill this? Execution gaps, team issues, resource constraints, capacity limits, capability gaps.

**Step 4: Surface hidden assumptions.** What are you assuming will be true that might not be? List them explicitly. Assumptions you can't articulate are the ones that kill you.

**Step 5: Prioritize by likelihood × impact.** Rate each potential failure on likelihood (low/medium/high) and impact (low/medium/high). Focus on anything that's high in either dimension.

**Step 6: Build mitigations with explicit triggers.** For each high-priority risk, define a mitigation in the form *"If X happens, then Y."* This is the critical move — a mitigation without a trigger is a hope. A mitigation with a trigger is a system.

### Worked example

Rachel wanted to launch a wholesale channel for her product business: 20 retail partners, $150K wholesale revenue, 40% margins by year-end. Her pre-mortem surfaced four high-priority risks:

- Manufacturer can't scale → mitigation: qualify a backup manufacturer before launching.
- Retailers want exclusivity she can't offer → mitigation: pre-draft a clear non-exclusive partnership policy.
- 60-90 day payment terms kill cash flow → mitigation: require 50% deposit on first orders; build 90-day cash buffer before scaling.
- Margins drop below sustainable threshold → mitigation: set a hard 35% margin floor; if breached, pause acquisition.

Reality: her manufacturer hit quality issues in June and her biggest retail partner went out of business. Because she'd qualified a backup, she switched in three weeks instead of three months. Because she'd set a margin floor, she paused expansion rather than chasing volume into unprofitable territory. She finished the year at 12 retailers, $85K revenue, 36% margins — below her original ambitious targets, but with a sustainable wholesale channel intact. Without the pre-mortem, the year would have killed the business.

### Deliverable

Revised Key Results if the pre-mortem surfaced unrealistic targets, plus a list of named mitigation triggers ("if X, then Y") for each high-priority risk. These mitigations stay with you into the Ongoing Arc.

# Part Two: The Ongoing Arc

Setup is done. Goals are constructed, systems are designed, mitigations are named. Now you operate.

The Ongoing Arc has five cadences, from daily to annual. They're nested — each cadence's output feeds the next one up. The two highest-frequency cadences (daily and weekly) are the most important. They're also the easiest to skip, which is why they have to be the easiest to do.

The cadences are:

1. **Daily** — write the goal
2. **Weekly** — pulse check (5 min, Mondays)
3. **Monthly** — KR review (1 hour)
4. **Quarterly** — system + planning review + recurring pre-mortem (half day to full day)
5. **Annual** — vision check (1 day, loops back to Stage 1)

Total time investment: roughly 4-5 hours per week beyond normal operations. About 10% of a 40-hour week. That 10% is what transforms you from operator to owner.

---

## The Daily Ritual: write the goal

This is the highest-frequency touchpoint in the playbook. Writing out your goal by hand, every day, is the cheapest reliable way to keep it genuinely in front of you — a daily act of attention and recommitment, not decoration. But there's a catch.

### Why the Post-it on the fridge fails

You stop seeing what never changes. If you write your goal once and stick it on the fridge, you'll see it every morning for a week. After that, it becomes part of the visual furniture — you'll stop registering it. The note is still there. You've stopped seeing it.

### The protocol: write it out every day, change the location

The fix is to **write the goal out by hand every day**, and to **vary where you do it**. Different room, different notebook, different time of day. Writing by hand is slower and more deliberate than typing or re-reading — it forces you to actually attend to the words; the location variation prevents the habituation that turns the goal into wallpaper.

This takes 60 to 90 seconds. It is the cheapest, highest-impact daily habit in the playbook.

### What to write

Write the Objective for each active goal. You can also write the current quarter's most important Key Result, or the system you're committed to running this week. Keep it short — this is about embedding, not journaling.

### Pairing with the Four Laws

Apply the same system-design principles to the daily ritual itself:

- **Obvious:** keep a small notebook in a visible place — the location varies day to day.
- **Attractive:** use a pen you like; do it with your morning coffee.
- **Easy:** under two minutes, no formatting requirements.
- **Satisfying:** check off the day on a habit tracker after writing.

### Deliverable

A short daily entry, every day. That's it. The compounding happens over weeks and months.

---

## The Weekly Pulse Check

Every Monday, five minutes, one question.

### The question

> *"Last week, did I execute my systems? If yes, am I seeing progress in my Key Results? If no, what needs to change?"*

That's it. One question. Five minutes.

### Why this small check is the most important habit

Most goals fail not because the goal was wrong, but because there was no feedback loop. You set a goal. You get busy. Three months pass. You forgot what you were trying to accomplish.

The weekly pulse does three things:

- **Keeps the goal visible.** You can't forget what you review every week.
- **Reveals broken systems fast.** If you're executing but not seeing progress, you know by week 3, not week 12.
- **Creates compounding wins.** Small weekly progress compounds into 52 wins per year vs. four quarterly "oh crap" moments.

### This is intelligent adaptation, not guilt-and-discipline accountability

The pulse check isn't a judgment ritual. You're not asking "did I work hard enough?" You're asking "is the design working?" If you executed the system but didn't see progress, the system is wrong. If you didn't execute the system, the design is wrong. Either way, the data tells you what to change.

### Deliverable

A short note in your goal document each Monday answering the three sub-questions. Five minutes, no more.

---

## The Monthly KR Review

Once a month, one hour, deeper question.

### The question

*Are we on track? And if not, is the goal wrong, or is the execution wrong?*

This is the diagnostic move the pulse check can't make in five minutes. The pulse check catches problems; the monthly review classifies them.

### The two failure modes

- **Execution is wrong.** You're not running the system consistently, or the system isn't designed well. Fix: revise the system using the Four Laws. Don't change the goal.
- **Goal is wrong.** You're running the system consistently and still not seeing KR progress. Fix: revise the KR (or the Objective, if the misfit is deeper). Don't blame execution.

Most owners default to blaming execution when the goal is actually wrong. The monthly review is your chance to catch this.

### Deliverable

A short written assessment per active goal: on track / off track, classification of why, and the specific change you're making (if any) for the next month.

---

## The Quarterly Review

Every 90 days, half a day to a full day. This is the deepest cadence below the annual vision check.

### What happens here

Three things in sequence:

**Re-run the Anchor Areas Scorecard.** Has anything moved? Did the area you chose to focus on actually improve? Are new vulnerabilities surfacing? This is a fresh diagnostic, not a reaffirmation of last quarter's choices.

**Audit the systems.** For each active system, ask: is this still moving the Key Results, or has it become ceremony? Systems can decay into theater — you're doing the meeting but not gaining the insight. Cut or revise systems that have stopped producing.

**Plan the next quarter's objectives.** Set the new Objective and Key Results for the next 90 days. Same constraints apply: maximum three objectives, one per active anchor area, must pass the three diagnostic tests from Stage 1.

### The Pre-mortem (Recurring version)

The launch pre-mortem (Stage 6) assumed you knew nothing about how the goal would unfold. The recurring pre-mortem is different — you now have a quarter of real data on what's actually breaking. Re-run the pre-mortem with that data:

- Which of the original mitigation triggers fired? Did they work?
- What unexpected risks surfaced that you didn't anticipate?
- What's most likely to kill the *next* quarter, given what you've now seen?

This produces a revised mitigation list for the next 90 days. The launch version is broad and theoretical. The recurring version is sharp and specific.

### Deliverable

Updated Anchor Areas Scorecard, list of system changes (keep/revise/cut), the next quarter's Objectives and Key Results, and a revised mitigation list from the recurring pre-mortem.

---

## The Annual Vision Check

Once a year, one full day. This is where the Ongoing Arc loops back into the Setup Arc.

### What happens here

Reopen Stage 1. Ask the original questions again:

- *What game am I playing? Is this still the right game?*
- *What do I actually want? Is the Horizon 3 vision still true?*
- *Does the direction from last year still survive the three tests?*

Most years, the answer is *yes, with refinements.* Some years — after a major life change, business shift, or market disruption — the answer is *no, the vision needs to change.*

When the vision changes, every downstream stage gets reopened. Horizon Mapping, Anchor Areas, Objectives — all of it cascades from a revised Horizon 3.

### Why this matters

Without the annual vision check, you can spend a decade executing brilliantly against a goal you outgrew years ago. The check is the only thing that prevents that.

### Deliverable

Either a confirmed continuation of the existing vision, or a revised Horizon 3 vision statement. If the vision changed, you re-run the Setup Arc from Stage 2.

# Part Three: How to use this playbook

A few principles for working with the method itself.

**Work the stages in order the first time.** The most common failure mode is skipping ahead to Stage 4 because OKRs feel familiar. Don't. Stages 1-3 are what make Stage 4 useful.

**Don't perfect each stage before moving on.** Get to a working draft, move to the next stage, expect to revise. Version 3 of any stage is usually the one that works. Version 1 rarely is.

**The three-goal rule is the rule that matters most.** Three anchor areas, three objectives, three systems to start. If you remember nothing else, remember this. It's the constraint that prevents the system from collapsing under its own weight.

**The daily and weekly cadences are non-negotiable.** Monthly, quarterly, and annual can slip a week without breaking anything. If you skip the daily writing and the weekly pulse for a month, the whole system stops working.

**The pre-mortem is not optional.** It is the highest-evidence intervention in the playbook. Fifteen minutes of pre-mortem thinking will save you months of scrambling.

**Iterate ruthlessly.** Your business is organic. Your systems should be too. The goal isn't the perfect plan; it's the plan that gets adapted fastest in response to real conditions.

# Appendix A: Quick Start — Rough V1 in 90 Minutes

This playbook is designed to be worked carefully over a week or two. But if you want a rough first pass in a single sitting, here's the compressed sequence. The goal is not a polished V1 — it's a working V1 you can iterate on. Remember: Version 3 is the one that works. V1 just gets you started.

Set a 90-minute timer. Have a notebook open. Don't second-guess.

**15 min — Stage 1: Orient.** Write down what you actually want for the business over the next quarter or year. One sentence. Then run it through the three tests: can you explain it in 30 seconds, does it assume superhuman conditions, is it a should-want or actually-matters? Revise once.

**15 min — Stage 2: Horizon Mapping.** Name your three time horizons (e.g., this quarter, this year, three years). Write a one-paragraph Horizon 3 vision: if everything goes right, what does the business look like, and what does your life look like?

**10 min — Stage 3: Anchor Areas.** Score yourself 1-10 across all six anchor areas. Don't overthink — first instinct is usually right. Pick ONE area to focus on first. You can add a second later. You do not need three for V1.

**20 min — Stage 4: Goal Construction.** For your chosen anchor area, write one Objective. Then write two to three Key Results. Be specific. Make sure they're measurable — "I'll know we hit it because X."

**15 min — Stage 5: System Design.** Design one weekly system for that Objective. Apply each of the Four Laws in one sentence each: When and where will it happen (Obvious)? What's pleasant about it (Attractive)? What friction can you remove (Easy)? How will you track it (Satisfying)?

**15 min — Stage 6: Pre-mortem.** Assume failure at the end of the period. Brainstorm the top three things most likely to kill this goal. For each, write a mitigation in "if X, then Y" form.

That's V1. Tomorrow morning, do your daily writing. Next Monday, do your weekly pulse check. In 30 days, run the monthly review and decide what to revise.

You'll iterate. That's not failure — that's the process.

# Appendix B: Restart Protocol — When You Fall Off

You will fall off. Sometime in the next twelve months, you'll skip three daily writings in a row, miss two Monday pulse checks, blow past a monthly review, and look up six weeks later wondering if the system is broken.

The system is not broken. You are not broken. Falling off is data, not a verdict.

When it happens, here's the restart:

**1. Skip the guilt.** Treat the lapse the way you'd treat a missed workout — note it, restart Monday, move on. Re-litigating why you fell off wastes the time the playbook needs.

**2. Re-score your Anchor Areas.** Your scores have moved. Some areas you thought were 7s have decayed to 5s. Some areas you ignored have become urgent. Ten minutes, honest scoring.

**3. Restart with one system, not all of them.** The temptation is to relaunch at full intensity. Don't. Choose the single most important system in your most important anchor area and run only that one for two weeks. Once it's holding, add the next.

**4. Resume the daily writing.** Start tomorrow morning. New notebook page, new location. Don't try to "catch up" past missed days — the practice resets clean.

**5. Skip the next quarterly review if it's close.** If you fell off going into a planning quarter, don't immediately try to run the full quarterly review. Let one weekly pulse cycle clean first. Then do the next monthly. Then the next quarterly.

The restart protocol is itself a system. It exists so that falling off doesn't end the practice — it just interrupts it.

# Appendix C: Data Structures — Object Reference

This appendix defines the core objects in the playbook and how they relate. It exists to make the methodology portable: into a Notion workspace, a custom dashboard, an AI agent that runs the cadences, or any other implementation. It's also the canonical reference for anyone building tooling around this playbook.

### Core objects

**Direction** *(output of Stage 1)*
- `text`: one-sentence statement of what you actually want
- `passes_30_second_test`: bool
- `passes_human_aware_test`: bool
- `passes_actually_matters_test`: bool
- `created_at`, `revised_at`

**HorizonSet** *(output of Stage 2)*
- `horizon_1_label`: e.g., "this quarter"
- `horizon_2_label`: e.g., "this year"
- `horizon_3_label`: e.g., "three years out"
- `horizon_3_vision`: paragraph-length vision statement
- `current_time_distribution`: {h1_pct, h2_pct, h3_pct, reactive_pct} — captured from the time audit

**AnchorArea**
- `name`: string (one of the canonical seven for the business set: Demand Generation, Conversion, Delivery, Operations, Nurturing, Profit, Personal Leadership)
- `description`: string
- `current_score`: 1-10
- `is_active`: bool — whether goals are currently being set against this area
- `score_history`: array of {date, score}

**Objective**
- `id`, `title`, `description`
- `anchor_area_id`: foreign key to AnchorArea
- `period_start`, `period_end`
- `status`: planning | active | achieved | missed | abandoned | superseded — the closed
  statuses are dispositions, set at the quarterly closeout; every Objective that leaves
  `active` gets one, plus `lessons`
- `goal_contract`: owner, baseline + evidence source, target + deadline, leading/lagging
  indicators, measurement delay, countermetric (what must not deteriorate), capacity
  commitment, dependencies, non-goals, legitimate-revision conditions
- `revision_history`: append-only records of { date, field, original value, actual at
  change, new value, reason } — a revision never overwrites the original commitment
- **Constraint:** max 3 Objectives in `active` status at any time across all anchor areas.

**KeyResult**
- `id`, `description`
- `objective_id`: foreign key to Objective
- `metric_name`: e.g., "qualified leads"
- `baseline_value`, `target_value`, `current_value`
- `status`: on_track | off_track | achieved | revised (revisions append to the Objective's
  `revision_history`)

**System**
- `id`, `description`
- `objective_id`: foreign key to Objective (may also link to specific KRs)
- `trigger_type`: time | location | habit_stack
- `trigger_detail`: e.g., "Mondays 9-11am" or "after morning coffee"
- `four_laws`: { obvious, attractive, easy, satisfying } — short string per law
- `version`: integer — increments on revision (Version 3 works)
- `status`: active | paused | retired | replaced — `paused` is the Restart Protocol's holding state

**Mitigation** *(output of Stage 6 and the Quarterly Recurring Pre-mortem)*
- `id`, `risk_description`
- `objective_id` or `system_id`: what it protects
- `likelihood`: low | medium | high
- `impact`: low | medium | high
- `trigger_condition`: the "if X" clause
- `action`: the "then Y" clause
- `monitored_signal`, `threshold`, `check_frequency` (weekly | monthly | quarterly),
  `owner`, `deadline` — what's watched, what counts as fired, and which cadence sweeps it
  (weekly slots are scarce — at most ~3, so the pulse stays five minutes)
- `status`: untriggered | triggered_active | resolved (resolution requires
  `response_evidence`); `last_checked` records when the signal was actually read — an
  unchecked sweep is recorded as unchecked, never as clear
- `source`: launch_premortem | recurring_premortem

### Cadence entries

**DailyEntry**
- `date`, `location` (varies day to day), `written_text`

**WeeklyPulseEntry**
- `date`
- `per_objective`: array of { objective_id, system_executed: yes | no | unknown | mixed,
  kr_progressing: yes | no | unknown | n/a | mixed } — one record per active Objective; a
  half-answer is recorded as `unknown`, never inferred; `mixed` (divergence within one
  goal) carries a one-line note naming the split
- `mitigations_checked`: per weekly mitigation: fired ID | clear | unchecked (with date)
- `what_needs_to_change`: text (only if anything is no, unknown, or mixed)

**MonthlyReview**
- `date`
- `per_objective_assessment`: array of { objective_id, on_track, classification: goal_wrong | execution_wrong | both, change_made }

**QuarterlyReview**
- `date`
- `closeouts`: array of { objective_id, disposition: achieved | missed | abandoned | superseded, final_actuals, lessons } — required for every ending Objective before new ones are set
- `updated_scorecard`: snapshot of AnchorArea scores
- `system_changes`: array of { system_id, action: keep | revise | retire }
- `next_quarter_objectives`: array of new Objective IDs
- `recurring_premortem_mitigations`: array of new Mitigation IDs

**AnnualVisionCheck**
- `date`
- `vision_continued`: bool
- `revised_horizon_3_vision`: text (only if `vision_continued` is false)
- Triggers re-entry into the Setup Arc starting at Stage 2 if the vision changed.

### Key relationships

- **Setup flow:** Direction → HorizonSet → AnchorAreas (active subset) → Objectives → KeyResults + Systems → Mitigations.
- **Ongoing cadence references:** WeeklyPulse → System; MonthlyReview → Objective; QuarterlyReview → AnchorAreas + Systems + Objectives + Mitigations.
- **The three-goal constraint** is enforced at the Objective level (`status = active`), with secondary enforcement at the AnchorArea level (`is_active = true` capped at 3) and the System level (one per active anchor area at initial setup).

This object model is implementation-neutral. It can back a database, a Notion workspace, an Airtable base, or an LLM agent's state.

# Appendix D: The playbook on one page

**Foundational principles**

1. Organic, not mechanical.
2. Goals must account for how humans actually work.
3. Finite vs. infinite games — you're playing the infinite one.
4. You fall to your systems, not rise to your goals.
5. Goal-setting is iterative.
6. Never more than three goals at once.

**Setup Arc** *(work in order)*

1. **Orient** — finite/infinite framing + three tests + difficulty calibration → one-sentence direction.
2. **Horizon Mapping** — three time horizons + 70/25/5 + time audit → named horizons + Horizon 3 vision.
3. **Anchor Areas** — scorecard + selection logic → 1-3 active anchor areas.
4. **Goal Construction** — OKRs, one Objective per anchor area, max 3 total → Objective + KRs.
5. **System Design** — Four Laws + triggers + visual focus + accountability nuance → one weekly system per anchor area.
6. **Pre-mortem (Launch)** — assume failure + brainstorm + prioritize + mitigation triggers → revised KRs + named mitigations.

**Ongoing Arc** *(run continuously)*

- **Daily** (60-90 sec): write the goal by hand, vary the location.
- **Weekly** (5 min, Mondays): pulse check — *executed? progressing? what needs to change?*
- **Monthly** (1 hr): KR review — *on track? goal wrong or execution wrong?*
- **Quarterly** (half day to full day): re-score anchors + audit systems + plan next quarter + recurring pre-mortem.
- **Annual** (1 day): vision check — *still the right game? still the right vision?* Loops back to Stage 1.

**The rule that matters most:** never more than three goals at once.
