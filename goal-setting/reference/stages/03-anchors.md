# Stage 3 — Anchor Areas

*Setup Arc stage reference. Full prose in [playbook.md → Stage 3](../playbook.md#stage-3--anchor-areas). Companion depth per area in [../anchor-areas/](../anchor-areas/README.md). The engine reads this file to run the stage.*

## Purpose

You can't set a goal without knowing which domain of the business it serves. Anchor Areas force an honest look across the whole business — instead of always working the area screaming loudest, the user grades all of them and chooses where to invest. Anchors produces a scored scorecard plus the one to three areas the user will set goals in, feeding Stage 4.

## Framework: the seven business anchor areas

Six functional areas plus the leader who runs the business:

1. **Demand Generation** — getting noticed by the right people.
2. **Conversion** — turning interest into action.
3. **Delivery** — creating genuine customer value.
4. **Operations** — the systems that let the team work together.
5. **Nurturing** — the ongoing work of customer relationships.
6. **Profit** — the financial sustainability that funds everything else.
7. **Personal Leadership** — the leader's own development, health, and energy.

The seventh is asymmetric on purpose — the leader is qualitatively different from the functions, and treating it as a peer anchor is the only way to keep it on the radar. Each area has a full chapter in [`../anchor-areas/`](../anchor-areas/README.md); point the user to the chapter for any area they don't fully understand before they score it, and to the chapter for their chosen area after, to inform what kind of Objective would move it.

## Diagnostics: the Anchor Areas Scorecard

Rate each area honestly, 1–10:

- **1-3:** Completely reactive, no systems.
- **4-6:** Inconsistent, happens when you remember.
- **7-8:** Pretty good, occasional slip-ups.
- **9-10:** Rock solid, automatic.

Don't average — look at the **spread**. Score what *is*, not what *should be* (the signals of strength in each chapter are the check). Personal Leadership decays silently; give it an extra honest look.

## Selection logic: pick by weakness, urgency, or impact

Three legitimate ways to choose where to focus:

- **Weakest score** — fix the vulnerability.
- **Most urgent** — even if not weakest, a six-week-out cash crunch makes Profit win.
- **Highest leverage** — a 7 in Conversion can be the highest-leverage move because everything compounds through it.

Different starting points, same constraint: pick few.

## Constraint enforcement (HARD)

- **Max three active Anchor Areas.** If the user tries to mark a fourth as active, **refuse** — require them to deactivate one first. One active anchor is often the right answer; three is the ceiling. This is the first concrete appearance of the three-goal rule and the most enforceable place to apply it.

## Posture notes

- **Friction half:** a scorecard where everything is a 7 is usually scoring what-should-be, not what-is. Push the user to use the signals of strength as a reality check, and to be honest about Personal Leadership.
- **Lane half:** *which* anchor to prioritize is the user's decision once they've seen the scorecard honestly. Surface the three selection logics; don't pick for them.

## Deliverable

The full Anchor Areas Scorecard (all seven scored) plus the one to three areas marked active. Write the scorecard to `goals/scorecard.md` (with a dated score snapshot for history) and record the active set there and in `goals/STATE.md`. The active areas feed Stage 4.
