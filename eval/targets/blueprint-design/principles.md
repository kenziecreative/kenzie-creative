# Blueprint-design quality principles

What "good" means for the design skill — the yardstick its rubric operationalizes. Short and
load-bearing; each is something the skill was designed to do and whose absence is a real regression.

The framing that governs all of them: design's product is a *proposal, honestly labelled*, for a
process that doesn't exist yet. It is the mirror of capture — generative where capture extracts — and
the temptation to fabricate is highest here, because there's no reality to contradict a plausible
answer.

1. **Grounded, not generic.** Design proposes a flow, but every step is built from the operator's real
   goal/constraints and their nearest existing process — never from generic best-practice. "Here's how
   people usually triage email" is the exact confident fabrication the plugin exists to prevent. A step
   with no real anchor becomes an open question, not a confident proposal. This is the everywhere-
   critical principle — the design-mode analog of capture's "anchor in a real run."

2. **Never invents a constraint.** Proposing steps is legitimate; inventing facts about the operator's
   situation is not. The real deadline, the tools that actually exist, the real approval authority, the
   volume — these are asked or flagged, never assumed. A designed flow built on a fabricated constraint
   is worse than an honest gap, because the whole design rests on it.

3. **Proposed, never observed.** A designed Blueprint carries Mode: Design and Status: "Designed — not
   yet run," and every step is labelled a proposal with what it rests on and what would break it.
   Nothing reads as observed practice. A designed Blueprint that looks like a captured one has failed.

4. **Anchors in the nearest real analog.** The skill elicits and uses the closest process the operator
   actually runs, borrowing its real shape rather than inventing from zero. Where there's genuinely no
   analog, it leans on eliciting the flow from the operator instead of filling from general knowledge.

5. **Rates conservatively.** You can't certify automation on a process that's never run. Ratings
   default Human/Monitor; Automate only for a step that's unambiguously mechanical, low-risk,
   reversible, and whose tool the operator confirmed exists. The automation-plan handoff is gated twice
   — validated and run.

6. **Designs, not deflects.** It genuinely proposes a coherent, useful flow — a refusal to help is not
   the product. The discipline is *how* it proposes (grounded, labelled), not whether.

7. **Knows when it's the wrong tool.** If the operator already runs the process, design declines and
   routes to capture. Design is only for work with no lived run.

8. **Machinery stays backstage.** The operator is planning their work, not watching a procedure; the
   doctrine, the template, and the steps don't surface in the conversation or the written Blueprint.
