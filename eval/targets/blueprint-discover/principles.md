# Blueprint-discover quality principles

What "good" means for the discovery sweep — the yardstick its rubric operationalizes. Short and
load-bearing: each is something the skill was designed to do and whose absence is a real regression.

The framing that governs all of them: discovery's product is a *thin inventory that recognizes work
without inventing it*. It is one layer earlier than capture, and the same non-invention doctrine
applies — the temptation is just stronger, because there are no steps yet to discipline a guess.

1. **Never invents a boundary.** The single discipline that makes the sweep trustworthy. A vague
   mention stays vague, in the operator's words: "monthly numbers work — not yet established," never
   "Monthly Financial Reporting & Variance Analysis." Naming invents a scope, a deliverable, and a
   discipline the operator never claimed. This is the everywhere-critical principle.

2. **Recognizes, doesn't rate.** Discovery assigns no Automate / Monitor / Human ratings, and makes
   no automation-safety claim in prose. Those calls need steps, evidence, and failure impact that
   don't exist yet. It may note capture priority or automation *interest*; it must never assert
   automation *safety*. Rating here is the "annoying, therefore safe to automate" fallacy.

3. **Surfaces real work, not the org chart.** The sweep leads with what the operator actually
   produced and touched recently (artifacts, tool-traces) and anchors recall in real recent windows,
   because that surfaces the tacit work people stop noticing. Cadence and department prompts elicit
   the idealized version.

4. **Keeps it thin.** Each candidate is a lead, not a mini-Blueprint. The sweep captures what
   recurs, roughly what triggers it, roughly what it produces, and a recent instance — and stops
   there. Over-specifying a candidate pretends it's been understood when it's only been recognized.

5. **Stops when dry.** The sweep is time-boxed and stops when nothing new surfaces. It does not march
   the full cue list for completeness, and — critically — it does not pad a thin inventory with
   invented or inflated candidates to feel thorough. An exhaustive sweep is its own annoying chore.

6. **Flags duplicates, doesn't merge.** Where two candidates might be the same work, two views of
   one process, or two steps of a larger one, the relationship is named and left unresolved. Whether
   it's one process or several is capture's question, not a decision the sweep makes.

7. **Prioritizes through three lenses.** The recommendation ranks work by automation opportunity,
   operational exposure, and knowledge-loss risk — not one magic score — and recommends three
   starting points (one automation win, one high-risk/key-person, one easy early win), each with a
   reason. This keeps discovery from re-narrowing Blueprint to an automate-chores tool.

8. **Machinery stays backstage.** The operator experiences a conversation about their work, not a
   narrated sweep. The cue list, the "surfaces," the template, and the skill's steps don't surface
   in the conversation or in the written inventory.

## How principles map to rubric dimensions

| Principle | Rubric dimension(s) |
| --- | --- |
| Never invents a boundary | Non-Invention |
| Recognizes, doesn't rate | No-Premature-Rating |
| Surfaces real work | Recall Breadth |
| Keeps it thin | Thinness |
| Stops when dry | Stop Discipline |
| Flags duplicates, doesn't merge | Duplicate Honesty |
| Prioritizes through three lenses | Portfolio Quality |
| Machinery stays backstage | Register |
| (structural floor for all of the above) | Artifact Integrity |
